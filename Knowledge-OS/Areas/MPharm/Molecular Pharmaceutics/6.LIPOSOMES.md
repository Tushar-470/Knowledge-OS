# LIPOSOMES

## Types, Preparation and Evaluation

### Introduction

Liposomes are microscopic vesicles composed of one or more phospholipid bilayers surrounding an aqueous core. Due to their amphiphilic nature, liposomes can encapsulate both hydrophilic drugs (in the aqueous core) and lipophilic drugs (within the lipid bilayer). They are widely used as carriers for targeted and controlled drug delivery.

---

# Types of Liposomes

## A. Based on Lamellarity and Size

|Type|Abbreviation|Size|Characteristics|
|---|---|---|---|
|Small Unilamellar Vesicles|SUV|20–100 nm|Single phospholipid bilayer|
|Large Unilamellar Vesicles|LUV|100–1000 nm|Single bilayer with large aqueous core|
|Multilamellar Vesicles|MLV|0.5–5 µm|Multiple concentric bilayers|
|Giant Unilamellar Vesicles|GUV|1–200 µm|Very large single bilayer vesicles|
|Oligolamellar Vesicles|OLV|0.1–1 µm|Few bilayers|
|Multivesicular Vesicles|MVV|5–30 µm|Multiple internal aqueous compartments|

---

## B. Based on Surface Modification

### 1. Conventional Liposomes

- Phospholipid + cholesterol
    
- No surface modification
    

### 2. Stealth (PEGylated) Liposomes

- Coated with PEG
    
- Longer circulation time
    
- Reduced RES uptake
    

### 3. Immunoliposomes

- Antibody attached to surface
    
- Active targeting
    

### 4. Thermosensitive Liposomes

- Drug released at elevated temperature (40–42°C)
    

### 5. pH-Sensitive Liposomes

- Drug release at acidic pH
    

### 6. Cationic Liposomes

- Positively charged
    
- Used in gene delivery
    

### 7. Archaeosomes

- Prepared from archaeal lipids
    
- Highly stable
    

### 8. Virosomes

- Liposomes containing viral proteins
    
- Used in vaccines and antigen delivery

# Preparation of Liposomes

## 1. Thin Film Hydration Method (Bangham Method)

### Principle

Phospholipids self-assemble into bilayer vesicles upon hydration.

### Procedure

1. Dissolve phospholipids and cholesterol in chloroform:methanol.
    
2. Remove solvent using rotary evaporator.
    
3. Thin lipid film forms on flask wall.
    
4. Hydrate with aqueous phase above transition temperature.
    
5. MLVs are formed.
    
6. Sonication or extrusion used for size reduction.
    

### Advantages

- Simple and widely used
    
- Suitable for laboratory preparation
    

### Limitation

- Produces heterogeneous MLVs.
    

---

## 2. Probe Sonication Method

### Principle

Ultrasonic energy reduces vesicle size.

### Procedure

1. Prepare MLVs.
    
2. Subject suspension to probe sonication.
    
3. Large vesicles break into SUVs.
    

### Advantages

- Produces very small vesicles
    

### Limitation

- Possible metal contamination
    
- Not suitable for heat-sensitive drugs
    

---

## 3. Membrane Extrusion Method

### Principle

Liposomes are forced through membranes of defined pore size.

### Procedure

1. Prepare MLVs.
    
2. Pass repeatedly through polycarbonate membrane.
    
3. Uniform LUVs produced.
    

### Advantages

- Monodisperse vesicles
    
- GMP compatible
    

### Limitation

- Additional processing step required
    

---

## 4. Reverse Phase Evaporation Method (REV)

### Procedure

1. Dissolve lipids in organic solvent.
    
2. Add aqueous drug solution.
    
3. Form water-in-oil emulsion.
    
4. Remove solvent.
    
5. Large unilamellar vesicles formed.
    

### Advantages

- High entrapment efficiency
    
- Large aqueous core
    

### Limitation

- Residual solvent issues
    

---

## 5. Detergent Depletion Method

### Procedure

1. Lipids and proteins dissolved in detergent solution.
    
2. Detergent removed by dialysis.
    
3. Liposomes self-assemble.
    

### Application

- Proteoliposomes
    
- Virosomes
    

---

## 6. Drug Loading Methods

### A. Passive Loading

- Drug incorporated during liposome formation.
    
- Lower encapsulation efficiency.
    

### B. Remote (Active) Loading

- Uses pH or ammonium sulfate gradient.
    
- Entrapment efficiency >90%.
    
- Used for doxorubicin liposomes (Doxil®).

# Evaluation of Liposomes

## 1. Vesicle Size and Size Distribution

### Method

- Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS)
    
- Nanoparticle Tracking Analysis (NTA)
    

### Importance

- Determines circulation time and targeting efficiency.
    

---

## 2. Zeta Potential

### Method

Electrophoretic Light Scattering.

### Importance

- Indicates colloidal stability.
    
- High absolute value prevents aggregation.
    

Typical values:

- Stable system: ±20 to ±40 mV.
    

---

## 3. Lamellarity Determination

### Method

- 31P-NMR
    
- Electron microscopy
    

### Purpose

- Determines number of bilayers.
    

---

## 4. Morphological Evaluation

### Methods

- TEM
    
- SEM
    
- Cryo-TEM
    

### Purpose

- Determines shape and vesicle structure.
    

---

## 5. Entrapment Efficiency (EE%)

### Formula

[  
EE% = \frac{Entrapped\ Drug}{Total\ Drug} \times 100  
]

### Importance

Measures amount of encapsulated drug.

---

## 6. Drug Loading (DL%)

### Formula

[  
DL% = \frac{Entrapped\ Drug}{Total\ Liposome\ Weight} \times 100  
]

### Importance

Measures drug content of formulation.

---

## 7. Phase Transition Temperature (Tm)

### Method

Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC)

### Purpose

- Evaluates bilayer stability.
    
- Studies lipid-drug interactions.
    

---

## 8. In-vitro Drug Release

### Methods

- Dialysis bag method
    
- Ultrafiltration method
    

### Purpose

Determines release profile and kinetics.

---

## 9. Stability Studies

Evaluate:

- Drug leakage
    
- Aggregation
    
- Fusion
    
- Lipid oxidation
    
- Hydrolysis
    

### Purpose

Determines shelf life and formulation stability.

---

## 10. Biological Evaluation

### In-vitro Studies

- Cellular uptake studies
    
- Cytotoxicity studies (MTT assay)
    
- Hemolysis studies
    
- Complement activation studies
    

### In-vivo Studies

- Pharmacokinetics
    
- Biodistribution
    
- Tumor targeting
    
- Antitumor efficacy studies
    

---

# Advantages of Liposomes

- Biocompatible and biodegradable
    
- Carry both hydrophilic and lipophilic drugs
    
- Targeted drug delivery
    
- Controlled drug release
    
- Reduced toxicity
    
- Improved bioavailability
    
- Protection of drug from degradation
    

---

# Limitations of Liposomes

- Drug leakage
    
- Physical instability
    
- Oxidation and hydrolysis of phospholipids
    
- High production cost
    
- Short shelf life
    
- Scale-up challenges
    

---

# Conclusion

Liposomes are phospholipid vesicular carriers capable of encapsulating both hydrophilic and lipophilic drugs. Various types such as SUVs, LUVs, MLVs, stealth liposomes, immunoliposomes, and thermosensitive liposomes are prepared by methods including thin film hydration, sonication, extrusion, reverse phase evaporation, and detergent depletion. Evaluation involves particle size, zeta potential, morphology, entrapment efficiency, drug release, stability, and biological studies. Their ability to improve efficacy while reducing toxicity has made liposomes one of the most successful nanocarrier systems in modern drug delivery.
