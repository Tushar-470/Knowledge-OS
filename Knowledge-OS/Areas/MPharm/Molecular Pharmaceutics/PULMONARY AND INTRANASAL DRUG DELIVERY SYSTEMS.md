# PULMONARY AND INTRANASAL DRUG DELIVERY SYSTEMS

## 1. INTRODUCTION (2 Marks)
*   **Pulmonary Route:** Administration of APIs to the respiratory tract for treatment/prevention of airway diseases.
*   **Advantages over oral/parenteral:** Rapid onset of action, lowered dose (reduces adverse effects and cost), useful for poorly absorbed drugs and those undergoing first-pass metabolism.
*   **Why the Lung?** Large surface area (70–80 m² in adult males), abundance of blood capillaries, and lower thickness of the air-blood barrier.
*   **Anatomy Context:** The respiratory tract includes the central region (trachea to terminal bronchioles) and peripheral region (respiratory bronchioles and alveoli). Conducting airways are lined with ciliated epithelial cells that trap and sweep away foreign particles via the mucociliary escalator.

## PART A: PULMONARY DRUG DELIVERY SYSTEMS (AEROSOLS) (16 Marks)

### 2. AEROSOLS: DEFINITION & ADVANTAGES (2 Marks)
*   **Definition:** Products that depend on the power of compressed or liquefied gas to expel contents from the container as a fine spray, foam, or semisolid stream. They contain APIs dissolved/emulsified in a propellant or solvent-propellant mixture.
*   **Key Advantages:**
    *   Tamper-proof and protected from external contamination.
    *   Metered valves ensure accurate dosing.
    *   Topical spray reduces irritation compared to mechanical application.
    *   Rapid onset of action; avoids GI degradation and first-pass metabolism.
    *   No need to maintain strict sterility like parenterals.
    *   Compact, convenient, and feasible for drugs with erratic oral PK behavior.

### 3. PREREQUISITES FOR FORMULATION (1 Mark)
*   Non-irritating to nasal/respiratory mucosa.
*   Reasonably soluble in respiratory fluid and effective at low doses.
*   Must exhibit passive transport through the respiratory membrane.
*   Minimal local mucosal activity (unless intended).
*   Stable, compatible, and pH maintained between 5.5 and 7.5.

### 4. PROPELLANTS (4 Marks)
**Definition:** Compounds maintaining proper pressure within the container to expel the product.
**Types:**
**A. Liquefied Gases:**
*   Establish equilibrium between liquid and vapor phases. Pressure is independent of liquid quantity.
*   Evaporate upon reaching air, leaving airborne droplets/particles. Product cools slightly due to latent heat of vaporization, but pressure drop is minimal (even discharge rate).
*   *Sub-types:*
    1.  **CFCs (Propellants 11, 12, 114):** High expansion ratio, low toxicity/inflammability. Largely phased out (Montreal Protocol) due to ozone depletion; now restricted to asthma/COPD inhalers.
    2.  **HCFCs & HFCs (Propellants 22, 134a, 142b, 152a, 227):** Fast atmospheric breakdown, lower ozone depletion. Propellants 22, 142b, 152a have good water miscibility. Propellants 134a and 227 are standard for modern MDIs.
    3.  **Hydrocarbons (Propane, Butane, Isobutane):** Environmentally friendly, immiscible with water, density < 1 (float on aqueous layer). Used in 3-phase systems. *Drawback:* Highly flammable.
**B. Compressed Gases (N₂, N₂O, CO₂):**
*   No propellant reservoir; pressure diminishes as product is used.
*   Expels product in the same form as placed in the container. Low expansion ratio yields wetter sprays and less stable foams.

**Numerical Designation Rule for Fluorinated Hydrocarbons:**
*   Rightmost digit = Number of Fluorine atoms.
*   2nd digit from right = Number of Hydrogen atoms + 1.
*   3rd digit from right = Number of Carbon atoms - 1 (omitted if 0).
*   Chlorine atoms = Total saturated atoms - (H + F) atoms.

### 5. CONTAINERS (3 Marks)
*   **Glass Containers:**
    *   *Pros:* Fewer chemical compatibility issues, no corrosion, allows viewing of contents.
    *   *Cons:* Low pressure safety. Used only if pressure < 25 psig and propellant < 50%.
    *   *Modification:* Plastic coatings applied to prevent shattering, absorb crimping shock, provide UV protection, and aid in color identification. Not used for suspensions (aesthetic issue). Size: 15-30 mL.
*   **Tin-Plated Steel:**
    *   Lightweight, inexpensive. Requires protective inner coatings (oleoresin, phenolic, vinyl, epoxy) to prevent corrosion. Used for topical aerosols.
*   **Aluminum:**
    *   Extremely lightweight, less reactive. Made by impact extrusion (seamless -> high safety against leakage). Can be anodized or resin-coated. Standard for most MDIs. Sizes: 10 mL to >1000 mL.
*   **Stainless Steel:** Highly chemically resistant but limited by high cost.
*   *(Note: Plastics have limited success due to vapor permeability and drug-plastic interactions).*

### 6. VALVES AND ACTUATORS (Brief Overview - 1 Mark)
*   **Components:** Actuator (button), Stem, Gasket (prevents leakage), Spring (retracts actuator), Mounting Cup (holds valve, needs inert coating), Housing (links dip tube to stem), Dip tube (brings formulation to valve).
*   **Types:** Spray valves (fine/coarse wet sprays), Vapor tap valves (for powders/suspensions to prevent clogging), Foam valves (single orifice, large expansion chamber), and Metered Dose Inhaler (MDI) valves (deliver 25-150 µL accurately using a dual valve chamber mechanism).

### 7. PRODUCT CONCENTRATE FORMULATIONS (2 Marks)
*   **Homogeneous (Solution Systems):** API dissolved in propellant/solvents (alcohol, PEG, etc.). Higher propellant = finer spray. Particle size: 5-10 µm (inhalation), 50-100 µm (topical).
*   **Heterogeneous Systems:**
    *   **Suspension Systems:** For insoluble APIs. Issues include caking, agglomeration, and valve clogging.
        *   *Critical parameters:* Moisture < 300 ppm; Particle size 1-5 µm (inhalation) or 40-50 µm (topical); API and propellant densities must nearly match to prevent sedimentation; surfactants must be non-toxic.
    *   **Emulsion Systems:** Propellant as internal phase = stable foam; Propellant as external phase = spray/quick-breaking foam. Types include aqueous stable foams, non-aqueous stable foams, quick-breaking foams, and thermal foams.

### 8. PREPARATION / MANUFACTURING (2 Marks)
**Stage 1:** Manufacture and testing of concentrate (saves time/money if rejected).
**Stage 2:** Addition of propellants and sealing.
*   **Cold Filling:**
    *   Propellant chilled to liquid form, mixed with concentrate, filled into open cans, and crimped.
    *   *Advantage:* Good for high-loading suspensions; controls crystallization/particle size.
    *   *Disadvantage:* Risk of moisture contamination from condensation.
*   **Pressure Filling (Preferred for pharma):**
    *   Uses pressure instead of cold to liquefy propellant. Less moisture risk and less propellant loss.
    *   *Two-stage:* Concentrate added to can -> crimped -> propellant forced through valve. (Used for thick suspensions).
    *   *Single-stage:* Concentrate and propellant mixed under pressure -> forced through valve into can. (Ideal for solutions and low-loading suspensions).

### 9. EVALUATION / TESTING OF AEROSOLS (3 Marks)
**A. Quality Control Tests:**
*   *Propellants:* Vapor pressure checked; purity tested via GC for moisture/halogens.
*   *Valves:* Delivery uniformity tested (Limits: ±15% for ≤54 µL, ±10% for 55-200 µL). 4+ failures out of 50 = lot rejection.
*   *Containers:* Checked for lining defects, conductivity (exposed metals), and glass flaws.
*   *Leak Testing:* Crimp dimensions measured; final check via water bath.
**B. Physicochemical Characteristics:**
*   Vapor pressure (can puncturing device), Density (hydrometer/pycnometer), Moisture (Karl-Fisher), Identification (GC/IR).
**C. Performance Testing:**
*   *Discharge Rate:* Weight loss per unit time (g/sec).
*   *Spray Pattern:* Impingement on dye-talc treated paper.
*   *Dosage per Metered Valve:* Assayed by dispensing into solvents/absorbing materials.
*   *Net Contents:* Weighing filled vs. empty tared cans.
*   *Foam Stability:* Visual evaluation, penetration time, rotational viscometer.
*   *Particle Size:* Cascade impactor or light scattering decay.
**D. Biological Testing:** Therapeutic activity (dependent on particle size for inhalation) and Toxicity studies (skin chilling/irritation for topicals; inhalation toxicity for pulmonary).

## PART B: INTRANASAL ROUTE DELIVERY SYSTEMS (12 Marks)

### 10. ADVANTAGES OF THE NASAL CAVITY (2 Marks)
*   Large surface area for absorption.
*   Convenience and good patient compliance.
*   Rapid attainment of therapeutic blood levels.
*   High permeability (especially for lipophilic/low MW drugs).
*   Avoids harsh GI conditions and hepatic first-pass metabolism.
*   **Unique advantage:** Potential for *direct drug delivery to the brain* via olfactory nerves.
*   Direct contact site for vaccines with lymphatic tissues.

### 11. TYPES OF NASAL DOSAGE FORMS (6 Marks)
*   **Nasal Drops:**
    *   Simplest and most convenient (delivered via pipette/squeezy bottle).
    *   *Limitations:* Lack of dosing precision, high risk of contamination, mucociliary dysfunction, and non-specific loss down the throat.
*   **Nasal Sprays:**
    *   Consist of a chamber, piston, and actuator.
    *   Generate precise, consistent doses (25–200 µL) with reproducible plume geometry.
    *   Droplet size/dose accuracy influenced by formulation thixotropy, viscosity, surface tension, applied force, and orifice size.
*   **Nasal Gels:**
    *   Semi-solid systems characterized by elastic modulus (G’) and viscous modulus (G”).
    *   Range from viscous solutions (hypromellose, methylcellulose, chitosan) to hard/brittle gels (gellan gum, pectin).
    *   *Advantage:* Bioadhesive polymers control drug release, decrease administration frequency, prolong residence time, and improve bioavailability by slowing mucociliary clearance.
*   **Nasal Powders:**
    *   Prepared by mixing, spray-drying, or freeze-drying drug with excipients.
    *   Often contain bioadhesive polymers (e.g., water-insoluble cellulose derivatives, Carbopol 934P).
    *   *Mechanism:* Powder contacts nasal fluid, swells, and forms a gel in-situ, providing prolonged release (highly effective for peptides/proteins like insulin).

### 12. PREPARATION OF NASAL FORMULATIONS (1 Mark)
*   **Liquid Forms (Drops/Sprays):** Prepared by simple dissolution/dispersion of API in aqueous or non-aqueous vehicles with required buffers, preservatives, and viscosity modifiers.
*   **Gels:** Prepared by dispersing gelling/bioadhesive polymers in the vehicle using high shear mixing to achieve the desired rheological modulus.
*   **Powders:** Prepared by physically mixing micronized drug with bioadhesive excipients, or via advanced techniques like spray-drying and freeze-drying to achieve optimal particle size and flow properties.

### 13. EVALUATION OF NASAL FORMULATIONS (3 Marks)
Alongside routine aerosol evaluation tests (where applicable), nasal formulations are specifically evaluated for:
*   **pH:** Must be within the acceptable nasal physiological range (typically 5.5 - 6.5) to avoid mucosal irritation and maintain ciliary function.
*   **Viscosity and Rheology:** Essential for gels to ensure ease of spraying/administration and proper bioadhesion (measured via G’ and G” moduli).
*   **Osmolarity:** Must be isotonic or slightly hypertonic to prevent mucosal irritation and ciliary paralysis.
*   **Drug Release Studies:** In-vitro release testing using dialysis membranes or Franz diffusion cells to ensure the bioadhesive polymers provide the intended sustained release profile.
*   **Stability Studies:** Chemical and microbiological stability under various temperature/humidity conditions to ensure safety throughout shelf life.

## 14. CONCLUSION (2 Marks)
Pharmaceutical aerosols and intranasal systems represent highly effective, non-invasive drug delivery methods. They provide targeted therapy for localized conditions (asthma, COPD, rhinitis) while offering a systemic route that bypasses first-pass metabolism. With advancements in propellant technology (shift from CFCs to HFCs), precision metered valves, and bioadhesive nasal gels/powders (enabling direct nose-to-brain delivery), these routes hold immense potential for the future delivery of challenging molecules like proteins and peptides.
