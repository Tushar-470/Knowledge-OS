# BIODISTRIBUTION, PHARMACOKINETICS, ANTISENSE MOLECULES, AND APTAMERS

## 1. INTRODUCTION TO PK/BD OF NUCLEIC ACID THERAPEUTICS (1 Mark)
Traditional drugs are governed by Lipinski’s Rule of Five (small, lipophilic). In contrast, nucleic acid therapeutics (DNA, RNA, oligonucleotides) are **large (4–15 kDa), highly negatively charged, and hydrophilic**. Because of this, their Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, and Excretion (ADME) profiles are vastly different and present unique pharmaceutical challenges.

---

## 2. PHARMACOKINETICS AND BIODISTRIBUTION (PK/BD) (5 Marks)

### A. Absorption
*   **Oral Route Failure:** Nucleic acids cannot be given orally. They are rapidly degraded by stomach acid, pancreatic nucleases, and intestinal flora. Furthermore, their large size and negative charge prevent passive diffusion across the intestinal epithelium.
*   **Parenteral Routes:** They are almost exclusively administered via intravenous (IV), subcutaneous (SC), or intrathecal (into the spinal canal) injections. SC injections allow slow absorption into the lymphatic system before entering systemic circulation.

### B. Distribution (Biodistribution)
*   **Plasma Protein Binding:** Once in the blood, oligonucleotides bind heavily to plasma proteins (like albumin and alpha-1-acid glycoprotein). This is a double-edged sword: it protects them from renal excretion but limits their movement out of the bloodstream into tissues.
*   **Tissue Uptake:** They distribute poorly to most tissues. The primary organs of distribution are the **liver, kidneys, and spleen**, because these organs have highly permeable, fenestrated (leaky) blood vessels (sinusoids) that allow large molecules to escape.
*   **The "Endosomal Trap":** Even when a nucleic acid enters a target cell via endocytosis, it gets stuck inside an endosome. A massive percentage (often >90%) of the drug is eventually degraded in lysosomes. Escaping the endosome is the biggest bottleneck in nucleic acid delivery.
*   **Active Targeting Strategy:** To overcome poor distribution, modern drugs use targeting ligands. E.g., conjugating the drug to **GalNAc (N-acetylgalactosamine)**, which binds specifically to receptors on liver cells (hepatocytes), ensuring highly targeted liver distribution.

### C. Metabolism
*   Nucleic acids are not metabolized by Cytochrome P450 (CYP450) enzymes like normal drugs.
*   They are degraded by **endonucleases** (cut from the inside) and **exonucleases** (chew away from the ends) present in blood and tissues.
*   To prevent this, therapeutic nucleic acids are heavily chemically modified (discussed below) to resist nuclease degradation, giving them tissue half-lives ranging from days to weeks.

### D. Excretion
*   Small, unbound oligonucleotides are rapidly filtered by the kidneys and excreted in urine.
*   Larger or protein-bound molecules are metabolized into short nucleotide fragments by the liver and kidneys, and these inactive metabolites are excreted via urine or feces.

## 3. THERAPEUTIC ANTISENSE MOLECULES (5 Marks)

### A. What are Antisense Oligonucleotides (ASOs)?
*   ASOs are short, synthetic, single-stranded molecules of DNA or RNA, typically 15 to 25 nucleotides long.
*   They are designed to be **exactly complementary** to a specific sequence of target Messenger RNA (mRNA).

### B. Mechanism of Action
Once inside the cell, the ASO binds to the target mRNA via Watson-Crick base pairing. This binding triggers one of two mechanisms:
1.  **RNase H Dependent Degradation:** If the ASO has a "DNA-like" region, it forms a DNA-RNA hybrid duplex. This recruits an endogenous enzyme called **RNase H**, which acts like molecular scissors and cuts up and destroys the target mRNA. (Result: No protein is made).
2.  **Steric Blockade:** If the ASO is heavily modified (e.g., 2'-O-methyl RNA), it binds tightly to the mRNA but does *not* recruit RNase H. Instead, it simply acts as a physical roadblock. It blocks the ribosome from reading the mRNA, preventing protein translation. It can also block splicing (used to correct faulty splicing in genetic diseases).

### C. Generations of ASOs (Exam Favorite)
To survive in the human body, ASOs must be chemically modified. They are classified into three generations:
*   **First Generation:** Backbone modified (e.g., **Phosphorothioate** - replacing an oxygen with sulfur). Increases nuclease resistance and protein binding.
    *   *Example:* Fomivirsen (first FDA-approved ASO, for CMV retinitis).
*   **Second Generation:** Sugar ring modified (e.g., **2'-O-Methoxyethyl** or 2'-MOE). Greatly increases binding affinity to mRNA and half-life in tissues.
    *   *Example:* Nusinersen (Spinraza) for Spinal Muscular Atrophy (given intrathecally).
*   **Third Generation:** Locked configurations (e.g., **Locked Nucleic Acids - LNAs**, or Peptide Nucleic Acids - PNAs). The sugar ring is chemically "locked" to prevent flexibility. Offers unmatched binding affinity and extreme nuclease resistance.

## 4. APTAMERS AS DRUGS OF THE FUTURE (5 Marks)

### A. What are Aptamers?
*   While ASOs target *RNA*, **Aptamers target *Proteins***.
*   Aptamers are single-stranded DNA or RNA molecules (typically 20-80 nucleotides) that fold into complex 3D structures (stem-loops, G-quadruplexes).
*   Because of this 3D folding, they can bind to specific proteins with extremely high affinity, exactly like a **"Chemical Antibody."**

### B. How are they discovered? (SELEX)
Aptamers are not designed on a computer; they are evolved in a lab using a process called **SELEX** (Systematic Evolution of Ligands by EXponential enrichment). Millions of random DNA/RNA sequences are exposed to a target protein. The ones that bind are collected, amplified by PCR, and the process is repeated until only the tightest-binding sequences remain.

### C. Advantages over Monoclonal Antibodies (mAbs)
Aptamers are considered the drugs of the future because they offer several advantages over traditional antibody drugs:
1.  **Synthetic vs. Biological:** mAbs are produced in living cells (expensive, batch-to-batch variation). Aptamers are chemically synthesized (precise, scalable, lower cost).
2.  **Immunogenicity:** mAbs can trigger an immune response. Aptamers are generally non-immunogenic.
3.  **Size:** Aptamers are much smaller (~15 kDa vs. ~150 kDa for mAbs), allowing them to penetrate tumors and dense tissues much better.
4.  **Stability:** Can be stored at room temperature; mAbs require strict cold chains.
5.  **Reversibility:** Aptamer-protein binding is often reversible, allowing for better safety profiles.

### D. Challenges and Solutions
*   **Rapid Renal Clearance:** Because they are small, kidneys filter them out quickly (short half-life).
    *   *Solution:* **PEGylation** (attaching Polyethylene Glycol) increases their size above the renal filtration threshold, extending half-life from minutes to weeks.
*   **Nuclease Degradation:** Unmodified RNA aptamers are destroyed in minutes.
    *   *Solution:* Chemical modifications (2'-Fluoro, 2'-O-methyl substitutions) make them virtually indestructible to nucleases.

### E. Clinical Examples
1.  **Pegaptanib (Macugen):** An anti-VEGF RNA aptamer (PEGylated). It was the first aptamer approved by the FDA (2004) for the treatment of Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD). It binds to VEGF protein, preventing it from causing abnormal blood vessel growth in the eye.
2.  **AS1411:** A DNA aptamer currently in clinical trials for cancer. It targets *nucleolin*, a protein overexpressed on the surface of many cancer cells, inhibiting tumor growth.

### SUMMARY / EXAM CONCLUSION
While gene therapy aims to *add* a functional gene, ASOs and Aptamers function at the post-transcriptional and translational levels to *silence* or *block* disease-causing proteins. Understanding their unique PK—specifically their inability to cross cell membranes easily and their rapid renal clearance—has driven massive innovation in chemical modifications (LNA, MOE, PEGylation) and targeted delivery (GalNAc), firmly establishing these molecules as the cornerstone of precision medicine in the 21st century.
