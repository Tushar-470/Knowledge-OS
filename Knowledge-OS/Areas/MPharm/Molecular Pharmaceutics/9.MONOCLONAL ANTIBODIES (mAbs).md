# MONOCLONAL ANTIBODIES (mAbs)

## Preparation and Applications (10 Marks Answer)

# Introduction

Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) are antibodies produced from a **single clone of B-lymphocytes**, therefore all antibody molecules are identical and recognize the same antigenic epitope. They are highly specific and are widely used in cancer, autoimmune diseases, infectious diseases, and targeted drug delivery. Hybridoma technology for monoclonal antibody production was developed by **Köhler and Milstein (1975)**.

---

# Preparation of Monoclonal Antibodies

## (Hybridoma Technology)

### Step 1: Immunization

- Mouse is immunized with the desired antigen.
    
- Several booster doses are given.
    
- Antigen-specific B lymphocytes are produced in the spleen.
    

### Step 2: Isolation of Spleen Cells

- Spleen is removed from the immunized mouse.
    
- B-cells producing the desired antibody are isolated.
    

### Step 3: Preparation of Myeloma Cells

- Myeloma cells (cancerous plasma cells) are cultured.
    
- These cells are immortal and can divide continuously.
    
- They do not produce antibodies.
    

### Step 4: Cell Fusion

- B-cells are fused with myeloma cells using **Polyethylene Glycol (PEG)**.
    
- Hybrid cells called **Hybridomas** are formed.
    

### Hybridoma = B-cell + Myeloma Cell

- B-cell provides antibody production.
    
- Myeloma cell provides immortality.
    

### Step 5: HAT Selection

**HAT = Hypoxanthine + Aminopterin + Thymidine**

- Unfused myeloma cells die.
    
- Unfused B-cells die naturally.
    
- Only hybridoma cells survive and grow.
    

### Step 6: Screening

- Hybridoma cells are screened by **ELISA**.
    
- Cells producing the desired antibody are selected.
    

### Step 7: Cloning

- Positive hybridomas are cloned by limiting dilution.
    
- A single clone producing one type of antibody is obtained.
    

### Step 8: Large Scale Production

- Selected hybridoma cells are grown in bioreactors.
    
- Antibodies are produced in large quantities and purified.


# Applications of Monoclonal Antibodies

## 1. Cancer Therapy

Monoclonal antibodies specifically target cancer cells.

### Examples

- Rituximab → Non-Hodgkin's lymphoma
    
- Trastuzumab → HER2 positive breast cancer
    
- Cetuximab → Colorectal cancer
    
- Bevacizumab → Anti-angiogenic therapy
    
- Pembrolizumab → Immunotherapy (PD-1 inhibitor)
    

---

## 2. Autoimmune Diseases

Used to suppress abnormal immune responses.

### Examples

- Adalimumab → Rheumatoid arthritis
    
- Infliximab → Crohn's disease
    
- Tocilizumab → Rheumatoid arthritis
    
- Ustekinumab → Psoriasis
    

---

## 3. Infectious Diseases

Used for prevention and treatment of infections.

### Examples

- Palivizumab → Respiratory Syncytial Virus (RSV)
    
- Bezlotoxumab → Clostridium difficile infection
    

---

## 4. Organ Transplantation

Prevent organ rejection.

### Examples

- Basiliximab
    
- Eculizumab
    

---

## 5. Allergy and Asthma

### Example

- Omalizumab
    

Binds IgE and reduces allergic reactions.

---

## 6. Cardiovascular Diseases

### Examples

- Evolocumab
    
- Alirocumab
    

Reduce LDL cholesterol levels.

---

## 7. Bone Disorders

### Example

- Denosumab
    

Used in osteoporosis and bone metastasis.

---

## Advantages

- Highly specific action
    
- Targeted drug delivery
    
- Reduced side effects
    
- High therapeutic efficacy
    
- Useful in diagnosis and treatment of diseases
    

---

## Limitations

- Expensive production
    
- Immunogenic reactions
    
- Infusion reactions
    
- Limited penetration into solid tumors
    
- Long manufacturing process
    

---

# Conclusion

Monoclonal antibodies are highly specific antibodies produced from a single B-cell clone using hybridoma technology. They are prepared by immunization, cell fusion, HAT selection, screening, cloning, and large-scale production. They play an important role in cancer therapy, autoimmune diseases, infectious diseases, transplantation, allergy, cardiovascular diseases, and targeted drug delivery.
