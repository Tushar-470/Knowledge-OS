# TUMOR TARGETING

## Definition

Tumor targeting is a drug delivery approach in which anticancer drugs are delivered selectively to tumor cells or tumor tissue while minimizing exposure to normal healthy cells. The main aim is to increase drug concentration at the tumor site and reduce systemic toxicity.

---

# Need for Tumor Targeting

Conventional chemotherapy affects both cancer cells and normal cells, leading to side effects such as:

- Bone marrow suppression
    
- Hair loss (alopecia)
    
- Nausea and vomiting
    
- Liver and kidney toxicity
    

Therefore, tumor targeting is required to:

- Increase drug concentration at the tumor site
    
- Reduce toxicity to healthy tissues
    
- Improve therapeutic efficacy
    
- Enhance the therapeutic index of anticancer drugs
    

---

# Biological Basis of Tumor Targeting

Tumors possess unique characteristics that distinguish them from normal tissues and make selective targeting possible.

## 1. Enhanced Permeability and Retention (EPR) Effect

Tumors develop abnormal blood vessels due to rapid growth.

These blood vessels are:

- Leaky
    
- Irregular
    
- Poorly organized
    

The gaps between endothelial cells are large (100–800 nm), allowing nanoparticles to enter tumor tissue easily. In addition, tumors have poor lymphatic drainage, causing nanoparticles to remain trapped inside the tumor.

This phenomenon is called the **Enhanced Permeability and Retention (EPR) Effect**.

### Significance

- Forms the basis of passive targeting.
    
- Produces higher drug accumulation in tumor tissue than in normal tissue.
    

---

## 2. Receptor Overexpression

Cancer cells overexpress specific receptors compared to normal cells.

Examples include:

- Folate receptor (FR)
    
- Human Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor-2 (HER2)
    
- Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor (EGFR)
    
- Transferrin Receptor (TfR)
    

These receptors serve as molecular targets for selective drug delivery.

---

## 3. Tumor Microenvironment (TME)

The tumor microenvironment differs from normal tissue and exhibits:

- Acidic pH
    
- Hypoxia (low oxygen level)
    
- High glutathione (GSH) concentration
    
- Increased Matrix Metalloproteinase (MMP) enzyme levels
    

These differences can be exploited for selective drug release at the tumor site.

---

# Strategies of Tumor Targeting

## 1. Passive Targeting

Passive targeting relies on the EPR effect.

### Mechanism

1. Nanoparticles are administered intravenously.
    
2. They circulate in the bloodstream.
    
3. Due to leaky tumor vasculature, nanoparticles extravasate into tumor tissue.
    
4. Poor lymphatic drainage retains them within the tumor.
    

### Advantages

- Simple and effective approach.
    
- No targeting ligand required.
    

### Example

- PEGylated liposomes
    
- Doxil® (liposomal doxorubicin)
    

---

## 2. Active Targeting

Active targeting involves attaching a ligand to the surface of a carrier system.

### Ligands Used

- Antibodies
    
- Peptides
    
- Folic acid
    
- Transferrin
    

### Mechanism

1. The carrier reaches the tumor through the EPR effect.
    
2. The ligand binds specifically to receptors on tumor cells.
    
3. Receptor-mediated endocytosis occurs.
    
4. Drug enters the cancer cell and produces its therapeutic effect.
    

### Advantages

- Greater specificity
    
- Increased cellular uptake
    
- Reduced toxicity to healthy tissues
    

### Example

Folate-conjugated nanoparticles targeting folate receptors.

---

## 3. Antibody–Drug Conjugates (ADCs)

Antibody–Drug Conjugates are highly specific tumor-targeting systems.

### Components

1. Monoclonal antibody
    
2. Cytotoxic drug
    
3. Chemical linker
    

### Mechanism

- Antibody recognizes and binds to tumor-specific antigen.
    
- ADC enters the tumor cell by endocytosis.
    
- The linker is cleaved.
    
- The drug is released intracellularly.
    
- Cancer cell death occurs.
    

### Example

**Kadcyla® (Trastuzumab-DM1)** used for HER2-positive breast cancer.

---

## 4. Stimuli-Responsive Targeting

Drug release occurs only in response to tumor-specific stimuli.

|Stimulus|Response|
|---|---|
|Acidic pH|pH-sensitive drug release|
|High GSH|Reduction-sensitive release|
|MMP enzymes|Enzyme-triggered release|
|Hypoxia|Hypoxia-responsive release|

### Advantage

Provides controlled and selective release of the drug within the tumor tissue.

---

# Nanocarriers Used in Tumor Targeting

### 1. Liposomes

- Phospholipid vesicles
    
- Biocompatible and biodegradable
    
- Improve drug stability
    

### 2. Polymeric Nanoparticles

- Prepared from polymers such as PLGA
    
- Provide sustained drug release
    

### 3. Albumin Nanoparticles

- Natural carrier systems
    
- Example: Abraxane®
    

### 4. Dendrimers

- Highly branched polymers
    
- High drug-loading capacity
    

### 5. Antibody–Drug Conjugates

- Highly selective targeted delivery systems
    

---

# Advantages of Tumor Targeting

- Site-specific drug delivery
    
- Reduced systemic toxicity
    
- Increased therapeutic efficacy
    
- Improved therapeutic index
    
- Better patient compliance
    
- Reduced adverse effects of chemotherapy
    

---

# Limitations

- EPR effect varies among tumors.
    
- High production cost.
    
- Complex manufacturing process.
    
- Variable receptor expression among patients.
    
- Possibility of drug resistance.
    

---

# Conclusion

Tumor targeting is an advanced drug delivery strategy that selectively delivers anticancer drugs to tumor tissue by exploiting the EPR effect, receptor overexpression, and tumor microenvironment characteristics. Passive targeting, active targeting, antibody–drug conjugates, and stimuli-responsive systems improve drug accumulation at the tumor site, enhance therapeutic efficacy, and reduce systemic toxicity. Thus, tumor targeting plays a crucial role in modern cancer therapy.
