# MICROCAPSULES

## Types, Preparation and Evaluation (10 Marks)

### Introduction

Microcapsules are small particles (1–1000 μm) in which the drug core is surrounded by a polymeric coating or membrane. The coating protects the drug and controls its release rate. They are used for sustained release, taste masking, protection of unstable drugs, and site-specific drug delivery.

---

# 1. Types of Microcapsules

## A. Based on Structure

### 1. Mononuclear Microcapsules

- Single drug core surrounded by one coating layer.
    
- Most common type.
    

### 2. Polynuclear Microcapsules

- Multiple drug cores enclosed within a single coating.
    

### 3. Matrix Type (Microsphere Type)

- Drug uniformly dispersed throughout polymer matrix.
    
- No distinct core and shell.
    

### 4. Multilayered Microcapsules

- Multiple coating layers around the core.
    
- Provides sequential or controlled release.
    

### 5. Coated Microcapsules

- Core surrounded by more than one protective coating.
    

---

### Natural Polymers

- Gelatin

- Pectin
    

### Synthetic Polymers

### Waxes and Lipids

- Carnauba wax

### Inorganic Materials

- Calcium carbonate

# 2. Preparation of Microcapsules

## 1. Coacervation Phase Separation Method

### Principle

Polymer separates from solution and deposits around the drug particles.

### Procedure

1. Drug dispersed in polymer solution.
    
2. Coacervation induced by pH or temperature change.
    
3. Polymer deposits around drug core.
    
4. Wall hardened by crosslinking.
    
5. Microcapsules collected and dried.
    

### Types

- Simple Coacervation
    
- Complex Coacervation (Gelatin–Acacia system)
    

---

## 2. Interfacial Polymerization

### Principle

Polymer membrane forms at the interface of two immiscible phases.

### Procedure

1. Drug dispersed in one phase.
    
2. Monomers added in both phases.
    
3. Polymerization occurs at interface.
    
4. Thin polymer wall forms around core.
    

### Advantages

- Uniform coating
    
- Thin membrane formation
    

---

## 3. In-situ Polymerization

### Principle

Polymerization occurs around dispersed drug particles.

### Examples

- Urea-formaldehyde
    
- Melamine-formaldehyde
    

### Application

- Controlled release systems
    

---

## 4. Spray Drying

### Principle

Drug-polymer solution is atomized into hot air.

### Procedure

1. Prepare drug-polymer solution.
    
2. Atomize through spray nozzle.
    
3. Solvent evaporates rapidly.
    
4. Dry microcapsules formed.
    

### Advantages

- Simple
    
- Economical
    
- Industrially preferred
    

---

## 5. Spray Congealing

### Principle

Molten wax/polymer containing drug is sprayed into cool air.

### Procedure

1. Melt coating material.
    
2. Disperse drug.
    
3. Spray into cooling chamber.
    
4. Solid microcapsules formed.
    

### Advantage

- No solvent required.
    

---

## 6. Solvent Evaporation Method

### Principle

Polymer dissolved in volatile solvent.

### Procedure

1. Drug and polymer dissolved in organic solvent.
    
2. Emulsified in aqueous phase.
    
3. Solvent evaporates.
    
4. Polymer precipitates around drug.
    

### Application

- PLGA microcapsules
    
- Sustained release systems
    

---

## 7. Pan Coating / Fluidized Bed Coating

### Procedure

1. Drug particles placed in coating chamber.
    
2. Polymer solution sprayed.
    
3. Drying occurs simultaneously.
    
4. Uniform coating obtained.
    

### Application

- Enteric coating
    
- Sustained release pellets

# Advantages of Microcapsules

- Controlled and sustained drug release
    
- Taste masking
    
- Protection of unstable drugs
    
- Reduced dosing frequency
    
- Site-specific drug delivery
    
- Conversion of liquids into free-flowing powders
    
- Reduced toxicity and side effects
    

---

# Conclusion

Microcapsules are drug delivery systems in which a drug core is enclosed within a polymer membrane. They are classified based on structure and wall material. Common preparation methods include coacervation, interfacial polymerization, spray drying, spray congealing, solvent evaporation, and fluidized bed coating. Evaluation includes particle size, morphology, wall thickness, drug content, encapsulation efficiency, dissolution studies, stability studies, and in-vivo performance. Their ability to provide controlled release and targeted delivery makes them an important novel drug delivery system.
