# 🔵 NIOSOMES

## Definition

Niosomes are microscopic vesicular carrier systems formed by self-assembly of non-ionic surfactants in an aqueous medium, usually in the presence of cholesterol, capable of entrapping both hydrophilic and lipophilic drugs.

### 🔑 Keyword

**Non-ionic Surfactant Vesicles**

---

## Structure

### Diagram Labels

- Hydrophilic Head
- Hydrophobic Tail
- Cholesterol
- Bilayer Membrane
- Aqueous Core
- Drug Molecule

### Description

Niosomes are closed bilayer vesicles formed by non-ionic surfactants. Hydrophilic heads face the aqueous environment while hydrophobic tails orient inward. Cholesterol is incorporated into the bilayer to increase rigidity and stability. Hydrophilic drugs are entrapped within the aqueous core whereas lipophilic drugs are incorporated into the bilayer membrane.

---

## Types / Classification

### 1. Small Unilamellar Vesicles (SUV)

- Consist of a single bilayer membrane.
- Diameter ranges from 10–100 nm.
- Suitable for small drug molecules.

### 2. Large Unilamellar Vesicles (LUV)

- Possess a single large bilayer.
- Large aqueous core.
- Higher drug loading capacity.

### 3. Multilamellar Vesicles (MLV)

- Consist of multiple concentric bilayers.
- Greater stability.
- High entrapment efficiency.

### 4. Proniosomes

- Dry free-flowing precursor of niosomes.
- Converted to niosomes upon hydration.
- Better storage stability.

### 5. Elastic Niosomes

- Highly flexible vesicles.
- Improved skin permeation.

### 6. Discomes

- Large disc-shaped vesicles.
- Mainly used in ocular delivery.

---

## Composition

|Component|Function|
|---|---|
|Span, Tween, Brij|Vesicle formation|
|Cholesterol|Membrane stability|
|Dicetyl phosphate/Stearylamine|Charge induction|
|Water|Hydration medium|
|Drug|Therapeutic activity|

### Formula

**Surfactant + Cholesterol + Drug + Water**

---

## Methods of Preparation

### 1. Thin Film Hydration Method ⭐

Surfactant + Cholesterol ↓ Organic Solvent (Chloroform:Methanol) ↓ Rotary Evaporation ↓ Thin Film Formation ↓ Hydration with Drug Solution ↓ Multilamellar Niosomes

### 2. Reverse Phase Evaporation Method

Surfactant + Cholesterol ↓ Organic Solvent ↓ Addition of Aqueous Drug Solution ↓ Water-in-Oil Emulsion ↓ Vacuum Evaporation ↓ Niosomes Obtained

### 3. Ether Injection Method

Surfactant Solution in Ether ↓ Injection into Warm Aqueous Phase ↓ Ether Evaporation ↓ Unilamellar Niosomes

### 4. Sonication Method

Multilamellar Vesicles ↓ Probe Sonication ↓ Size Reduction ↓ Small Unilamellar Niosomes

### 5. Microfluidization Method

High Pressure Processing ↓ Microchannel Collision ↓ Uniform Vesicle Formation

---

- Entrapment Efficiency

# 🟢 PHYTOSOMES

## Definition

Phytosomes are lipid-compatible molecular complexes formed by interaction of phospholipids with phytoconstituents, resulting in improved absorption and bioavailability of herbal drugs.

### 🔑 Keyword

**Phytoconstituent + Phospholipid Complex**

---

## Structure

### Diagram Labels

- Phytoconstituent
- Phospholipid Head
- Fatty Acid Tail
- Molecular Complex

### Description

Phytosomes are molecular complexes in which active phytoconstituents are chemically linked with phospholipids. The phospholipid enhances membrane permeability and improves absorption through biological membranes.

---

## Types

### 1. Flavonoid Phytosomes

- Prepared from flavonoids.
- Example: Quercetin, Silybin.

### 2. Polyphenol Phytosomes

- Contain polyphenolic compounds.
- Example: Green tea polyphenols.

### 3. Terpenoid Phytosomes

- Contain terpenoid constituents.
- Improved membrane penetration.

### 4. Herbal Extract Phytosomes

- Prepared using standardized herbal extracts.
- Example: Curcumin, Ashwagandha.

---

## Composition

|Component|Function|
|---|---|
|Phytoconstituent|Active agent|
|Phosphatidylcholine|Complex formation|
|Organic Solvent|Preparation medium|

### Formula

**Phytoconstituent + Phospholipid**

---

## Methods of Preparation

### 1. Solvent Evaporation Method ⭐

Phytoconstituent + Phospholipid ↓ Organic Solvent ↓ Reflux ↓ Complex Formation ↓ Solvent Evaporation ↓ Phytosomes Obtained

### 2. Anti-Solvent Precipitation Method

Drug + Phospholipid ↓ Organic Solvent ↓ Anti-Solvent Addition ↓ Precipitation ↓ Drying

### 3. Reflux Method

Drug + Phospholipid ↓ Heating under Reflux ↓ Complex Formation ↓ Cooling

### 4. Rotary Evaporation Method

Drug + Phospholipid ↓ Rotary Evaporation ↓ Thin Film Formation ↓ Drying

### 5. Solvent Injection Method

Phospholipid Solution ↓ Injection into Drug Solution ↓ Complex Formation

---

## Special Evaluation Tests

- FTIR Analysis
- Complexation Efficiency
- Solubility Studies
- Dissolution Studies

---

## Unique Applications

- Herbal Drug Delivery
- Hepatoprotective Therapy
- Antioxidant Therapy
- Nutraceutical Delivery

---

# 🟠 AQUASOMES

## Definition

Aquasomes are self-assembled three-layered nanoparticulate carrier systems consisting of a solid core coated with a carbohydrate layer onto which biologically active molecules are adsorbed.

### 🔑 Keyword

**Core + Carbohydrate + Biomolecule**

---

## Structure

### Diagram

Solid Core ↓ Carbohydrate Layer ↓ Protein/Peptide/Vaccine

### Description

Aquasomes possess a three-layered architecture. The solid core provides mechanical support, the carbohydrate layer preserves biological activity by maintaining a water-like environment, and the therapeutic biomolecule is adsorbed onto the outer surface.

---

## Types

### 1. Ceramic Aquasomes

- Core composed of ceramic materials.
- Example: Calcium phosphate.

### 2. Polymeric Aquasomes

- Core prepared from biodegradable polymers.
- Improved biocompatibility.

### 3. Nanocrystalline Aquasomes

- Core composed of nanocrystalline materials.
- Enhanced surface area.

---

## Composition

|Component|Function|
|---|---|
|Ceramic/Polymeric Core|Structural support|
|Carbohydrate Coating|Preserves biological activity|
|Protein/Peptide/Vaccine|Therapeutic agent|

### Formula

**Core + Carbohydrate + Biomolecule**

---

## Methods of Preparation

### 1. Core Formation ⭐

Calcium Phosphate ↓ Controlled Precipitation ↓ Nanoparticle Formation ↓ Purification

### 2. Carbohydrate Coating ⭐

Prepared Core ↓ Carbohydrate Solution ↓ Surface Adsorption ↓ Coated Core

### 3. Drug Loading ⭐

Coated Core ↓ Protein/Peptide Solution ↓ Adsorption ↓ Aquasomes Formed

### 4. Sonication Method

Aquasomal Dispersion ↓ Sonication ↓ Uniform Particle Size

### 5. Lyophilization Method

Suspension ↓ Freezing ↓ Freeze Drying ↓ Dry Aquasomes

---

## Special Evaluation Tests

- Protein Loading Efficiency
- Biological Activity Studies
- Carbohydrate Coating Confirmation

---

## Unique Applications

- Protein Delivery
- Peptide Delivery
- Vaccine Delivery
- Enzyme Delivery

---

## Marketed Products

- No major commercial aquasomal products available.
- Technology remains largely research-oriented.


# 🔴 ELECTROSOMES

## Definition

Electrosomes are electrically responsive vesicular drug delivery systems designed to enhance drug transport and enable controlled release through the application of electrical stimuli. These carriers can encapsulate therapeutic agents and improve site-specific delivery and therapeutic efficacy.

### 🔑 Keyword

**Electrically Responsive Vesicular Carrier**

---

## Structure

### Diagram Labels

- Drug Molecule
- Vesicular Membrane
- Charged Surface
- Aqueous Core
- Electrical Stimulus

### Description

Electrosomes are vesicular systems possessing electrically charged surfaces. The drug may be entrapped within the aqueous core or incorporated into the vesicular membrane. Application of an electric field alters membrane permeability and facilitates controlled drug release. The charged surface improves interaction with biological membranes and enhances targeted delivery.

---

## Types / Classification

### 1. Cationic Electrosomes

- Positively charged vesicles.
- Strong interaction with negatively charged cell membranes.
- Suitable for gene and nucleic acid delivery.

### 2. Anionic Electrosomes

- Negatively charged vesicles.
- Reduced cellular toxicity.
- Used in controlled drug delivery.

### 3. Neutral Electrosomes

- Electrically neutral vesicles.
- Improved physical stability.
- Reduced nonspecific interactions.

### 4. Stimuli-Responsive Electrosomes

- Respond to external electric fields.
- Enable controlled and site-specific drug release.
- Used in advanced targeted therapy.

---

## Composition

|Component|Function|
|---|---|
|Lipids/Polymers|Vesicle formation|
|Charged Molecules|Surface charge generation|
|Drug|Therapeutic activity|
|Water/Aqueous Phase|Hydration medium|
|Stabilizers|Improve stability|

### Formula

**Lipid/Polymer + Charged Component + Drug + Water**

---

## Methods of Preparation

### 1. Electroformation Method ⭐

#### Principle

An electric field is applied to a lipid film resulting in the formation of electrically responsive vesicles.

#### Flowchart

Lipids/Polymers ↓ Organic Solvent ↓ Thin Film Formation ↓ Application of Electric Field ↓ Vesicle Formation ↓ Drug Loading ↓ Electrosomes Obtained

---

### 2. Electroporation Method

#### Principle

Electrical pulses temporarily increase membrane permeability allowing drug incorporation.

#### Flowchart

Preformed Vesicles ↓ Drug Addition ↓ Electric Pulse Application ↓ Temporary Pore Formation ↓ Drug Entrapment ↓ Electrosomes Obtained

---

### 3. Thin Film Hydration Method

Lipids/Polymers ↓ Organic Solvent ↓ Thin Film Formation ↓ Hydration ↓ Electrosomes Obtained

---

### 4. Sonication Method

Prepared Vesicles ↓ Probe Sonication ↓ Size Reduction ↓ Uniform Electrosomes Obtained

---

### 5. Microfluidization Method

Lipid Dispersion ↓ High Pressure Processing ↓ Microchannel Collision ↓ Uniform Electrosomes Obtained

---

## Special Evaluation Tests
