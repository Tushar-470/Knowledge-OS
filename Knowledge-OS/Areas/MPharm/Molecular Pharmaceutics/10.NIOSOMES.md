# NIOSOMES

## Preparation and Applications (10 Marks Answer)

# Introduction

Niosomes are vesicular drug delivery systems composed of **non-ionic surfactants and cholesterol**, forming a bilayer structure enclosing an aqueous core. They are similar to liposomes but are more stable, less expensive, and easier to store. Niosomes can encapsulate both hydrophilic and lipophilic drugs and are widely used for controlled and targeted drug delivery.

---

# Composition of Niosomes

### Main Components

1. Non-ionic surfactants
    
    - Span 20
        
    - Span 40
        
    - Span 60
        
    - Tween 60
        
2. Cholesterol
    
    - Stabilizes bilayer membrane
        
    - Reduces drug leakage
        
3. Charge Inducers
    
    - Dicetyl phosphate (DCP)
        
    - Stearylamine
        

These improve stability and prevent aggregation.

---

# Preparation of Niosomes

## 1. Thin Film Hydration Method (Most Important)

### Procedure

1. Surfactant and cholesterol dissolved in chloroform:methanol.
    
2. Solvent removed using rotary evaporator.
    
3. Thin lipid film formed on flask wall.
    
4. Aqueous drug solution added.
    
5. Hydration produces multilamellar niosomes (MLV).
    
6. Sonication may be used to reduce size.
    

### Advantages

- Simple and widely used.
    
- High drug entrapment.
    

---

## 2. Ether Injection Method

### Procedure

1. Surfactant dissolved in ether.
    
2. Slowly injected into warm aqueous phase.
    
3. Ether evaporates immediately.
    
4. Vesicles formed spontaneously.
    

### Advantage

- Produces unilamellar vesicles.
    

---

## 3. Reverse Phase Evaporation Method (REV)

### Procedure

1. Surfactant and cholesterol dissolved in organic solvent.
    
2. Aqueous drug solution added.
    
3. Water-in-oil emulsion formed.
    
4. Solvent removed under reduced pressure.
    
5. Large unilamellar vesicles obtained.
    

### Advantage

- High entrapment efficiency for hydrophilic drugs.
    

---

## 4. Sonication Method

### Procedure

1. Multilamellar niosomes prepared.
    
2. Subjected to probe or bath sonication.
    
3. Large vesicles converted into small unilamellar vesicles.
    

### Advantage

- Uniform particle size.
    

---

## 5. Microfluidization Method

### Procedure

1. Two liquid streams collide at high velocity.
    
2. Uniform mixing occurs.
    
3. Small and homogeneous niosomes produced.
    

### Advantage

- Narrow size distribution.
    

---

## 6. Proniosome Method

### Procedure

1. Surfactant coated onto carrier such as sorbitol or maltodextrin.
    
2. Dry powder obtained (Proniosome).
    
3. Hydration before use forms niosomes.
    

### Advantage

- Excellent storage stability.
    
- Reduced aggregation problems.
    

---

# Flow Chart

```text
Non-Ionic Surfactant + Cholesterol
                ↓
      Dissolve in Solvent
                ↓
      Thin Film Formation
                ↓
       Hydration with Drug
                ↓
      Niosome Formation
                ↓
      Sonication/Size Reduction
                ↓
      Final Niosomal Dispersion
```

# Applications of Niosomes

## 1. Targeted Drug Delivery

- Deliver drugs selectively to target tissues.
    
- Reduce systemic toxicity.
    

### Example

- Anticancer drug delivery.
    

---

## 2. Controlled and Sustained Release

- Drug released slowly over long periods.
    
- Reduces dosing frequency.
    

---

## 3. Transdermal Drug Delivery

- Enhance penetration through skin.
    
- Improve bioavailability.
    

### Examples

- Diclofenac
    
- Ketoprofen
    

---

## 4. Ophthalmic Drug Delivery

- Increase corneal contact time.
    
- Improve ocular bioavailability.
    

### Examples

- Timolol
    
- Pilocarpine
    

---

## 5. Oral Drug Delivery

- Protect drugs from GI degradation.
    
- Improve absorption and bioavailability.
    

---

## 6. Parenteral Drug Delivery

- Used for intravenous administration.
    
- Improved circulation time.
    

### Example

- Doxorubicin-loaded niosomes.
    

---

## 7. Vaccine Delivery

- Act as vaccine adjuvants.
    
- Enhance immune response.
    

---

## 8. Gene Delivery

- Deliver DNA and RNA molecules.
    
- Improve cellular uptake.
    

---

# Advantages

- Biocompatible
    
- Biodegradable
    
- Stable than liposomes
    
- Low cost
    
- Can carry hydrophilic and lipophilic drugs
    
- Controlled release
    
- Improved bioavailability
    
- Targeted drug delivery
    

---

# Limitations

- Aggregation during storage
    
- Drug leakage
    
- Scale-up difficulties
    
- Some surfactants may cause toxicity
    

---

# Conclusion

Niosomes are non-ionic surfactant-based vesicular carriers used for targeted and controlled drug delivery. They are prepared by methods such as thin film hydration, ether injection, reverse phase evaporation, sonication, microfluidization, and proniosome techniques. Due to their stability, low cost, and ability to improve drug bioavailability, niosomes have applications in transdermal, ophthalmic, oral, parenteral, vaccine, and gene delivery systems.
