# Drug Absorption Modeling (10 Marks)

## Introduction

Drug absorption is the process by which a drug moves from its site of administration into the systemic circulation. Computational Drug Absorption Modeling uses mathematical and physiological models to predict the rate and extent of drug absorption. These models help in formulation development, bioavailability assessment, IVIVC establishment, and dosage form optimization.

---

# Definition

**Drug Absorption Modeling** is the mathematical simulation of the movement of a drug from the site of administration into systemic circulation using physiological and pharmacokinetic principles.

---

# Objectives of Drug Absorption Modeling

1. Predict oral drug absorption.
    
2. Estimate bioavailability.
    
3. Support formulation development.
    
4. Optimize dosage forms.
    
5. Establish IVIVC (In Vitro–In Vivo Correlation).
    
6. Reduce experimental studies.
    

---

# Factors Affecting Drug Absorption

## Drug-Related Factors

- Solubility
    
- Dissolution rate
    
- Particle size
    
- Lipophilicity (Log P)
    
- pKa
    
- Molecular weight
    

## Physiological Factors

- Gastric emptying time
    
- Intestinal transit time
    
- GI pH
    
- Blood flow
    
- Surface area of intestine

# Major Drug Absorption Models

## 1. Compartmental Absorption Model

### Principle

Assumes the GI tract behaves as one or more compartments.

### First-Order Absorption Model

Rate of Absorption = Ka × Amount Remaining

where:

- Ka = Absorption Rate Constant
    

### Characteristics

- Simple model.
    
- Widely used in pharmacokinetics.
    

### Limitation

- Does not represent actual GI physiology.
    

---

## 2. ACAT Model

### Full Form

**Advanced Compartmental Absorption and Transit Model**

### Principle

The gastrointestinal tract is divided into multiple compartments representing different intestinal regions.

### Components

- Stomach
    
- Duodenum
    
- Jejunum
    
- Ileum
    
- Colon
    

### Applications

- Oral absorption prediction.
    
- Food effect studies.
    
- Bioavailability prediction.
    

### Software

Used in **GastroPlus®**.

---

## 3. ADAM Model

### Full Form

**Advanced Dissolution, Absorption and Metabolism Model**

### Principle

Includes:

- Drug dissolution
    
- Intestinal absorption
    
- Gut wall metabolism
    
- Hepatic first-pass metabolism
    

### Advantages

- More realistic than ACAT.
    
- Predicts first-pass metabolism.
    

### Software

Used in **Simcyp®**.

---

## 4. PBPK Absorption Model

### Principle

Uses physiological parameters to simulate absorption.

Includes:

- GI anatomy
    
- Blood flow
    
- Transporters
    
- Metabolic enzymes
    

### Applications

- Pediatric studies.
    
- Drug-drug interaction studies.
    
- Special populations.
    

### Advantages

- Highly mechanistic.
    
- Excellent prediction capability.
    

---

# Important Parameters in Absorption Modeling

## Fraction Absorbed (Fa)

Fa = Amount Absorbed / Administered Dose

---

## Bioavailability (F)

F = (AUC Oral / AUC IV) × (Dose IV / Dose Oral)

---

## Absorption Rate Constant

Ka

Represents the speed of drug absorption.

---

## Effective Permeability

Peff

Measures drug permeability across intestinal membrane.

# Conclusion

Drug Absorption Modeling is a key component of computational drug disposition. It predicts how drugs are absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract and enter systemic circulation. Models such as ACAT, ADAM, and PBPK are widely used to optimize formulations, predict bioavailability, and support pharmaceutical development.
