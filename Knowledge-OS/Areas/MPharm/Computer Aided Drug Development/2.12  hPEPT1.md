# hPEPT1 (Human Peptide Transporter 1) (10 Marks)

## Introduction

**Human Peptide Transporter 1 (hPEPT1)** is an important influx transporter present in the small intestine. It is responsible for the absorption of dipeptides, tripeptides, and several peptide-like drugs. hPEPT1 plays a major role in enhancing oral drug absorption and bioavailability and is therefore an important target in drug design and computational drug disposition studies.

---

# Definition

**hPEPT1 (Human Peptide Transporter 1)** is a proton-coupled membrane transporter encoded by the **SLC15A1 gene**, which facilitates the uptake of dipeptides, tripeptides, and peptide-like drugs from the intestinal lumen into intestinal epithelial cells.

# Location of hPEPT1

Majorly present in:

- Duodenum
    
- Jejunum
    
- Ileum
    

Highest expression occurs in the **small intestine**, making it one of the most important drug absorption transporters.

---

# Mechanism of Transport

hPEPT1 uses the proton (H⁺) gradient across the intestinal membrane.

### Step 1

Drug or peptide binds to hPEPT1.

### Step 2

Proton (H⁺) binds simultaneously.

### Step 3

Transporter changes conformation.

### Step 4

Drug enters intestinal epithelial cell.

```text
Intestinal Lumen
        ↓
 Drug + H+
        ↓
     hPEPT1
        ↓
 Enterocyte
        ↓
 Blood Circulation
```

# Importance in Drug Absorption

Many drugs are intentionally designed as **prodrugs** to utilize hPEPT1-mediated absorption.

### Example

#### Acyclovir

Bioavailability = 10–20%

#### Valacyclovir (Prodrug)

Bioavailability ≈ 54%

Reason:

Valacyclovir is recognized by hPEPT1 and efficiently absorbed from the intestine.

---

# Role in Prodrug Design

hPEPT1 is widely exploited in pharmaceutical development.

### Strategy

Drug + Amino Acid/Peptide Moiety  
↓  
Recognized by hPEPT1  
↓  
Improved Absorption  
↓  
Higher Bioavailability

### Example

Acyclovir → Valacyclovir

This is one of the most famous transporter-targeted prodrug examples.

# Computational Modeling of hPEPT1

Transport kinetics generally follow **Michaelis-Menten kinetics**.

### Equation

J = (Jmax × C)/(Km + C)

Where:

- J = Transport rate
    
- Jmax = Maximum transport rate
    
- Km = Michaelis constant
    
- C = Drug concentration
