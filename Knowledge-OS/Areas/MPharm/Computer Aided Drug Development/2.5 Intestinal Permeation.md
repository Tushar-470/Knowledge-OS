# Intestinal Permeation (10 Marks)

## Introduction

Intestinal permeation is the process by which a drug crosses the intestinal membrane and enters the systemic circulation after oral administration. Along with solubility, intestinal permeability is a major determinant of oral drug absorption and bioavailability. Computational models are widely used to predict intestinal permeation during drug development.

**Absorption = Solubility + Permeability**

A drug may be highly soluble, but if it cannot permeate the intestinal membrane, absorption will be poor.

---

# Definition

**Intestinal Permeation** is the movement of drug molecules across the intestinal epithelial membrane from the gastrointestinal lumen into the blood circulation.

---

# Importance of Intestinal Permeation

1. Determines oral bioavailability.
    
2. Influences drug absorption rate.
    
3. Important for BCS classification.
    
4. Affects therapeutic efficacy.
    
5. Supports formulation development.
    
6. Helps predict human pharmacokinetics.

       ↓

       ↓

The small intestine is the major site of drug absorption because of:

- Large surface area
    
- Rich blood supply
    
- Long residence time
    
- Presence of villi and microvilli

# Mechanisms of Intestinal Permeation

## 1. Passive Diffusion

### Principle

Drug moves from higher concentration to lower concentration without energy expenditure.

### Characteristics

- Most common mechanism.
    
- Follows Fick's Law.
    
- Suitable for lipophilic drugs.
    

### Fick's First Law

dQ/dt = (D × A × K × ΔC)/(h)

where:

- dQ/dt = Rate of diffusion
    
- D = Diffusion coefficient
    
- A = Surface area
    
- K = Partition coefficient
    
- ΔC = Concentration gradient
    
- h = Membrane thickness
    

---

## 2. Paracellular Transport

### Principle

Drug passes through tight junctions between epithelial cells.

### Characteristics

- Suitable for hydrophilic drugs.
    
- Limited due to small pore size.
    

### Examples

- Atenolol
    
- Mannitol
    

---

## 3. Transcellular Transport

### Principle

Drug passes directly through epithelial cells.

### Characteristics

- Major route for lipophilic drugs.
    
- Depends on membrane permeability.
    

### Examples

- Propranolol
    
- Diazepam
    

---

## 4. Carrier-Mediated Transport

### Principle

Specific transport proteins assist drug movement.

### Types

#### Influx Transporters

Enhance absorption.

Examples:

- PEPT1
    
- OATP
    

#### Efflux Transporters

Reduce absorption.

Examples:

- P-glycoprotein (P-gp)
    
- BCRP
    
- MRP2
    

---

## 5. Endocytosis

### Principle

Drug enters cells through vesicle formation.

### Characteristics

- Important for peptides and proteins.
    
- Energy-dependent process.

# Factors Affecting Intestinal Permeation

## Drug Factors

- Molecular size
    
- Lipophilicity (Log P)
    
- Degree of ionization
    
- Hydrogen bonding
    
- Polar surface area
    

## Physiological Factors

- Intestinal pH
    
- Blood flow
    
- Membrane thickness
    
- Transporters
    
- Enzyme activity
    

---

# Permeability Coefficient

Effective Permeability:

Peff = (Rate of Drug Transport)/(Surface Area × Concentration)

### Interpretation

- High Peff → High absorption.
    
- Low Peff → Poor absorption.

# Models for Predicting Intestinal Permeation

## 1. Caco-2 Cell Model

### Characteristics

- Human intestinal epithelial cell line.
    
- Gold standard in vitro model.
    

### Application

- Permeability screening.
    

---

## 2. PAMPA

(Parallel Artificial Membrane Permeability Assay)

### Characteristics

- Artificial membrane model.
    
- Rapid screening method.
    

### Application

- Early drug discovery.
    

---

## 3. PBPK Models

### Characteristics

- Integrate permeability with physiological parameters.
    
- Predict in vivo absorption.
    

### Software

- GastroPlus
    
- Simcyp
    
- PK-Sim

# Conclusion

Intestinal permeation is a critical step in oral drug absorption and bioavailability. Drugs can cross the intestinal membrane through passive diffusion, paracellular transport, transcellular transport, carrier-mediated transport, or endocytosis. Computational models such as Caco-2, PAMPA, and PBPK models are widely used to predict intestinal permeability and support pharmaceutical development.
