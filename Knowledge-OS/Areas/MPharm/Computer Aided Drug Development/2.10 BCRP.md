# BCRP (Breast Cancer Resistance Protein) (10 Marks)

## Introduction

**Breast Cancer Resistance Protein (BCRP)** is an ATP-dependent efflux transporter belonging to the ATP-Binding Cassette (ABC) transporter family. It plays an important role in drug absorption, distribution, elimination, and multidrug resistance. Similar to P-glycoprotein (P-gp), BCRP pumps drugs out of cells and acts as a protective barrier against potentially harmful substances. It is particularly important in cancer chemotherapy and drug-drug interactions.

---

# Definition

**BCRP (Breast Cancer Resistance Protein)** is an ATP-dependent efflux transporter encoded by the **ABCG2 gene**, which actively transports drugs and xenobiotics from inside cells to the extracellular environment.

# Location of BCRP

BCRP is highly expressed in:

1. Small Intestine
    
2. Liver
    
3. Kidney
    
4. Placenta
    
5. Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)
    
6. Mammary Gland
    

These organs use BCRP as a protective mechanism to limit drug exposure.

---

# Mechanism of Action

### Step 1

Drug enters the cell.

### Step 2

BCRP recognizes the substrate.

### Step 3

ATP is hydrolyzed.

### Step 4

Drug is transported out of the cell.

### Step 5

Intracellular drug concentration decreases.

```text
Drug enters cell
       ↓
 BCRP binds drug
       ↓
 ATP Hydrolysis
       ↓
 Drug Efflux
       ↓
 Reduced Intracellular Drug
```

# Physiological Functions of BCRP

## 1. Limits Drug Absorption

In the intestine, BCRP pumps drugs back into the intestinal lumen and reduces oral absorption.

## 2. Protects the Brain

At the BBB, BCRP prevents excessive drug entry into the CNS.

## 3. Facilitates Drug Elimination

In liver and kidney, BCRP contributes to biliary and renal excretion.

## 4. Fetal Protection

In placenta, BCRP limits fetal exposure to xenobiotics and drugs.

---

# Role in Multidrug Resistance (MDR)

Cancer cells often overexpress BCRP.

### Consequences

- Increased drug efflux
    
- Reduced intracellular drug concentration
    
- Decreased effectiveness of chemotherapy
    

This phenomenon is called:

**Multidrug Resistance (MDR)**

BCRP is one of the major transporters responsible for anticancer drug resistance.

---

# Role at Blood-Brain Barrier (BBB)

BCRP is highly expressed on brain capillary endothelial cells.

### Function

- Pumps drugs from brain to blood.
    
- Limits CNS exposure.
    

### Importance

Many anticancer drugs fail to achieve adequate brain concentrations because of BCRP-mediated efflux.

---

# Clinical Importance

## 1. Statin Disposition

Rosuvastatin is a BCRP substrate.

BCRP inhibition can increase:

- Drug absorption
    
- Plasma concentration
    
- Risk of toxicity
    

---

## 2. Anticancer Therapy

Overexpression of BCRP causes resistance to:

- Methotrexate
    
- Topotecan
    
- Mitoxantrone
    

---

## 3. Drug-Drug Interactions

Co-administration of BCRP inhibitors may significantly alter drug exposure.

# Computational Modeling of BCRP

BCRP transport is often described using Michaelis-Menten kinetics.

### Equation

J = (Jmax × C)/(Km + C)

Where:

- J = Transport rate
    
- Jmax = Maximum transport capacity
    
- Km = Michaelis constant
    
- C = Drug concentration
    

### Applications

- PBPK modeling
    
- Drug-drug interaction prediction
    
- Oral absorption prediction
    
- BBB penetration prediction
    

### Software

- Simcyp
    
- GastroPlus
    
- PK-Sim

# Conclusion

BCRP (Breast Cancer Resistance Protein) is an ATP-dependent efflux transporter encoded by the ABCG2 gene. It plays a major role in drug absorption, distribution, excretion, blood-brain barrier protection, and multidrug resistance. Due to its significant influence on pharmacokinetics and anticancer drug response, BCRP is routinely incorporated into modern PBPK and computational drug disposition models.
