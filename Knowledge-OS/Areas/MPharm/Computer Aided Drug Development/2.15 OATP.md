# OATP (Organic Anion Transporting Polypeptides) (10 Marks)

## Introduction

**Organic Anion Transporting Polypeptides (OATPs)** are important membrane influx transporters that mediate the uptake of a wide variety of endogenous substances and drugs into cells. They belong to the **SLCO (Solute Carrier Organic Anion) family** and play a major role in drug absorption, hepatic uptake, tissue distribution, and drug-drug interactions. OATPs are especially important for the disposition of statins and many anticancer drugs.

---

# Definition

**OATPs (Organic Anion Transporting Polypeptides)** are sodium-independent influx transporters that facilitate the uptake of organic anions and various drugs across biological membranes.

---

# Family and Classification

### Family

**SLCO (Solute Carrier Organic Anion) Family**

### Major OATP Isoforms

1. OATP1B1 (SLCO1B1)
    
2. OATP1B3 (SLCO1B3)
    
3. OATP2B1 (SLCO2B1)
    

---

### Major Site

**Liver (Hepatocytes)**

OATPs transport drugs from blood into liver cells for metabolism and elimination.

# OATP1B1 (SLCO1B1)

## Location

Basolateral membrane of hepatocytes.

## Function

- Hepatic uptake of drugs.
    
- Important for statin disposition.
    

## Clinical Importance

Reduced OATP1B1 activity causes:

- Increased plasma statin concentration.
    
- Increased risk of myopathy.

# Clinical Importance

## 1. Statin Pharmacokinetics

Statins require OATP-mediated uptake into the liver.

### Example

Rosuvastatin

Blood  
↓  
OATP1B1  
↓  
Liver  
↓  
Cholesterol Reduction

Reduced OATP activity leads to:

- Higher plasma concentration.
    
- Increased muscle toxicity.
    

---

## 2. Drug-Drug Interactions

### Example

Rifampicin inhibits OATP1B1.

Result:

- Increased statin concentration.
    
- Increased risk of adverse effects.
    

---

## 3. Genetic Polymorphism

SLCO1B1 polymorphism may cause:

- Reduced transporter activity.
    
- Increased statin-induced myopathy.

# Computational Modeling of OATP

Transport kinetics follow **Michaelis-Menten kinetics**.

### Equation

J = (Jmax × C)/(Km + C)

Where:

- J = Transport rate
    
- Jmax = Maximum transport capacity
    
- Km = Michaelis constant
    
- C = Drug concentration
