# Drug Interactions: Introduction and Effect of Protein-Binding Interactions (10 Marks)

## Introduction

A **drug interaction** occurs when the pharmacological or pharmacokinetic effect of one drug is altered by the presence of another drug, food, or chemical substance. Drug interactions may increase or decrease the efficacy and safety of drugs. Pharmacokinetic interactions affect **Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, and Excretion (ADME)**.

---

# Protein-Binding Interactions

## Definition

Many drugs bind reversibly to plasma proteins such as:

- **Albumin** (acidic drugs)
    
- **α₁-Acid Glycoprotein (AAG)** (basic drugs)
    

Only the **free (unbound) drug** is pharmacologically active.

When two highly protein-bound drugs are administered together, one drug may displace the other from protein-binding sites, increasing the free drug concentration.

---

# Mechanism

```text
Drug A + Protein ⇌ Drug A–Protein Complex

Drug B added
        ↓
Drug B displaces Drug A
        ↓
Free Drug A increases
        ↓
Increased effect / toxicity
```

---

# Effects of Protein-Binding Interactions

## 1. Increase in Free Drug Concentration

Displacement from protein binding increases the free fraction of the drug.

### Result

- Increased pharmacological effect
    
- Increased toxicity
    

---

## 2. Change in Volume of Distribution (Vd)

As free drug increases:

- Drug distributes more into tissues.
    
- Apparent Vd increases.
    

---

## 3. Change in Clearance (CL)

For low-extraction drugs:

$$
CL = f_u \times CL_{int}
$$

Where:

- fu = Fraction unbound
    
- CLint = Intrinsic clearance
    

Increase in free drug → Increase in clearance.

---

## 4. Acute Effect

Immediately after displacement:

- Free drug concentration rises.
    
- Toxicity may occur.
    

This is the most clinically important phase.

---

## 5. Chronic (Steady-State) Effect

With time:

- Increased free drug causes increased elimination.
    
- Total drug concentration decreases.
    
- Free drug concentration returns near normal.
    

Therefore, long-term effects are often less significant than acute effects.

---

# Clinically Important Examples

### 1. Aspirin + Warfarin

- Aspirin displaces warfarin from albumin.
    
- Free warfarin increases.
    
- Increased anticoagulant effect.
    
- Risk of bleeding and hemorrhage.
    

### 2. Valproic Acid + Phenytoin

- Valproic acid displaces phenytoin.
    
- Free phenytoin increases.
    
- Neurotoxicity, ataxia, nystagmus may occur.
    

### 3. Sulfonamides + Bilirubin (Neonates)

- Sulfonamides displace bilirubin from albumin.
    
- Free bilirubin crosses BBB.
    
- Causes kernicterus.

# Conclusion

Protein-binding interactions occur when one drug displaces another from plasma protein binding sites, increasing the free drug concentration. This may lead to enhanced drug action, toxicity, changes in volume of distribution, and clearance. Clinically important examples include **Warfarin–Aspirin**, **Phenytoin–Valproic acid**, and **Sulfonamide–Bilirubin** interactions.
