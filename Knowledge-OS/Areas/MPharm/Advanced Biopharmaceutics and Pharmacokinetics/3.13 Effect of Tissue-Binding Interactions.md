# Effect of Tissue-Binding Interactions (5–10 Marks)

## Definition

Tissue-binding interactions occur when one drug displaces another drug from tissue-binding sites such as intracellular proteins, phospholipids, nucleic acids, or tissue receptors. This alters the distribution of the drug within the body and changes its pharmacokinetic behavior.

---

# Mechanism

Normally, some drugs are extensively stored in tissues.

When a second drug is administered:

- It competes for tissue-binding sites.
    
- The first drug is displaced from tissues.
    
- Drug moves from tissues into plasma.
    
- Plasma concentration temporarily increases.
    

---

# Effects of Tissue-Binding Interactions

## 1. Decrease in Volume of Distribution (Vd)

When drug is displaced from tissues:

$$
V_d
=
V_p
+
V_t
\left(
\frac{f_{u,p}}
{f_{u,t}}
\right)
$$


Increase in tissue free fraction (**fuₜ**) causes:

$$
V_d \downarrow
$$

### Result

- Less drug remains stored in tissues.
    
- More drug remains in plasma.
    

---

## 2. Increase in Plasma Drug Concentration

Displaced drug enters bloodstream.

### Result

- Temporary increase in plasma concentration.
    
- Increased pharmacological effect.
    
- Possible toxicity.
    

---

## 3. Increased Drug Elimination

Because more drug is present in plasma:

- More drug becomes available for metabolism and excretion.
    
- Clearance may increase.
    

### Result

- Faster removal from the body.
    

---

## 4. Reduction in Steady-State Drug Levels

After redistribution and elimination:

- Drug concentration eventually decreases.
    
- Therapeutic effect may reduce.
    

---

# Clinical Example: Digoxin–Quinidine Interaction

This is the **classic example** of tissue-binding interaction.

### Digoxin Alone

- Vd ≈ 7 L/kg
    
- Extensive tissue binding
    

### After Quinidine Administration

- Quinidine displaces digoxin from tissue sites.
    
- Vd decreases from about **7 L/kg to 4 L/kg**.
    
- Plasma digoxin concentration increases.
    
- Digoxin toxicity may occur.
    

### Toxic Effects

- Nausea
    
- Vomiting
    
- Bradycardia
    
- AV block
    
- Arrhythmias
    

Therefore, digoxin dose is usually reduced by about 50% when quinidine is started.

# Conclusion

Tissue-binding interactions occur when one drug displaces another from tissue storage sites. The major effects are **decreased volume of distribution, increased plasma concentration, increased elimination, and possible toxicity**. The **Digoxin–Quinidine interaction** is the most important clinical example.
