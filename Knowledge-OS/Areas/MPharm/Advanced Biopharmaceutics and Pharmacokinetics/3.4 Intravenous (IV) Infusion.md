# One-Compartment Model: Intravenous (IV) Infusion (10 Marks)

## Definition

In an **IV infusion one-compartment model**, the drug is administered continuously into the bloodstream at a constant rate. The body is considered as a **single homogeneous compartment**, and the drug distributes instantaneously throughout the body while elimination follows **first-order kinetics**.

---

# Diagram

```text
      IV Infusion
      (K₀ mg/hr)
           ↓
   ┌────────────────┐
   │ One Compartment│
   │      (Vd)      │
   └────────────────┘
           ↓
      Elimination
        (Kel)
```

Where:

- K₀ = Zero-order infusion rate
    
- Kel = First-order elimination rate constant
    
- Vd = Volume of distribution
    

---

# Assumptions

1. Body behaves as one compartment.
    
2. Drug distributes instantaneously.
    
3. Infusion occurs at a constant rate (K₀).
    
4. Elimination follows first-order kinetics.
    
5. Pharmacokinetic parameters remain constant.
    

---

# Drug Concentration During Infusion

### Rate of Change of Drug Amount

dA/dt = K0 - K_el_A

Where:

- A = Amount of drug in body
    
- K₀ = Infusion rate
    
- KelA = Elimination rate
    

---

# Derivation of Amount of Drug in Body

Integrating:

$$
A_t = \frac{K_0}{K_{el}}
\left(1 - e^{-K_{el}t}\right)
$$

Since

[  
C=\frac{A}{V_d}  
]

Therefore,

$$
C = \frac{A}{V_d}
$$
Since

$$
CL = V_d K_{el}
$$

Final Equation:

$$
\boxed{
C_t = \frac{K_0}{CL}
\left(1 - e^{-K_{el}t}\right)
}
$$

### This is the most important equation.

---

# Steady-State Concentration (Css)

At infinite time:

$$
e^{-K_{el}t} = 0
$$

Therefore,

$$
\boxed{
C_{ss} = \frac{K_0}{CL}
}
$$

### Significance

Steady state occurs when:

Rate of Drug Input = Rate of Drug Elimination

### Characteristics

- Concentration rises gradually.
    
- Approaches Css asymptotically.
    
- Never truly reaches Css.
    
- Practically achieved after 4–5 half-lives.
    

---

# Time Required to Reach Steady State

|Time|% Css Achieved|
|---|---|
|1 t½|50%|
|2 t½|75%|
|3 t½|87.5%|
|4 t½|93.75%|
|5 t½|96.9%|

### Important Point

$$
t_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{K_{el}}
$$

Time to steady state depends only on half-life, not on infusion rate.

---

# After Stopping Infusion

Drug concentration declines according to first-order kinetics.

### Equation

$$
C_t = C_{ss} e^{-K_{el}t}
$$

# Advantages of IV Infusion

1. Maintains constant plasma concentration.
    
2. Suitable for drugs with short half-life.
    
3. Useful in emergency situations.
    
4. Avoids fluctuations seen with repeated dosing.
    

---

# Limitations

1. Requires infusion equipment.
    
2. Risk of infection.
    
3. Not convenient for long-term therapy.
    
4. Continuous monitoring may be required.
    

---

# Clinical Examples

- Dopamine
    
- Heparin
    
- Lidocaine
    
- Aminophylline
    
- Insulin infusion

# Conclusion

In the **one-compartment IV infusion model**, drug enters the body at a constant rate and is eliminated by first-order kinetics. Plasma concentration increases exponentially until it approaches a steady-state concentration (**Css = K₀/CL**). The model is widely used for dosage regimen design and therapeutic drug monitoring.
