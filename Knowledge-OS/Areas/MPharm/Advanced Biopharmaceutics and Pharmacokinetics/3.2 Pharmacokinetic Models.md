# Pharmacokinetic Models (10 Marks)

## Definition

A **pharmacokinetic model** is a mathematical representation of the ADME (Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, and Excretion) processes of a drug in the body. It helps predict drug concentration in plasma and tissues over time.

---

# Need for Pharmacokinetic Models

1. Describe drug behavior in the body.
    
2. Calculate PK parameters such as Vd, CL, and t½.
    
3. Predict plasma drug concentrations.
    
4. Design dosage regimens.
    
5. Support therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM).
    

---

# Types of Pharmacokinetic Models

## 1. One-Compartment Open Model

### Definition

The body is considered as a single homogeneous compartment. Drug distributes instantaneously and uniformly throughout the body.

### Assumptions

- Instantaneous distribution.
    
- Uniform drug concentration.
    
- First-order elimination.
    
- Drug enters and leaves the body.
    

### Diagram

```
Dose → [Body Compartment] → Elimination
```

### Equation

$$
C = C_0 e^{-Kt}
$$

### Example

Theophylline, Gentamicin (approximation).

---

## 2. Two-Compartment Open Model

### Definition

Body is divided into:

- Central compartment (blood, liver, kidney, heart)
    
- Peripheral compartment (fat, muscle, bone)
    

Drug first enters central compartment and then distributes to peripheral compartment.

### Diagram

```
          k12
Central  ↔  Peripheral
          k21
   |
 Elimination
```

### Characteristics

- Distribution phase (α)
    
- Elimination phase (β)
    
- Biexponential concentration decline
    

### Equation

$$
C(t) = A e^{-\alpha t} + B e^{-\beta t}
$$

---

## 3. Multi-Compartment Model

### Definition

Body is represented by three or more compartments.

### Features

- More complex drug distribution.
    
- Triexponential decline.
    
- Used for drugs with extensive tissue distribution.
    

### Examples

- Chloroquine
    
- Gentamicin
    

---

## 4. Non-Compartmental Analysis (NCA)

### Definition

Model-independent approach that does not assume any compartment.

### Important Parameters

#### AUC

$$
AUC = \int C(t)\,dt
$$

#### AUMC

$$
AUMC = \int t\,C(t)\,dt
$$


#### MRT

$$
MRT = \frac{AUMC}{AUC}
$$


### Advantages

- Simple
    
- Widely used in bioavailability studies
    

---

## 5. Physiologically Based Pharmacokinetic (PBPK) Model

### Definition

A model based on actual physiological organs and blood flow.

### Features

- Each organ represented separately.
    
- Uses organ volume and blood flow data.
    
- Highly mechanistic and predictive.
    

### Applications

- Pediatric dose prediction
    
- Drug-drug interaction studies
    
- Food effect studies
    
- First-in-human dose prediction
    

---

# Linear vs Non-Linear Pharmacokinetic Models

### Linear PK

- AUC proportional to dose.
    
- Clearance constant.
    
- Most drugs follow linear kinetics.
    

### Non-Linear PK

- AUC not proportional to dose.
    
- Clearance changes with concentration.
    
- Example: Phenytoin.
    

---

# Conclusion

Pharmacokinetic models are mathematical tools used to describe and predict drug disposition in the body. Major models include **one-compartment, two-compartment, multi-compartment, non-compartmental analysis, and PBPK models**. These models are essential for dose optimization, therapeutic monitoring, and drug development.
