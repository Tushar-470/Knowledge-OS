# One-Compartment Model: Extravascular Administration (Oral Route) (10 Marks)

## Definition

The **one-compartment extravascular model** describes drug kinetics after administration by routes other than IV, such as:

- Oral
    
- Intramuscular (IM)
    
- Subcutaneous (SC)
    

The drug must first be **absorbed into systemic circulation**, after which it distributes instantaneously throughout the body and is eliminated by first-order kinetics.

---

# Model Diagram

```text
       Drug Dose
           ↓
   Absorption Site
           ↓ Ka
           ↓
   ┌───────────────┐
   │ One Compartment│
   │      (Vd)      │
   └───────────────┘
           ↓ Kel
      Elimination
```

Where:

- Ka = Absorption rate constant
    
- Kel = Elimination rate constant
    
- Vd = Volume of distribution
    

---

# Assumptions

1. Body behaves as one compartment.
    
2. Drug absorption follows first-order kinetics.
    
3. Drug elimination follows first-order kinetics.
    
4. Instantaneous distribution after absorption.
    
5. Ka > Kel in most situations.
    

---

# Plasma Concentration-Time Profile

After oral administration:

```text
Concentration

│
│                *
│              *   *
│            *       *
│          *           *
│        *               *
│      *                   *
│____*_______________________*____ Time
     Absorption     Elimination
```

### Phases

### 1. Absorption Phase

- Drug enters bloodstream.
    
- Concentration rises.
    

### 2. Peak Concentration (Cmax)

- Maximum plasma concentration achieved.
    

### 3. Elimination Phase

- Elimination exceeds absorption.
    
- Concentration decreases.
    

---

# Mathematical Model

### Amount Remaining at Absorption Site

$$
A_g = A_0 e^{-K_a t}
$$


Where:

- Ag = Amount remaining for absorption
    
- A₀ = Initial dose
    

---

### Plasma Concentration Equation

(Bateman Equation)

$$
\boxed{
C_t =
\frac{F \cdot D \cdot K_a}
{V_d \left( K_a - K_{el} \right)}
\left(
e^{-K_{el} t}
-
e^{-K_a t}
\right)
}
$$


Where:

- F = Bioavailability
    
- D = Dose
    
- Ka = Absorption rate constant
    
- Kel = Elimination rate constant
    
- Vd = Volume of distribution
    

### Most Important Equation

This is called the **Bateman Function**.

---

# Important Parameters

## 1. Cmax

Maximum plasma concentration.

### Significance

- Indicates extent of absorption.
    

---

## 2. Tmax

Time required to reach maximum concentration.

### Formula

# [  
$$
T_{\max}
=
\frac{\ln\!\left(\frac{K_a}{K_{el}}\right)}
{K_a - K_{el}}
$$


### Significance

- Indicates rate of absorption.
    

---

## 3. AUC

Area Under Curve

$$
AUC =
\frac{F \times \text{Dose}}
{CL}
$$

### Significance

- Measures extent of absorption.
    
- Indicates bioavailability.

# Method of Residuals (Feathering)

Used to determine:

- Ka
    
- Kel
    

### Steps

1. Plot concentration-time data.
    
2. Determine elimination line.
    
3. Extrapolate elimination phase.
    
4. Subtract observed values from extrapolated values.
    
5. Plot residuals.
    

---

# Flip-Flop Phenomenon

Occurs when:

$$
K_a < K_{el}
$$


### Characteristics

- Absorption becomes slower than elimination.
    
- Terminal slope represents Ka instead of Kel.
    

### Examples

- Sustained-release dosage forms
    
- Depot injections
    

---

# Advantages

1. Applicable to oral dosage forms.
    
2. Simple mathematical model.
    
3. Useful for bioavailability studies.
    

---

# Limitations

1. Assumes instant distribution.
    
2. Not suitable for multicompartment drugs.
    
3. Assumes first-order absorption and elimination.

# Conclusion

The **one-compartment extravascular model** describes drug absorption and elimination after oral or other non-IV administration. Drug concentration initially rises due to absorption, reaches **Cmax at Tmax**, and then declines by first-order elimination. The model is characterized by **Ka, Kel, Cmax, Tmax, and AUC** and is widely used in pharmacokinetic and bioavailability studies.
