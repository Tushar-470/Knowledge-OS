# Permeability–Solubility–Charge State and the pH Partition Hypothesis (10 Marks)

## Introduction

Drug absorption through biological membranes depends mainly on:

1. **Permeability** (ability to cross membrane)
    
2. **Solubility** (ability to dissolve)
    
3. **Charge State** (ionized or unionized form)
    

These factors form the basis of the **pH Partition Hypothesis**, which explains gastrointestinal drug absorption.

---

# 1. Permeability

## Definition

Permeability is the ability of a drug molecule to pass through a biological membrane.

### High Permeability Drugs

- Lipid soluble
    
- Easily cross membranes
    
- Better absorption
    

### Examples

- Diazepam
    
- Propranolol
    

### Low Permeability Drugs

- Hydrophilic
    
- Poor membrane penetration
    

### Examples

- Atenolol
    
- Gentamicin
    

✅ Higher permeability → Better absorption

---

# 2. Solubility

## Definition

Solubility is the ability of a drug to dissolve in GI fluids.

### Importance

Drug must dissolve before absorption.

### High Solubility

- Fast dissolution
    
- Better absorption
    

### Low Solubility

- Slow dissolution
    
- Poor absorption
    

### Examples

- BCS Class II drugs often have dissolution-limited absorption.
    

✅ Higher solubility → Better absorption

---

# 3. Charge State (Ionization)

Drugs exist in two forms:

### Unionized Form

- Lipid soluble
    
- Crosses membrane easily
    

### Ionized Form

- Water soluble
    
- Crosses membrane poorly
    

```text
Unionized Drug
      ↓
Membrane Crossing
      ↓
Absorption

Ionized Drug
      ↓
Poor Membrane Crossing
      ↓
Less Absorption
```

✅ Unionized form is absorbed better.

---

# pH Partition Hypothesis

## Definition

The pH Partition Hypothesis states that:

**"Only the unionized form of a drug can readily diffuse across biological membranes, and the degree of ionization depends on the pH of the medium and the pKa of the drug."**

---

# Weak Acids

### Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

[  
pH = pKa + \log \frac{A^-}{HA}  
]

Where:

- HA = Unionized acid
    
- A⁻ = Ionized acid
    

### In Stomach (pH 1–3)

- Weak acids remain mostly unionized.
    
- Better absorption.
    

### Example

- Aspirin
    
- Phenobarbital
    

```text
Weak Acid
Stomach (Low pH)
       ↓
Unionized
       ↓
Absorbed
```

---

# Weak Bases

### Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

[  
pH = pKa + \log \frac{B}{BH^+}  
]

Where:

- B = Unionized base
    
- BH⁺ = Ionized base
    

### In Intestine (pH 6–8)

- Weak bases become more unionized.
    
- Better absorption.
    

### Example

- Quinidine
    
- Morphine
    

```text
Weak Base
Intestine (High pH)
        ↓
Unionized
        ↓
Absorbed
```

---

# Relationship Between Permeability, Solubility and Charge State

|Property|Effect on Absorption|
|---|---|
|High Permeability|Absorption ↑|
|High Solubility|Absorption ↑|
|Unionized Form|Absorption ↑|
|Ionized Form|Absorption ↓|

---

# Limitations of pH Partition Hypothesis

1. Cannot explain active transport.
    
2. Cannot explain carrier-mediated absorption.
    
3. Cannot explain P-glycoprotein (P-gp) efflux.
    
4. Does not consider intestinal surface area.
    
5. Does not explain absorption of some ionized drugs.

# Conclusion

Drug absorption is governed by **permeability, solubility, and charge state**. According to the **pH Partition Hypothesis**, only the **unionized form of a drug readily crosses biological membranes**. Weak acids are absorbed better in acidic environments, whereas weak bases are absorbed better in alkaline environments.

# 30-Second Revision

### Key Formula

**Absorption ∝ Solubility × Permeability**

### pH Partition Theory

✅ Unionized = Absorbed

❌ Ionized = Poorly absorbed

### Examples

- Aspirin → Stomach
    
- Quinidine → Intestine
    

### Most Important Line

**"Only the unionized form of a drug can readily cross lipid membranes and undergo absorption."** ✅
