### **1. Introduction to Pharmacokinetics (PK) and Pharmacodynamics (PD)**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
*   **Pharmacokinetics (PK):** Defined as "what the body does to the drug." It is the study of the time course of drug absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion (ADME).
*   **Pharmacodynamics (PD):** Defined as "what the drug does to the body." It involves receptor binding, post-receptor effects, and biochemical/physiological effects.

**2. Phases of Pharmacokinetics (ADME):**
*   **Absorption:** Process by which a drug proceeds from the site of administration to the systemic circulation. *Key parameter: Bioavailability (F).*
*   **Distribution:** Reversible transfer of drug from systemic circulation to tissues. *Key parameter: Volume of Distribution (Vd).*
*   **Metabolism (Biotransformation):** Chemical alteration of the drug, primarily in the liver, to make it more water-soluble for excretion. Involves Phase I (CYP450) and Phase II (Conjugation) reactions.
*   **Excretion:** Irreversible elimination of the drug or its metabolites, primarily via the kidneys.

**3. Key Pharmacokinetic Parameters:**
*   **Half-life ($t_{1/2}$):** Time required for plasma concentration to decrease by 50%. Determines dosing frequency.
*   **Clearance (CL):** Volume of plasma cleared of drug per unit time. Determines maintenance dose.
*   **Area Under the Curve (AUC):** Measure of total systemic exposure.

**4. Principles of Pharmacodynamics:**
*   **Receptors:** Proteins that recognize and bind endogenous ligands or drugs.
*   **Agonists vs. Antagonists:** Agonists bind and activate receptors; antagonists bind but do not activate, blocking endogenous ligands.
*   **Dose-Response Relationship:** Graded (individual) vs. Quantal (population) dose-response curves.

**5. PK/PD Integration:**
*   Links drug concentration (PK) to clinical effect (PD).
*   Helps determine the therapeutic window: the range between the Minimum Effective Concentration (MEC) and the Minimum Toxic Concentration (MTC).

### **2. Drug Interactions**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
A drug interaction occurs when the pharmacological effect of a drug is altered by the presence of another drug, food, or environmental factor. They are broadly classified into Pharmacokinetic and Pharmacodynamic interactions.

**2. Pharmacokinetic Interactions:**
*   **Absorption:** Drugs may alter gastric pH (e.g., antacids reducing absorption of ketoconazole), form complexes (e.g., tetracyclines with calcium), or alter gastric emptying (e.g., metoclopramide speeding up absorption).
*   **Distribution:** Competition for plasma protein binding (e.g., Warfarin displaced by NSAIDs increases free warfarin, leading to toxicity).
*   **Metabolism:**
    *   *Enzyme Induction:* One drug increases the synthesis of CYP450 enzymes, speeding up the metabolism of another (e.g., Rifampin induces CYP3A4, reducing oral contraceptive efficacy).
    *   *Enzyme Inhibition:* One drug blocks CYP450 enzymes, causing accumulation of another (e.g., Ketoconazole inhibits CYP3A4, increasing cyclosporine toxicity).
*   **Excretion:** Alteration of urinary pH or competition for active tubular secretion (e.g., Probenecid inhibits penicillin excretion, prolonging its half-life).

**3. Pharmacodynamic Interactions:**
*   **Synergistic (1+1 = 3):** Combined effect is greater than the sum of individual effects (e.g., Sulfamethoxazole + Trimethoprim).
*   **Additive (1+1 = 2):** Combined effect equals the sum of individual effects (e.g., two NSAIDs together).
*   **Antagonistic (1+1 < 1):** One drug decreases or blocks the effect of another (e.g., Beta-agonist Salbutamol vs. Beta-blocker Propranolol).

**4. Clinical Significance:**
Interactions can lead to treatment failure (sub-therapeutic levels) or adverse drug reactions (toxicity). Managing them requires dose adjustment, spacing out administration times, or substituting drugs.

### **3. Application of PK: Modified-Release Drug Products**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
Modified-Release (MR) drug products are formulations where the rate and/or site of release of the active drug are altered from conventional immediate-release forms.

**2. Types of MR Products:**
*   **Extended-Release (ER/SR):** Releases the drug slowly over a prolonged period, reducing dosing frequency.
*   **Delayed-Release (DR):** Releases the drug at a time later than immediately after administration (e.g., enteric-coated tablets to bypass stomach acid).
*   **Targeted-Release:** Formulated to release the drug at a specific site in the body.

**3. Rationale and PK Applications:**
*   **Flattening Peak-to-Trough Fluctuations:** ER formulations prevent the sharp peaks (which cause toxicity) and deep troughs (which cause treatment failure) seen with immediate-release forms.
*   **Improved Patient Compliance:** Reduces dosing frequency (e.g., from TID to Once Daily).
*   **Reduced Side Effects:** By controlling the absorption rate, $C_{max}$ is lowered, reducing concentration-dependent side effects.

**4. PK Considerations and Challenges:**
*   **Absorption Rate Limits:** In ER formulations, the absorption rate is slower than the elimination rate ($K_a < K_{el}$). This leads to "Flip-Flop" kinetics, where the elimination phase is delayed as it depends on the slow absorption of the drug.
*   **Dose Dumping:** A sudden, unintended, rapid release of the entire dose, potentially causing acute toxicity. This is a major safety concern for ER formulations.
*   **First-Pass Metabolism:** Drugs with extensive hepatic first-pass metabolism may have reduced bioavailability in ER forms because the slow release keeps them in the liver/gut wall longer.

### **4. Application of PK: Targeted Drug Delivery Systems**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
Targeted Drug Delivery Systems (TDDS) aim to direct a drug exclusively to its site of action while minimizing exposure to non-target tissues, thereby maximizing therapeutic efficacy and minimizing systemic toxicity.

**2. Approaches to Targeting:**
*   **Passive Targeting:** Exploits natural physiological conditions. Example: The Enhanced Permeability and Retention (EPR) effect allows nanoparticles to leak out of tumor vasculature and accumulate in cancer tissue.
*   **Active Targeting:** Uses specific ligands (antibodies, peptides) attached to the carrier to bind to overexpressed receptors on target cells (e.g., folate-conjugated nanoparticles targeting folate receptors on cancer cells).
*   **Triggered Targeting:** Release is induced by an external or internal stimulus (pH, temperature, magnetic field, enzymes).

**3. PK Applications and Alterations:**
*   **Volume of Distribution ($V_d$):** Altered significantly. By encapsulating a highly lipophilic or toxic drug in a nanoparticle, the drug is confined to the plasma/vascular compartment until it reaches the target.
*   **Bioavailability:** Can be enhanced for poorly soluble drugs by formulating them as nanocrystals or lipid carriers.
*   **Half-life and Clearance:** Carriers like PEGylated liposomes evade reticuloendothelial system (RES) uptake, drastically reducing systemic clearance and increasing circulation half-life.

**4. Examples:**
*   **Liposomal Doxorubicin (Doxil):** Reduces cardiotoxicity of doxorubicin while maintaining anti-tumor efficacy.
*   **Monoclonal Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs):** Deliver highly potent cytotoxics directly to cancer cells.

### **5. Introduction to PK/PD of Biotechnological Products**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
Biotechnological products (biologics) are medicinal preparations derived from living organisms or produced using biotechnology. They include proteins, peptides, monoclonal antibodies, and nucleic acid-based products.

**2. Differences from Small Molecule Drugs:**
*   **Size and Complexity:** Biologics are massive, complex 3D molecules compared to small synthetic molecules.
*   **Route of Administration:** Cannot be given orally due to enzymatic degradation in the GI tract and poor membrane permeability. Usually given parenterally (IV, SC, IM).
*   **Metabolism:** Not metabolized by liver CYP450 enzymes. Instead, they undergo proteolytic degradation to amino acids.

**3. Unique PK Features of Biologics:**
*   **Distribution:** Large molecular size limits tissue distribution. $V_d$ is generally small, often close to plasma volume (3-5 L).
*   **Target-Mediated Drug Disposition (TMDD):** The drug binds to its target with high affinity, and this binding directly influences the drug's clearance and distribution. Often results in non-linear PK.
*   **Immunogenicity:** Biologics can induce an immune response, forming Anti-Drug Antibodies (ADAs). ADAs can neutralize the drug (loss of efficacy) or alter its clearance.
*   **Route-dependent Clearance:** SC administration often results in slower absorption via the lymphatic system, leading to prolonged half-life compared to IV administration.

**4. General PD Features:**
*   High specificity for target receptors.
*   Often exhibit a direct correlation between plasma concentration and pharmacodynamic effect, but with a time delay due to distribution to the effect site.

### **6. PK/PD of Proteins and Peptides**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
Peptides are short chains of amino acids (typically <50), while proteins are larger, folded structures. They include hormones (insulin, erythropoietin) and cytokines.

**2. Pharmacokinetics (PK):**
*   **Absorption:**
    *   Poor oral bioavailability due to GI peptidases and low permeability.
    *   Administered via IV, SC, or IM.
    *   Following SC injection, smaller peptides $(<10 kDa)$ are absorbed primarily by blood capillaries, while larger proteins $(>16 kDa)$ are absorbed via the lymphatic system.
*   **Distribution:**
    *   Limited to extracellular fluid due to high MW and hydrophilicity.
    *   May bind to specific plasma proteins (e.g., IGF-1 binds to binding proteins).
*   **Metabolism:**
    *   Degraded by proteases and peptidases ubiquitously in the blood, liver, and kidneys.
    *   They do not induce or inhibit CYP450 enzymes.
*   **Excretion:**
    *   Small peptides are filtered by the glomerulus and reabsorbed/degraded in the proximal tubule.
    *   Larger proteins are cleared via receptor-mediated endocytosis in various tissues.

**3. Pharmacodynamics (PD):**
*   Proteins/peptides act on cell-surface receptors (e.g., GPCRs, receptor tyrosine kinases) rather than intracellular targets.
*   Often exhibit a steep dose-response curve and may show "bell-shaped" dose-response curves (where very high doses cause diminished effect).

### **7. PK/PD of Monoclonal Antibodies (mAbs)**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) are highly specific, mono-specific antibodies produced by identical immune cells. Examples: Trastuzumab, Infliximab, Rituximab.

**2. Pharmacokinetics (PK):**
*   **Absorption:**
    *   Administered via IV or SC/IM. If given SC, absorption occurs mainly via the lymphatics due to large size ($\sim 150 kDa$). Bioavailability is typically 50-80%.
*   **Distribution:**
    *   $V_d$ is low (3-5 L), confined to the vascular space, though tissue penetration is slow.
*   **Metabolism & Elimination:**
    *   Not cleared by kidneys/liver conventional mechanisms.
    *   Cleared via two pathways:
        1.  **Target-Mediated Clearance (Non-linear):** The mAb binds to its specific antigen on cells, and the complex is internalized and degraded by lysosomes. At high doses, this pathway saturates.
        2.  **Reticuloendothelial System (Linear):** Non-specific pinocytosis by RES cells.
*   **Neonatal Fc Receptor (FcRn):** mAbs bind to FcRn in endosomes, which recycles them back to the plasma, significantly prolonging their half-life (days to weeks).

**3. Pharmacodynamics (PD):**
*   Mechanisms of action include: blocking ligand-receptor interactions, activating complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC), antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), and inducing apoptosis.

### **8. PK/PD of Oligonucleotides**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
Oligonucleotides are short, synthetic strands of DNA or RNA (e.g., Antisense oligonucleotides - ASOs, siRNA). They act by targeting messenger RNA (mRNA) to silence or modify gene expression.

**2. Pharmacokinetics (PK):**
*   **Absorption:** Due to their high negative charge and polarity, they do not cross cell membranes easily. Administered via IV, SC, or intrathecal (into the spinal cord for CNS targets). Chemical modifications (e.g., phosphorothioate backbone) improve stability.
*   **Distribution:**
    *   Distribute primarily to highly perfused organs (liver, kidney, spleen).
    *   Often conjugated with N-acetylgalactosamine (GalNAc) to specifically target hepatocytes in the liver.
*   **Metabolism:** Resistant to nucleases due to chemical modifications. Metabolized slowly by endo- and exonucleases into shorter fragments.
*   **Excretion:** Metabolites are primarily excreted via the urine.

**3. Pharmacodynamics (PD):**
*   **ASOs:** Bind to target mRNA via Watson-Crick base pairing, leading to mRNA degradation via RNase H1, preventing translation into protein.
*   **siRNA:** Loaded into the RNA-Induced Silencing Complex (RISC) in the cytoplasm, which cleaves the target mRNA.
*   **Effect Kinetics:** The pharmacodynamic effect (protein reduction) is delayed compared to peak plasma concentration, as it takes time for existing proteins to degrade and for mRNA translation to be suppressed. Effects can last for weeks to months.

### **9. PK/PD of Vaccines (Immunotherapy)**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
Vaccines are biological preparations that provide active acquired immunity to a particular disease. Immunotherapy includes both prophylactic vaccines and therapeutic immune-modulators.

**2. Pharmacokinetics (PK):**
*   Traditional PK concepts (ADME) are often not applicable to vaccines in the classical sense, as the "drug" (antigen) is usually administered in microgram quantities and is rapidly cleared by the immune system.
*   **Absorption:** Usually administered IM or SC. The antigen is taken up by Antigen-Presenting Cells (APCs) at the injection site.
*   **Distribution:** Distributed via lymphatic drainage to local lymph nodes.
*   **Metabolism/Elimination:** Antigens are degraded into peptides by APCs and presented to T-cells. The vaccine components are cleared locally and systemically within hours to days.

**3. Pharmacodynamics (PD):**
*   The PD of vaccines is actually the measurement of the **immune response**.
*   **Innate Immunity (Early Phase):** APCs (dendritic cells) process the antigen and migrate to lymph nodes. Adjuvants are included in vaccines to enhance this local immune reaction.
*   **Adaptive Immunity (Late Phase):**
    *   *Cell-mediated:* Activation of CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells.
    *   *Humoral:* B-cells produce specific antibodies (IgM, then IgG).
*   **Immunological Memory:** The hallmark PD effect of vaccines is the creation of memory B and T cells, which provide long-lasting protection upon future exposure to the pathogen.
*   *Therapeutic Cancer Vaccines (e.g., CAR-T cells, immune checkpoint inhibitors):* Here, PK tracks the cellular distribution of engineered T-cells, and PD is measured by tumor regression and cytokine release.

### **10. PK/PD of Gene Therapies**
**(10 Marks)**

**1. Introduction:**
Gene therapy involves the introduction, removal, or alteration of genetic material within a patient's cells to treat or cure disease. It relies on vectors (usually modified viruses like AAV or lentiviruses, or lipid nanoparticles) to deliver the genetic payload.

**2. Pharmacokinetics (Biodistribution):**
*   **Absorption:** Administered locally (e.g., intravitreal for eye diseases) or systemically (e.g., IV for systemic diseases).
*   **Distribution (Biodistribution):** PK is measured by tracking the vector genome using PCR.
    *   Following IV administration, AAV vectors predominantly distribute to the liver, but can also be found in the spleen, kidney, and heart.
    *   The goal is to restrict distribution only to target tissues to avoid off-target effects.
*   **Metabolism & Excretion:** The vector particles are taken up into cells (endocytosis), uncoated, and the genetic material is delivered to the nucleus. The empty capsids and excess genetic material are degraded by cellular proteases and nucleases, with fragments excreted via urine or bile. Vector shedding (excretion into saliva, semen, etc.) is a key PK safety consideration.

**3. Pharmacodynamics (Transgene Expression):**
*   The "drug effect" is not the vector itself, but the **transgene expression** (production of the therapeutic protein) by the transduced cells.
*   **Time Delay:** There is a significant delay between vector administration and clinical effect, as the vector must enter the cell, uncoat, and the cell must transcribe and translate the new gene into a protein.
*   **Durability:** The PD effect can be extremely long-lasting (months to years) if the genetic material integrates into the host genome or exists as stable episomes in non-dividing cells.
*   **Dose-Response:** Higher doses generally increase transduction efficiency but also increase the risk of immune reactions (e.g., anti-AAV capsid antibodies) and dose-dependent hepatotoxicity.
