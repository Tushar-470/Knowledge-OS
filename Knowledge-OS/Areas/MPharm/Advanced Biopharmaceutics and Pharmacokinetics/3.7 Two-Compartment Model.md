# Two-Compartment Model (10 Marks)

## Definition

The **Two-Compartment Model** is a pharmacokinetic model in which the body is divided into:

1. **Central Compartment**
    
2. **Peripheral Compartment**
    

After administration, the drug first enters the central compartment and then distributes to the peripheral compartment. Elimination occurs from the central compartment. This model is more realistic than the one-compartment model because drug distribution is not instantaneous.

---

# Compartments

## 1. Central Compartment

Includes:

- Blood
    
- Heart
    
- Liver
    
- Kidney
    
- Lungs
    

These organs receive blood rapidly.

---

## 2. Peripheral Compartment

Includes:

- Muscle
    
- Fat
    
- Skin
    
- Bone
    

These tissues receive blood slowly.

---

# Assumptions

1. Drug enters central compartment immediately.
    
2. Distribution occurs between compartments.
    
3. Elimination occurs only from central compartment.
    
4. Transfer follows first-order kinetics.
    
5. Each compartment is well mixed.

# Phases of Two-Compartment Model

## 1. Distribution Phase (α Phase)

### Characteristics

- Initial rapid decline in concentration.
    
- Drug moves from central compartment to peripheral tissues.
    
- Distribution predominates.
    

### Called

**Alpha (α) Phase**

---

## 2. Elimination Phase (β Phase)

### Characteristics

- Slower decline in concentration.
    
- Drug elimination predominates.
    
- Distribution equilibrium achieved.
    

### Called

**Beta (β) Phase**

---

# Mathematical Equation

The plasma concentration is represented by a biexponential equation:

$$
C_t = A e^{-\alpha t} + B e^{-\beta t}
$$


Where:

- Ct = Plasma concentration at time t
    
- A and B = Intercepts
    
- α = Distribution rate constant
    
- β = Elimination rate constant
    

⭐ Very important equation.

# Clinical Examples

### Drugs Following Two-Compartment Model

- Digoxin
    
- Lidocaine
    
- Gentamicin
    
- Theophylline
    
- Tobramycin
    

---

# Advantages

1. More realistic than one-compartment model.
    
2. Describes tissue distribution accurately.
    
3. Useful in dosage regimen design.
    
4. Better prediction of plasma drug levels.
    

---

# Limitations

1. More complex calculations.
    
2. Requires multiple blood samples.
    
3. Difficult parameter estimation.
    

---

# Comparison: One vs Two Compartment Model

|Feature|One Compartment|Two Compartment|
|---|---|---|
|Distribution|Instantaneous|Gradual|
|Curve|Monoexponential|Biexponential|
|Phases|One|α and β|
|Accuracy|Lower|Higher|

---

# Conclusion

The **Two-Compartment Model** divides the body into a central and peripheral compartment. After administration, the drug distributes rapidly into tissues (α phase) followed by elimination (β phase). The model is represented by the biexponential equation:

[  
C_t = Ae^{-\alpha t}+Be^{-\beta t}  
]

and is widely used for drugs showing significant tissue distribution.
