# Multi-Compartment Model (10 Marks)

## Definition

The **Multi-Compartment Model** is a pharmacokinetic model in which the body is represented by **two or more interconnected compartments**. Drug distribution is not instantaneous; instead, it occurs at different rates in different tissues. This model better describes the behavior of most drugs in the body.

---

# Why Multi-Compartment Model?

In reality, drugs do not distribute equally to all tissues.

### Rapidly Perfused Organs

- Heart
    
- Liver
    
- Kidney
    
- Brain
    

### Slowly Perfused Organs

- Fat
    
- Muscle
    
- Skin
    

Therefore, a single-compartment model cannot accurately describe drug distribution.

---

# Types of Multi-Compartment Models

## 1. Two-Compartment Model

### Compartments

### Central Compartment

Contains:

- Blood
    
- Heart
    
- Liver
    
- Kidney
    
- Lungs
    

### Peripheral Compartment

Contains:

- Fat
    
- Muscle
    
- Skin
    
- Bone
    

### Diagram

```text
             K12
Central  ↔  Peripheral
             K21
   │
   ↓
 Elimination (Kel)
```

Where:

- K12 = Transfer from central to peripheral
    
- K21 = Transfer from peripheral to central
    
- Kel = Elimination rate constant
    

---

## 2. Three-Compartment Model

### Compartments

1. Central compartment
    
2. Shallow peripheral compartment
    
3. Deep peripheral compartment
    

### Diagram

```text
            K12
Central ↔ Peripheral 1
   │
   │K13
   ↓
Peripheral 2

   │
 Elimination
```

Used for drugs with extensive tissue distribution.

```text
 α Phase    β Phase
```

---

# Phases

## 1. Distribution Phase (α Phase)

- Rapid decline in concentration.
    
- Drug distributes from central to peripheral tissues.
    

### Characteristics

- Fast process
    
- Dominated by distribution
    

---

## 2. Elimination Phase (β Phase)

- Slower decline.
    
- Drug eliminated from body.
    

### Characteristics

- Dominated by elimination
    

---

# Mathematical Equation

For a two-compartment model:

$$
C_t = A e^{-\alpha t} + B e^{-\beta t}
$$


Where:

- A and B = Intercepts
    
- α = Distribution rate constant
    
- β = Elimination rate constant
    

### This is called a biexponential equation.

---

# Comparison of Models

|Feature|One Compartment|Multi Compartment|
|---|---|---|
|Distribution|Instantaneous|Gradual|
|Curve|Monoexponential|Biexponential/Triexponential|
|Compartments|One|Two or More|
|Accuracy|Lower|Higher|
|Clinical Relevance|Limited|More Realistic|

---

# Examples of Drugs Following Multi-Compartment Model

### Two-Compartment

- Lidocaine
    
- Digoxin
    
- Gentamicin
    
- Theophylline
    

### Three-Compartment

- Thiopentone
    
- Diazepam
    
- Propofol
    

---

# Advantages

1. More realistic representation of the body.
    
2. Better prediction of plasma concentrations.
    
3. Useful for dosage regimen design.
    
4. Describes tissue distribution accurately.
    

---

# Limitations

1. Complex calculations.
    
2. Difficult parameter estimation.
    
3. Requires extensive sampling.

# Conclusion

The **Multi-Compartment Model** assumes that the body consists of two or more compartments with different drug distribution characteristics. Drug concentration declines in multiple phases, producing **biexponential or triexponential curves**. The **two-compartment model** is the most commonly used and provides a more realistic description of drug disposition than the one-compartment model.
