# One-Compartment Open Model – IV Bolus (10 Marks)

## Definition

The **One-Compartment Open Model (IV Bolus)** is the simplest pharmacokinetic model in which the body is considered as a single homogeneous compartment. After an intravenous (IV) bolus injection, the drug distributes instantaneously throughout the body and is eliminated by first-order kinetics.

# Assumptions

1. Body acts as a single compartment.
    
2. Drug distributes instantly and uniformly.
    
3. Drug concentration is same throughout the body.
    
4. Elimination occurs from the compartment.
    
5. Elimination follows first-order kinetics.
    

---

# Plasma Concentration-Time Equation

For first-order elimination:

$$
\frac{dC}{dt} = -K_{el}C
$$


After integration:

$$
C_t = C_0 e^{-K_{el}t}
$$


Where:

- (C_t) = Concentration at time t
    
- (C_0) = Initial concentration
    
- (K_{el}) = Elimination rate constant
    
- (t) = Time

# Important Pharmacokinetic Parameters

## 1. Volume of Distribution (Vd)

$$
V_d = \frac{\text{Dose}}{C_0}
$$

Represents the apparent volume in which the drug is distributed.

---

## 2. Elimination Rate Constant (Kel)

$$
K_{el} = \frac{0.693}{t_{1/2}}
$$


Indicates the rate of drug removal.

---

## 3. Half-Life (t½)

$$
t_{1/2} = \frac{0.693}{K_{el}}
$$


Time required for plasma concentration to decrease by 50%.

---

## 4. Clearance (CL)
$$
CL = K_{el} \times V_d
$$

or

$$
CL = \frac{\text{Dose}}{\text{AUC}}
$$


Volume of plasma cleared of drug per unit time.

---

## 5. Area Under Curve (AUC)

$$
AUC = \frac{C_0}{K_{el}}
$$

or

$$
AUC = \frac{\text{Dose}}{CL}
$$


Represents total drug exposure.

---

---

# Advantages

- Simple model.
    
- Easy calculation of PK parameters.
    
- Useful for many drugs showing monoexponential decline.
    

# Limitations

- Assumes instantaneous distribution.
    
- Not suitable for drugs with extensive tissue distribution.
    

---

# Conclusion

The One-Compartment Open Model (IV Bolus) assumes instantaneous distribution of drug throughout the body and first-order elimination. The plasma concentration declines monoexponentially and important parameters such as **Vd, Kel, t½, CL, and AUC** can be easily calculated. It forms the basis for understanding more complex pharmacokinetic models.
