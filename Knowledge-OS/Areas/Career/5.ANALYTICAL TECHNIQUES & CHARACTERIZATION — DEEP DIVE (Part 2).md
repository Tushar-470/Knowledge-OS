## 4.1 TECHNIQUE SELECTION GUIDE

  

| Technique | Size Range | What It Measures | When to Use |

|:---|:---|:---|:---|

| **Laser Diffraction** | 0.1–3000 µm | Volume-weighted particle size distribution | API PSD; granule sizing; suspension droplets; inhalation aerosols |

| **Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS)** | 1 nm–10 µm (ideal 10–1000 nm) | Intensity-weighted hydrodynamic diameter | Nanoparticles, liposomes, LNPs, nanoemulsions, protein aggregates |

| **Zeta Potential** | Colloidal range | Surface charge (mV) | Colloidal stability prediction; nanoparticle optimization |

| **Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)** | 1 nm–mm | Surface morphology; shape | API crystal habit; granule/pellet surface; coating quality; fracture surface |

| **Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)** | 0.1–500 nm | Internal structure; true nanoparticle size | Liposome bilayer; LNP internal structure; nanocrystal morphology |

| **Nanoparticle Tracking Analysis (NTA)** | 10–2000 nm | Number-weighted size; individual particle tracking | Exosomes; protein aggregates; heterogeneous nanoparticle populations |

| **Sieve Analysis** | 75 µm–25 mm | Mass-weighted distribution | API/excipient raw material grading; granule sizing |

| **Coulter Counter** | 0.4–1600 µm | Number/volume PSD | Parenteral sub-visible particles (USP <788>) |

## 4.2 DYNAMIC LIGHT SCATTERING (DLS)

  

### A) Principle

DLS (also called **Photon Correlation Spectroscopy, PCS**) measures the **Brownian motion** of particles in suspension. Smaller particles move faster → more rapid fluctuations in scattered light intensity. The autocorrelation function of intensity fluctuations is analyzed to give the **diffusion coefficient (D)**, then converted to **hydrodynamic diameter (dH)** via the **Stokes-Einstein equation:**

  

**dH = kBT / (3πηD)**

  

Where: kB = Boltzmann constant, T = temperature (K), η = viscosity of medium, D = diffusion coefficient.

  

### B) Key Parameters Measured

  

| Parameter | What It Means | Target Values |

|:---|:---|:---|

| **Z-average diameter (nm)** | Intensity-weighted mean hydrodynamic diameter | 50–200 nm for most nanoformulations |

| **Polydispersity Index (PDI)** | Width of size distribution; 0 = monodisperse, 1 = very broad | **<0.2** for drug delivery; <0.3 acceptable; >0.5 = too polydisperse for DLS |

| **Size distribution (number/volume/intensity)** | Histogram of particle sizes | Check for bimodal peaks (indicates aggregation or two populations) |

  

### C) Industry Acceptance Criteria

  

| Nanoformulation Type | Size Target | PDI Target |

|:---|:---|:---|

| **Liposomes (Doxil-type)** | 80–120 nm | <0.10 |

| **LNPs (mRNA delivery)** | 60–100 nm | <0.20 |

| **Polymeric nanoparticles** | 100–300 nm | <0.25 |

| **Nanoemulsions** | 50–200 nm | <0.20 |

| **PLGA microspheres** | 1–100 µm (use laser diffraction, not DLS) | — |

| **Parenteral sub-visible (USP <788>)** | Must be <10 µm and <25 µm per limits | — |

  

### D) Common DLS Errors and How to Avoid Them

  

| Error | Cause | Solution |

|:---|:---|:---|

| **Large PDI (>0.5)** | Aggregated sample; dust; multiple populations | Filter sample (0.22–0.45 µm); sonicate briefly; dilute |

| **Irreproducible results** | Air bubbles; sample too concentrated; temperature not equilibrated | Degas sample; dilute to translucent (count rate 200–500 kcps); equilibrate 120 sec |

| **Intensity peak ≠ number peak** | Large particles dominate intensity (I ∝ d⁶); few large particles shift Z-average | Always report BOTH intensity and number distributions; clarify which is used |

| **Size too large** | Aggregation; sedimentation during measurement | Measure immediately after preparation; use fresh samples |

  

### Interview Q: "Why does the intensity-weighted DLS size differ from TEM size?"

> **Model Answer:** DLS measures the **hydrodynamic diameter** — the particle PLUS its hydration layer/PEG corona/electric double layer. Also, DLS is **intensity-weighted**, meaning larger particles scatter disproportionately more light (intensity ∝ d⁶), so even a tiny fraction of large particles skews the Z-average upward. TEM measures the **dry, core diameter** of individual particles from images — no hydration layer. So for PEGylated liposomes: DLS might report 120 nm, while TEM shows 80 nm core because PEG corona (5–10 nm on each side) and hydration shell are included in DLS but not visible in TEM. **Always report both when possible** and specify which technique in publications.

## 4.3 ZETA POTENTIAL

  

### A) Principle

Zeta potential (ζ) measures the **electrical potential at the slipping plane** of a particle's electrical double layer. Determined by **Electrophoretic Light Scattering (ELS)** — applying an electric field → particles move at velocity proportional to charge → measured as electrophoretic mobility → converted to ζ via **Henry equation**.

  

### B) Stability Prediction

  

| Zeta Potential (mV) | Colloidal Stability |

|:---|:---|

| **±0 to ±5** | Rapid aggregation/flocculation |

| **±10 to ±20** | Incipient instability |

| **±20 to ±30** | Moderate stability |

| **>±30** | Good stability (electrostatic stabilization dominant) |

  

> **Exception:** Sterically stabilized systems (PEGylated liposomes, PEG-coated nanoparticles) can be stable near ζ = 0 mV because the PEG layer provides steric repulsion, not electrostatic.

  

### C) Factors Affecting Zeta Potential

  

| Factor | Effect |

|:---|:---|

| **pH** | Most critical; ζ shifts from positive (low pH) to negative (high pH) for most particles |

| **Ionic strength** | Higher salt → compressed double layer → lower |ζ| → less stable |

| **Surfactant type** | SLS (anionic) → negative ζ; CTAB (cationic) → positive ζ; Tween 80 → near-neutral |

| **Medium conductivity** | Too high conductivity → Joule heating → unreliable measurement |

  

### Interview Q: "Your nanoparticle has a zeta potential of -5 mV but is stable for 3 months. How?"

> This is likely a **sterically stabilized** system. If the nanoparticle has a PEG coating (e.g., DSPE-PEG 2000 on the surface of a liposome or LNP), the PEG chains create a hydrophilic brush layer that prevents particle-particle contact regardless of charge. The steric barrier keeps particles from aggregating even when electrostatic repulsion is minimal. In LNPs (Comirnaty, Spikevax), the PEG-lipid (ALC-0159 or DMG-PEG 2000) at 1–2 mol% provides this steric stabilization, and the ionizable lipid renders the particle near-neutral at pH 7.4 (ζ ≈ 0 to -5 mV). This is deliberate — a highly charged particle would be rapidly cleared by the immune system.

## 4.4 ELECTRON MICROSCOPY (SEM & TEM)

  

### SEM (Scanning Electron Microscopy)

  

| Aspect | Detail |

|:---|:---|

| **Principle** | Focused electron beam scans sample surface → secondary/backscattered electrons detected → surface topography image |

| **Resolution** | 1–10 nm |

| **Sample prep** | Sputter coating with gold/platinum (5–20 nm layer) to make non-conductive samples conductive |

| **Magnification** | 10× to 500,000× |

| **Applications** | API crystal morphology (needles, plates, prisms); tablet surface/cross-section; granule shape; coating uniformity; microsphere surface; nanofiber morphology |

| **Key measurement** | Particle shape classification; surface roughness; coating thickness (cross-section) |

  

### TEM (Transmission Electron Microscopy)

  

| Aspect | Detail |

|:---|:---|

| **Principle** | Electron beam transmitted THROUGH ultra-thin sample (<100nm thick) → internal structure visible |

| **Resolution** | 0.1–1 nm (atomic resolution possible) |

| **Sample prep** | Negative staining (uranyl acetate/phosphotungstic acid) for nanoparticles; cryo-TEM for hydrated samples |

| **Applications** | Liposome bilayer structure (unilamellar vs multilamellar); LNP internal architecture; nanocrystal size; polymeric nanoparticle core-shell structure |

| **Cryo-TEM** | Flash-frozen sample in vitreous ice → observe native hydrated structure without staining artifacts; **gold standard for LNP characterization** |

  

### Interview Q: "How do you characterize a liposome formulation? List all techniques."

  

| Property | Technique | Target |

|:---|:---|:---|

| Particle size | **DLS** (Z-average, PDI) | 80–150 nm; PDI <0.15 |

| Morphology/lamellarity | **Cryo-TEM** | Unilamellar vesicles (for Doxil-type) |

| Surface charge | **Zeta potential** | Negative for anionic; near-neutral for PEGylated |

| Encapsulation efficiency | **Ultracentrifugation + HPLC** or fluorescence assay | >85% for active loading; >30% for passive |

| Lipid composition | **HPLC with ELSD** or **TLC densitometry** | Matches theoretical |

| Drug content | **HPLC** (dissolve liposomes in methanol/Triton X-100) | Per specification |

| In vitro release | **Dialysis bag** or **Franz cell** | Release profile over 24–72h |

| Stability | **DLS** + **drug leakage** over time at 4°C | Stable size; <10% leakage at 30 days |

| Sterility | **Membrane filtration** (USP <71>) | Sterile |

| Endotoxin | **LAL assay** (USP <85>) | <5 EU/kg body weight |

## 4.5 LASER DIFFRACTION

  

### A) Principle

A laser beam passes through a dispersed sample → particles scatter light at angles inversely proportional to particle size → scatter pattern analyzed using **Mie theory** (or Fraunhofer approximation for large particles) → volume-weighted particle size distribution.

  

### B) Key Parameters

  

| Parameter | Definition | Significance |

|:---|:---|:---|

| **D10** | 10% of particles below this size | Fine particle fraction |

| **D50** | Median particle size (50th percentile) | Most commonly reported value |

| **D90** | 90% of particles below this size | Coarse fraction indicator |

| **Span** | (D90 - D10) / D50 | Distribution width; lower = narrower = better uniformity |

  

### C) Instruments

- **Malvern Mastersizer 3000** (most common in pharma)

- **Beckman Coulter LS 13 320**

- **Horiba LA-960**

  

### D) Industry Specifications (Examples)

  

| Material | D50 Target | Why |

|:---|:---|:---|

| **Micronized API** | 2–10 µm | Adequate dissolution; manageable flow |

| **Nanosuspension** | 200–500 nm | Enhanced dissolution (Noyes-Whitney) |

| **Inhalation powder (DPI)** | 1–5 µm (FPF: Fine Particle Fraction) | Reach deep lungs (alveolar deposition) |

| **Granules for compression** | 100–500 µm | Optimal flow into dies |

| **Pellets/multiparticulates** | 500–1500 µm | Suitable for capsule filling |

## 5A) USP DISSOLUTION APPARATUS

  

| Apparatus | USP # | Design | Speed | When to Use | Example |

|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|

| **Basket** | I | Wire mesh basket holding dosage form; rotates in vessel | 50–100 RPM | Capsules (prevents floating); coated tablets; beads/pellets | Hard gelatin capsules |

| **Paddle** | II | Paddle stirs medium; dosage form sinks (sinker may be needed) | 50–75 RPM | **Most common for tablets**; standard for IR and ER tablets | IR/ER tablets |

| **Reciprocating Cylinder** | III | Dosage form in cylindrical tubes; moves up/down through media | 5–30 dpm | pH-change studies; enteric-coated products; delayed-release | Enteric-coated tablets |

| **Flow-Through Cell** | IV | Medium flows continuously through cell containing dosage form | 4–16 mL/min | Poorly soluble drugs; implants; microspheres; low-dose products | PLGA microspheres; poorly soluble APIs |

| **Paddle-Over-Disk** | V | Modified Apparatus II with disk assembly holding transdermal patch | 25–50 RPM | Transdermal patches | Fentanyl patch |

| **Cylinder** | VI | Rotating cylinder holding transdermal patch | 25 RPM | Transdermal patches (alternative to V) | — |

| **Reciprocating Holder** | VII | Reciprocating holder; small volumes | — | Transdermal patches; osmotic systems; stents | Drug-eluting stents |

  

### Key Dissolution Medium Components

  

| Medium | Composition | pH | When Used |

|:---|:---|:---|:---|

| **0.1N HCl** | HCl in water | 1.2 | Simulated gastric fluid (SGF) without enzymes |

| **Acetate buffer** | Sodium acetate + acetic acid | 4.5 | Intermediate pH; BCS biowaiver |

| **Phosphate buffer** | KH₂PO₄ + NaOH | 6.8 | Simulated intestinal fluid (SIF); standard for many methods |

| **Water** | Purified water | ~5.5–7 | Simplest medium; used when pH is not critical |

| **With surfactant** | SLS (0.1–2%), Tween 80 (0.1–1%) added to above | — | Poorly soluble drugs — surfactant improves wetting and maintains sink |

| **FaSSIF** | Fed-state simulated intestinal fluid (bile salts, lecithin) | 6.5 | Biorelevant dissolution; IVIVC development |

| **FeSSGF** | Fed-state simulated gastric fluid | 5.0 | Biorelevant; food effect studies |

  

### Sink Conditions

> **Sink conditions** = volume of medium is at least **3× the saturation volume** (i.e., the drug can dissolve to at least 3× its total dose in the medium). This ensures dissolution rate is not limited by saturation. If sink conditions cannot be achieved (very poorly soluble drugs), add **surfactant** or use **large-volume media (1000 mL or more)**.

## 5B) DISSOLUTION METHOD DEVELOPMENT — Step by Step

  

```

STEP 1: GATHER DRUG INFORMATION

   → Solubility at pH 1.2, 4.5, 6.8 (from preformulation)

   → BCS class → determines regulatory approach

   → Dose and expected dissolution time

   → Innovator (RLD) dissolution data if available (FDA Dissolution Methods Database)

       ↓

STEP 2: SELECT APPARATUS

   → Tablets → Apparatus II (paddle), 50 RPM standard starting point

   → Capsules → Apparatus I (basket), 100 RPM

   → Modified release → Apparatus II or III

   → Special dosage forms → Apparatus IV (microspheres), V/VI/VII (patches)

       ↓

STEP 3: SELECT MEDIUM

   → Start with: 0.1N HCl (pH 1.2), phosphate buffer pH 4.5, phosphate buffer pH 6.8

   → Check: Does drug dissolve completely in 900 mL of each medium?

     If YES → use that medium without surfactant

     If NO → add SLS (start 0.5%) or Tween 80 (0.5%) to achieve sink

   → Volume: 900 mL standard; 500 mL for low-dose; 1000 mL for poorly soluble

       ↓

STEP 4: SELECT SAMPLING TIMEPOINTS

   → IR tablets: 5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 45, 60 min

   → ER tablets: 1, 2, 4, 6, 8, 12h (or longer for 24h products)

   → Sampling: Manual (syringe + filter) or Automated (autosampler with online UV)

       ↓

STEP 5: DEVELOP ANALYTICAL FINISH

   → UV-Vis: Simple, fast; use when no excipient interference at λmax

   → HPLC: When excipients absorb at same wavelength or multiple analytes

   → Filter validation: Verify that filter (0.22µm or 0.45µm PVDF/nylon) does not adsorb drug

     → Test: Filter drug solution → compare with unfiltered → recovery must be >98%

       ↓

STEP 6: ESTABLISH DISCRIMINATORY POWER

   → Purpose: Method must DETECT meaningful changes in formulation/process

   → Test with intentional variants:

     → Change disintegrant level (50%, 100%, 150% of target)

     → Change particle size of API (micronized vs unmicronized)

     → Change compression force (low, medium, high)

   → If method differentiates these variants → it is DISCRIMINATORY

   → If method gives same profile regardless → method is NOT discriminatory → re-optimize

       ↓

STEP 7: RUN PROFILE COMPARISON (f2) WITH RLD

   → Test innovator (RLD) and your generic under identical conditions

   → Calculate f1 and f2

   → Target: f2 ≥50 (similarity confirmed)

       ↓

STEP 8: VALIDATE THE METHOD

   → Specificity, linearity, accuracy, precision, filter validation, solution stability

   → Document in dissolution method validation protocol

```

## 5C) IVIVC (In Vitro-In Vivo Correlation)

  

### Definition

A **predictive mathematical model** describing the relationship between an **in vitro property** (dissolution rate) and a relevant **in vivo response** (plasma drug concentration/absorption).

  

### Levels of IVIVC

  

| Level | Correlation Type | Description | Regulatory Use |

|:---|:---|:---|:---|

| **Level A** | Point-to-point | Direct relationship between in vitro dissolution and in vivo absorption at each time point | **Most useful** — can waive bioequivalence studies for formulation changes; FDA preferred |

| **Level B** | Statistical moments | Mean in vitro dissolution time (MDT) vs mean in vivo residence time (MRT) | Limited regulatory utility — not point-to-point |

| **Level C** | Single point | One dissolution parameter (e.g., t50%) vs one PK parameter (Cmax or AUC) | Weakest; used for QC specification setting |

| **Multiple Level C** | Multiple single points | Multiple dissolution timepoints vs multiple PK parameters | Better than single Level C |

  

### When IVIVC Is Valuable

- **Modified-release products** — FDA/EMA expect IVIVC development for ER formulations

- **BCS Class II drugs** — dissolution is rate-limiting; in vitro dissolution predicts absorption

- **Formulation changes** — Level A IVIVC can justify biowaiver for post-approval changes

- **Dissolution specification setting** — scientifically justified spec based on IVIVC rather than arbitrary limits

  

### Interview Q: "What is a Level A IVIVC and why is it the gold standard?"

> A Level A IVIVC establishes a **point-to-point relationship** between in vitro dissolution and in vivo absorption fraction at corresponding time points. It uses **deconvolution** (Wagner-Nelson or Loo-Riegelman) to calculate fraction absorbed from plasma data, then correlates with fraction dissolved. The relationship is typically linear: Fraction Absorbed = a × Fraction Dissolved + b. Why gold standard: (1) **Predictive** — can predict the entire plasma profile from dissolution data alone. (2) **Regulatory leverage** — FDA allows biowaiver for formulation changes (SUPAC-MR guidance) if Level A IVIVC is established. (3) **Specification justification** — dissolution limits can be scientifically set based on clinically relevant absorption. For a fresher, knowing the concept and deconvolution methods is sufficient; execution requires PK data and software (WinNonlin/Phoenix).

## 5D) DISCRIMINATORY POWER

  

### What It Means

A dissolution method has **discriminatory power** if it can **detect differences** between formulations that are intentionally varied in a meaningful way (e.g., different polymer levels, different particle sizes, different compression forces).

  

### How to Demonstrate

  

| Variant | What You Change | Expected Result |

|:---|:---|:---|

| Disintegrant level (0%, target, 2× target) | CCS/SSG amount | Slower/faster disintegration → different profiles |

| API particle size (unmicronized vs micronized) | Surface area | Slower/faster dissolution |

| Polymer level in ER (low/medium/high HPMC) | Matrix strength | Faster/slower extended release |

| Compression force (low/high) | Tablet porosity | Different dissolution rates |

| Storage conditions (fresh vs 6-month aged) | Potential degradation/hardening | Should detect stability changes |

  

> **Key Rule:** A method that gives the same dissolution profile for ALL variants is **non-discriminatory** and therefore NOT suitable for routine QC. FDA reviewers specifically look for evidence of discriminatory power in ANDA submissions.

## 5E) f1 and f2 SIMILARITY FACTOR CALCULATIONS

  

### Formulas

  

**f1 (Difference Factor):**

```

f1 = {[Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ |Rᵢ - Tᵢ|] / [Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ Rᵢ]} × 100

```

  

**f2 (Similarity Factor):**

```

f2 = 50 × log {[1 + (1/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ (Rᵢ - Tᵢ)²]⁻⁰·⁵ × 100}

```

  

Where: Rᵢ = % dissolved of reference at time i; Tᵢ = % dissolved of test at time i; n = number of timepoints

  

### Acceptance Criteria

- **f1 ≤ 15** (difference factor — lower = more similar)

- **f2 ≥ 50** (similarity factor — higher = more similar; f2 = 100 means identical profiles)

  

### Rules for f2 Calculation (FDA/EMA)

1. Minimum **12 units** per product (6 reference + 6 test)

2. Use **mean % dissolved** at each timepoint

3. Only ONE timepoint should have **>85% dissolved** for both products (use only timepoints up to and including the first where both reach 85%)

4. RSD at earlier timepoints should be **≤20%**; at later timepoints **≤10%**

5. Minimum of **3 timepoints** (excluding zero)

  

### Worked Example

  

| Time (min) | Reference (R) | Test (T) | (R - T)² |

|:---|:---|:---|:---|

| 10 | 25 | 22 | 9 |

| 15 | 42 | 38 | 16 |

| 20 | 65 | 60 | 25 |

| 30 | 82 | 78 | 16 |

| 45 | 95 | 92 | 9 |

  

n = 5 (but only use up to 30 min since both >85% at 45 min → use n = 4)

  

Using n=4 (10, 15, 20, 30 min): Σ(R-T)² = 9+16+25+16 = 66

  

f2 = 50 × log {[1 + (1/4)(66)]⁻⁰·⁵ × 100}

f2 = 50 × log {[1 + 16.5]⁻⁰·⁵ × 100}

f2 = 50 × log {[17.5]⁻⁰·⁵ × 100}

f2 = 50 × log {0.239 × 100}

f2 = 50 × log(23.9)

f2 = 50 × 1.378 = **68.9** ✓ (≥50, profiles are similar)

## 5F) TOP 10 DISSOLUTION INTERVIEW QUESTIONS

  

**Q1: "Why is dissolution testing the most important QC test for oral solid dosage forms?"**

> Dissolution is the **rate-limiting step** for drug absorption from oral solids. A tablet that doesn't dissolve can't be absorbed → no therapeutic effect. Dissolution bridges in vitro and in vivo: BCS biowaiver for Class I/III drugs relies on rapid dissolution (≥85% in 30 min in pH 1.2, 4.5, 6.8). For ER products, the dissolution profile IS the product — it defines the release rate. Regulatory agencies use dissolution as the primary tool for batch release, stability testing, and generic equivalence (f2 comparison). No other single test gives this much information about product performance.

  

**Q2: "When would you add surfactant to dissolution medium?"**

> When the drug is **poorly soluble** (BCS Class II/IV) and sink conditions cannot be achieved in standard aqueous buffers. Start without surfactant → if <70% dissolves at the end of the test, add **SLS (0.5–2% w/v)** or **Tween 80 (0.1–1%)**. FDA Guidance: Use the **lowest effective concentration** of surfactant — demonstrate that it's necessary to achieve sink. Too much surfactant may mask formulation differences (reduce discriminatory power). Justification must be provided in the ANDA filing. Example: Ibuprofen dissolution in phosphate buffer pH 7.2 without surfactant may only reach 60% → add SLS 0.5% → achieves >90%.

  

**Q3: "What is the difference between Apparatus I and II?"**

> **Apparatus I (Basket):** Dosage form is placed INSIDE a rotating wire-mesh basket (40-mesh screen). Medium is static except for basket rotation. Use for: capsules (prevents floating and sticking to paddle shaft), coated tablets, beads/pellets. Speed: typically 100 RPM. **Apparatus II (Paddle):** Dosage form drops to bottom of vessel; paddle rotates above. Most widely used for tablets. Speed: typically 50 RPM (sometimes 75 RPM for ER). Key difference: In Apparatus I, the dosage form is confined → more controlled hydrodynamics. In Apparatus II, dosage form can move → can create "coning" effect (mound of undissolved particles under paddle center, especially at 50 RPM) → may need sinker for floating/sticking dosage forms.

  

**Q4: "Your dissolution result at the 30-min timepoint is 72% instead of Q ≥ 80%. What do you do?"**

> This is an **S1 stage failure** (USP dissolution acceptance criteria). Per USP <711>: **S1:** Test 6 units → each unit must be ≥Q+5% (i.e., ≥85% if Q=80%). If any unit fails → proceed to **S2:** Test additional 6 units (total 12) → average of 12 ≥Q; no unit <Q-15% (i.e., no unit <65%). If S2 fails → **S3:** Test additional 12 (total 24) → average ≥Q; no more than 2 units <Q-15%; no unit <Q-25%. If S3 fails → batch fails. At 72%, one unit failed S1 → don't panic, proceed to S2 stage testing. Simultaneously, initiate investigation: check medium preparation, apparatus calibration, temperature (37±0.5°C), RPM verification, medium deaeration, sample preparation.

  

**Q5: "What is deaeration and why is it critical?"**

> Dissolved gases (air) in dissolution media form **bubbles** on the dosage form surface or vessel walls → creates a barrier reducing wetting and dissolution → artificially LOW dissolution results. Deaeration methods: (1) Heat medium to 41°C → vacuum filter through 0.45µm membrane. (2) Helium sparging. (3) Sonication + vacuum. FDA and USP recommend deaeration for all dissolution testing. It is the **#1 cause of variable dissolution results** in QC labs. Mechanical calibration with USP Prednisone tablets (calibrators) also checks for deaeration adequacy.

  

**Q6: "Explain BCS-based biowaiver. When can dissolution replace a BE study?"**

> Per FDA/EMA guidance, **BCS Class I drugs** (high solubility, high permeability) with **rapid dissolution** (≥85% in 30 min in pH 1.2, 4.5, and 6.8 using USP App II at 50 RPM) in IR formulations can obtain a **biowaiver** — no in vivo BE study needed. Recently extended to **BCS Class III** (high solubility, low permeability) if BOTH test and reference dissolve very rapidly (≥85% in 15 min). Criteria: (1) Drug is BCS I or III; (2) IR dosage form; (3) Same excipients in similar amounts as RLD; (4) Rapid/very rapid dissolution demonstrated; (5) No narrow therapeutic index drug; (6) Not designed for absorption in oral cavity.

  

**Q7–Q10 (Concise):**

  

| # | Question | Key Answer Points |

|:--|:---|:---|

| 7 | "What is a sinker and when do you use one?" | Wire coil (USP-specified) holding dosage form at bottom of vessel; use when tablet floats or sticks to paddle shaft; must not restrict disintegration |

| 8 | "What temperature is used for dissolution and why?" | 37.0 ± 0.5°C — simulates body temperature; even 0.5°C deviation can affect dissolution by 5–10% for some drugs |

| 9 | "What is the difference between QC dissolution and biorelevant dissolution?" | QC: compendial conditions (simple buffers, sink conditions) for routine batch release. Biorelevant: FaSSIF/FeSSGF media, non-sink conditions, attempts to predict in vivo performance |

| 10 | "How do you set dissolution specifications for an ER product?" | Based on dissolution profile of BE batches; typically multi-point: 20–40% at T1h, 40–65% at T4h, NLT 80% at Tend; justify using IVIVC if available; FDA OGD provides guidance on dissolution spec ranges |

# PART 6 — METHOD VALIDATION (ICH Q2(R2))

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

## 6A) ALL VALIDATION PARAMETERS — Complete Reference

  

| Parameter | Definition | Assay | Impurities (Quant.) | Impurities (Limit) | ID Test | Dissolution |

|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|:---|

| **Specificity** | Ability to measure analyte in presence of interferences | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |

| **Linearity** | Proportional response over concentration range | ✅ | ✅ | ✗ | ✗ | ✅ |

| **Range** | Interval between upper and lower concentrations with suitable linearity/accuracy/precision | ✅ | ✅ | ✗ | ✗ | ✅ |

| **Accuracy** | Closeness to true value | ✅ | ✅ | ✗ | ✗ | ✅ |

| **Precision (Repeatability)** | Closeness of results under same conditions | ✅ | ✅ | ✗ | ✗ | ✅ |

| **Precision (Intermediate)** | Variation across days/analysts/instruments | ✅ | ✅ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |

| **LOD** | Lowest detectable amount | ✗ | ✗ | ✅ | ✗ | ✗ |

| **LOQ** | Lowest quantifiable amount with acceptable precision/accuracy | ✗ | ✅ | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ |

| **Robustness** | Measure of reliability under small deliberate variations | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✗ | ✅ |

  

### Detailed Acceptance Criteria

  

### 1. SPECIFICITY

  

| Test | Requirement |

|:---|:---|

| **Placebo interference** | No peak at retention time of API from placebo solution |

| **Forced degradation** | All degradant peaks resolved from API (Rs ≥1.5, ideally ≥2.0) |

| **Peak purity** | PDA purity angle < purity threshold for API peak in all stress samples |

| **Mass balance** | Assay + total impurities ≈ 100% (98–102%) across all stress conditions |

  

### 2. LINEARITY

  

| Test | Requirement |

|:---|:---|

| **Concentration range** | Minimum 5 concentration levels (e.g., 50%, 75%, 100%, 125%, 150% for assay) |

| **Correlation coefficient (r)** | **r ≥ 0.999** (preferred); r ≥ 0.998 acceptable for impurities |

| **r²** | ≥ 0.998 |

| **Y-intercept** | Should be statistically not significant (within ±2% of 100% response) |

| **Residual plot** | Random scatter (no systematic pattern = good) |

  

### 3. RANGE

  

| Method Type | Range Requirement |

|:---|:---|

| **Assay (drug substance)** | 80–120% of target concentration |

| **Assay (drug product)** | 80–120% (or as per specification, e.g., 90–110%) |

| **Content uniformity** | 70–130% of target concentration |

| **Dissolution** | ±20% over specified range (e.g., if spec is Q ≥80%, range is 0–120%) |

| **Impurities (quantitation)** | From LOQ to 120% of specification limit |

  

### 4. ACCURACY

  

| Test | Requirement |

|:---|:---|

| **Method** | Spike known amounts of pure API into placebo matrix (3 levels × 3 replicates = 9 determinations) |

| **Levels** | 80%, 100%, 120% of target (or 50%, 100%, 150% for wider range) |

| **Acceptance** | **% Recovery: 98.0–102.0%** at each level (for assay); **95.0–105.0%** for impurities |

| **Overall mean recovery** | 98.0–102.0% |

| **RSD across all 9 determinations** | ≤2.0% |

  

### 5. PRECISION

  

**a) Repeatability (Intra-day):**

  

| Test | Requirement |

|:---|:---|

| **Procedure** | 6 determinations at 100% concentration; OR 3 concentrations × 3 replicates = 9 determinations |

| **Acceptance** | **RSD ≤2.0%** for assay; **RSD ≤5.0%** for impurities (at specification level); **RSD ≤10%** for impurities near LOQ |

  

**b) Intermediate Precision (Inter-day/Ruggedness):**

  

| Test | Requirement |

|:---|:---|

| **Procedure** | Different days (min 2), different analysts (min 2), different instruments (if applicable) |

| **Acceptance** | **Overall RSD ≤3.0%** for assay; combined data from all conditions |

  

**c) Reproducibility:**

- Between different laboratories (for method transfer or pharmacopoeial studies)

- Not typically required for in-house validation

  

### 6. LOD and LOQ

  

| Parameter | Calculation Method | Acceptance |

|:---|:---|:---|

| **LOD** | LOD = 3.3 × σ / S | S/N ≥ 3; must demonstrate peak is detectable at this concentration |

| **LOQ** | LOQ = 10 × σ / S | S/N ≥ 10; must demonstrate acceptable precision (RSD ≤10%) and accuracy (±20%) at LOQ |

  

Where: σ = standard deviation of y-intercept of regression line (or SD of blank response); S = slope of calibration curve

  

### 7. ROBUSTNESS

  

| HPLC Parameter | Variation to Test |

|:---|:---|

| **Mobile phase pH** | ±0.2 units |

| **Mobile phase composition (%B)** | ±2% absolute (e.g., 60±2% ACN) |

| **Column temperature** | ±5°C |

| **Flow rate** | ±0.1 mL/min (or ±10%) |

| **Detection wavelength** | ±2 nm |

| **Column lot/brand** | Different lot or equivalent column from different manufacturer |

| **Acceptance** | SST criteria still met; results within specification; identify critical parameters |

## 6B) STEP-BY-STEP VALIDATION PROTOCOL

  

```

1. TITLE AND OBJECTIVE

   → "Validation Protocol for [Assay/Related Substances/Dissolution] Method

      for [Drug Name] [Dosage Form] by [RP-HPLC/UV/etc.]"

   → Objective: To demonstrate that the method is fit for intended purpose per ICH Q2(R2)

  

2. SCOPE

   → Which product(s), which test(s), which laboratory

  

3. RESPONSIBILITIES

   → Analyst: Execute validation experiments

   → Reviewer: Review data and calculations

   → QA: Approve protocol and report

  

4. MATERIALS AND EQUIPMENT

   → Reference standard (with CoA, assigned purity)

   → API, excipients, placebo

   → HPLC system (qualified), columns, reagents (HPLC grade)

   → Calibrated volumetric glassware, balances (±0.01 mg)

  

5. METHOD DESCRIPTION

   → Full chromatographic conditions (column, mobile phase, flow, temp, wavelength, injection volume, run time)

   → Sample preparation procedure

   → Standard preparation procedure

   → System suitability criteria

  

6. VALIDATION EXPERIMENTS (in execution order)

   6.1 Specificity (forced degradation + placebo interference + peak purity)

   6.2 Linearity (5+ levels, triplicate injections each)

   6.3 Range (defined from linearity)

   6.4 LOD and LOQ (if impurity method)

   6.5 Accuracy (3 levels × 3 replicates)

   6.6 Repeatability (6 at 100%)

   6.7 Intermediate Precision (Day 2, different analyst)

   6.8 Robustness (vary pH, %B, temp, flow, wavelength)

   6.9 Solution Stability (standard and sample at 0, 6, 12, 24h on bench/autosampler)

   6.10 Filter Validation (if applicable for dissolution method)

  

7. ACCEPTANCE CRITERIA

   → As per ICH Q2(R2) — detailed in section 6A above

  

8. DATA RECORDING

   → All raw data in controlled lab notebook

   → Electronic data with audit trail (Empower/OpenLab)

   → Chromatograms printed and attached

  

9. APPROVAL SIGNATURES

   → Protocol approved BEFORE execution begins

```

## 6C) HOW TO WRITE A VALIDATION REPORT

  

### Structure

  

```

1. COVER PAGE

   → Protocol number, report number, product name, method, date, signatures

  

2. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

   → "The [HPLC] method for [assay/related substances] of [Drug X Tablets, 500mg]

      was validated per ICH Q2(R2). ALL acceptance criteria were met. The method

      is considered validated for its intended purpose."

  

3. PROTOCOL REFERENCE

   → Reference approved validation protocol

  

4. DEVIATIONS (if any)

   → Document any deviations from protocol with justification

   → If no deviations: "No deviations from the approved protocol occurred."

  

5. RESULTS — Parameter by Parameter

   5.1 Specificity

       → Table: Forced degradation results (condition, %degradation, Rs, peak purity)

       → Conclusion: "Method is specific; all degradants resolved from API"

   5.2 Linearity

       → Table: Concentration vs Area; Graph: Calibration curve

       → r = 0.9998; equation: y = 24567x + 1234; y-intercept = 0.3% of 100% response

       → Conclusion: "Linear over 50–150% range"

   5.3 Accuracy

       → Table: Level, Amount added, Amount found, % Recovery for each replicate

       → Mean recovery = 99.8%; RSD = 0.6%

       → Conclusion: "Accuracy demonstrated at 80%, 100%, 120%"

   [Continue for each parameter]

  

6. OVERALL CONCLUSION

   → "The analytical method meets all ICH Q2(R2) validation criteria and is

      suitable for [routine QC testing / stability studies / regulatory submission]."

  

7. ATTACHMENTS

   → Representative chromatograms

   → Raw data summaries

   → Instrument qualification records

   → Column lot certificate

```

## 6D) COMMON AUDIT FINDINGS RELATED TO METHOD VALIDATION

  

| Finding | Regulatory Citation | How to Avoid |

|:---|:---|:---|

| **Validation not performed before use** | 21 CFR 211.194(a)(2) | Complete validation before ANY sample analysis |

| **Insufficient forced degradation** | ICH Q2(R2) specificity | Achieve 5–20% degradation; all 5 stress conditions; document mass balance |

| **No intermediate precision** | ICH Q2(R2) | Include Day 2 + different analyst at minimum |

| **Linearity with insufficient points** | ICH Q2(R2) requires ≥5 levels | Use 5–7 concentration levels, not 3 |

| **No robustness study** | ICH Q2(R2) | Test ≥5 chromatographic variables |

| **Validation for assay used for impurities** | — | Validate separately for impurity method (different range, LOD/LOQ required) |

| **No filter validation for dissolution** | USP <1092> | Test each filter type with drug solution; ≥98% recovery |

| **Solution stability not demonstrated** | — | Prove standards and samples are stable for the duration of the analytical run (typically 24h) |

| **Audit trail not reviewed** | 21 CFR Part 11; EU Annex 11 | Review audit trail for all electronic data; sign-off by second person |

| **Reprocessing without justification** | Data integrity | Any integration changes must be justified, documented, and original data preserved |

| **Partial validation missing for method transfer** | ICH Q2(R2) / Q14 | At minimum: specificity, accuracy, precision must be repeated at receiving site |

## 7.3 HOW TO DESCRIBE ANALYTICAL SKILLS IN INTERVIEWS

  

### Framework: **Technique → Purpose → Result → Impact**

  

**Example 1 (HPLC):**

> "I developed and validated a **reverse-phase HPLC method** for simultaneous estimation of my API and two known impurities using a C18 column (250×4.6mm, 5µm) with ACN:phosphate buffer (60:40 v/v) at 254nm. The method was **stability-indicating** — I demonstrated specificity through forced degradation under five ICH stress conditions achieving 5–15% degradation with all degradants resolved (Rs >2.5). Linearity was confirmed from 10–150 µg/mL (r² = 0.9999). I validated per **ICH Q2(R2)** — accuracy 99.2–100.8%, precision RSD 0.6%. This method was used for all **stability and dissolution** analysis in my project."

  

**Example 2 (Dissolution):**

> "I developed a discriminatory dissolution method for my sustained-release tablets using **USP Apparatus II** at 50 RPM in phosphate buffer pH 6.8 (900 mL). I demonstrated discriminatory power by testing tablets with varying HPMC levels (20%, 30%, 40%) — the method clearly differentiated the three formulations with f2 values of 32, 51, and 78 respectively when compared pairwise. The optimized formulation achieved **f2 = 62 with the innovator product**, confirming dissolution similarity."

  

**Example 3 (DSC/Preformulation):**

> "I performed comprehensive preformulation characterization using **DSC** (melting point, polymorphism screening), **FTIR** (functional group identification and excipient compatibility), and **XRPD** (crystallinity confirmation). DSC revealed my API had a sharp melting endotherm at 172°C with ΔH = 85 J/g, confirming crystalline Form I. Excipient compatibility by DSC showed a **significant interaction with lactose** (Maillard reaction — disappearance of API melting peak at 40°C/75% RH, 4 weeks), leading me to replace lactose with MCC in my formulation."

## QUICK REFERENCE: TECHNIQUE SELECTION DECISION TREE

  

```

WHAT DO YOU NEED TO KNOW?

│

├── WHAT is it? (Identity)

│   ├── Functional groups → FTIR

│   ├── Molecular structure → NMR, MS

│   ├── Crystalline form → XRPD, DSC

│   └── Match to reference → HPLC Rt, FTIR overlay, UV λmax

│

├── HOW MUCH is there? (Quantitation)

│   ├── Drug content (assay) → HPLC (primary), UV-Vis (simple)

│   ├── Impurities → HPLC (gradient), LC-MS/MS (for identification)

│   ├── Trace impurities (ppm/ppb) → LC-MS/MS (MRM), GC-MS

│   ├── Moisture → Karl Fischer titration

│   ├── Residual solvents → Headspace GC-FID

│   └── Elemental impurities → ICP-MS (ICH Q3D)

│

├── HOW DOES IT DISSOLVE? (Performance)

│   ├── Dissolution rate → USP dissolution (App I–VII)

│   ├── Intrinsic dissolution rate → IDR apparatus

│   └── Solubility → Shake-flask (thermodynamic), DMSO (kinetic)

│

├── WHAT DOES IT LOOK LIKE? (Physical)

│   ├── Particle size (µm) → Laser diffraction (Malvern)

│   ├── Particle size (nm) → DLS (Zetasizer)

│   ├── Shape/morphology → SEM

│   ├── Internal nanostructure → TEM, Cryo-TEM

│   ├── Surface charge → Zeta potential

│   └── Crystal habit → SEM, PLM

│

└── IS IT STABLE? (Stability)

    ├── Thermal behavior → DSC, TGA

    ├── Crystallinity on aging → XRPD

    ├── Moisture uptake → DVS

    ├── Chemical degradation → HPLC (stability-indicating method)

    └── Photostability → ICH Q1B light chamber + HPLC

```
