## 1.1 ICH Q1 SERIES — STABILITY TESTING

  

### Q1A(R2): Stability Testing of New Drug Substances and Products

  

| Aspect               | Detail                                                                                                                    |
| :------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **What it covers**   | Conditions, duration, and testing frequency for stability studies of new chemical entities (NCEs) and their drug products |
| **Key requirements** | Store under defined conditions; test at defined intervals; use validated stability-indicating methods                     |
| **2025 Update**      | New draft ICH Q1 aims to **consolidate Q1A–Q1F + Q5C** into one unified, science-based, lifecycle approach                |

  

**Storage Conditions by Climatic Zone:**

  

| Study Type       | Condition                | Duration                          | Applicable Zone                             |
| :--------------- | :----------------------- | :-------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------ |
| **Long-term**    | 25°C ± 2°C / 60% RH ± 5% | 12 months minimum (36 for filing) | Zone I, II (USA, EU)                        |
| **Long-term**    | 30°C ± 2°C / 65% RH ± 5% | 12 months minimum                 | Zone III                                    |
| **Long-term**    | 30°C ± 2°C / 75% RH ± 5% | 12 months minimum                 | **Zone IVa (India, Brazil)**                |
| **Intermediate** | 30°C ± 2°C / 65% RH ± 5% | 6 months                          | Zone I, II (triggered if accelerated fails) |
| **Accelerated**  | 40°C ± 2°C / 75% RH ± 5% | 6 months                          | All zones                                   |
| **Refrigerated** | 5°C ± 3°C                | 12 months                         | Refrigerated products                       |
| **Frozen**       | -20°C ± 5°C              | 12 months                         | Frozen products                             |

  

**Testing Frequency:**

- **Long-term:** 0, 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36 months (every 3 months in Year 1; every 6 months in Year 2; annually thereafter)

- **Accelerated:** 0, 1, 2, 3, 6 months (minimum 3 timepoints)

- **Intermediate:** 0, 3, 6, 9, 12 months

  

**What to Test:**

  

| Drug Substance                            | Drug Product                     |
| :---------------------------------------- | :------------------------------- |
| Appearance                                | Appearance                       |
| Assay                                     | Assay                            |
| Related substances / degradation products | Degradation products             |
| Moisture content                          | Dissolution / drug release       |
| Polymorphic form (if applicable)          | Moisture / water activity        |
| Particle size (if critical)               | pH (liquid formulations)         |
| Chiral purity (if applicable)             | Microbial limits                 |
| —                                         | Hardness, friability (tablets)   |
| —                                         | Content uniformity (suspensions) |
  

**Interview Q: "A significant change occurs at the 3-month accelerated station. What do you do?"**

> **Model Answer:** Per ICH Q1A(R2), a "significant change" at accelerated conditions means: ≥5% change in assay from initial; any degradant exceeds specification; failure to meet appearance, physical properties, or functionality test (e.g., dissolution); pH outside limits. **Action:** (1) Cannot use the 6-month accelerated data for shelf-life extrapolation. (2) Must conduct **intermediate stability study** (30°C/65%RH for 12 months). (3) Shelf-life based on long-term data with statistical analysis (regression, Arrhenius if applicable). (4) May propose a **tentative** shelf-life of up to 24 months based on 12 months of long-term data. (5) Commit to continuing long-term studies post-approval and reporting any significant changes as per annual report requirements.

  

### Q1B: Photostability Testing

  

| Aspect                   | Detail                                                                               |
| :----------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **What it covers**       | Exposure of drug substance and drug product to light                                 |
| **Key requirement**      | Expose to ≥1.2 million lux hours of visible light AND ≥200 Watt hours/m² of UV light |
| **Light source options** | Option 1: D65 (daylight) lamp; Option 2: Cool white fluorescent + near-UV lamp       |
| **What to test**         | Appearance, assay, degradation products; use dark control for comparison             |
  
  
  **Formulation R&D Impact:** If drug is photolabile → amber glass containers, aluminum overwrap, opaque blister packs, photoprotective coating. Example: Nifedipine (extremely photolabile) → always packaged in amber blisters.

  

### Q1C–Q1F Quick Reference

  

| Guideline | Topic                                     | Key Point for R&D                                                                                  |
| :-------- | :---------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Q1C**   | New dosage forms of registered substances | Reduced stability program for line extensions (e.g., new strength, new container)                  |
| **Q1D**   | Bracketing and matrixing                  | Statistical designs to reduce stability testing workload for multiple strengths/pack sizes         |
| **Q1E**   | Evaluation of stability data              | How to determine shelf-life from stability data; statistical methods; extrapolation rules          |
| **Q1F**   | Stability in zones III and IV             | **Critical for India (Zone IVa: 30°C/75% RH)** — defines long-term conditions for tropical markets |
  

**Interview Q: "Explain bracketing and matrixing in stability testing."**

> **Bracketing** tests only the extremes of design factors (e.g., smallest and largest container size; lowest and highest strength), assuming intermediate levels are covered. **Matrixing** is a fractional factorial design where only a subset of all possible combinations is tested at each timepoint, with the full design completed over time. Both reduce the total number of samples and tests. **Key rule:** Only applicable when strengths have the same qualitative formulation (same excipients, different amounts proportionally). You CANNOT bracket formulations with different excipient compositions.

## 1.2 ICH Q2(R2) — ANALYTICAL VALIDATION

  

| Aspect             | Detail                                                                               |
| :----------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **What it covers** | Validation of analytical procedures for pharmaceutical use                           |
| **Effective**      | Step 4 reached November 2023; legally effective in EU from June 2024; adopted by FDA |
| **Replaces**       | Q2(R1) — the previous standard                                                       |
| **Works with**     | **ICH Q14** (Analytical Procedure Development) — companion guideline                 |
  

**Key Change from Q2(R1) to Q2(R2):**

- Lifecycle approach to analytical procedures (not just one-time validation)

- Includes guidance for **multivariate** and **spectroscopic** methods (NIR, Raman)

- Greater flexibility in statistical approaches

- Emphasis on **Analytical Target Profile (ATP)** concept (from Q14)

  

**Validation Parameters (covered in detail in analytical_techniques_part2.md):**

  

| Parameter                 | Assay | Impurities (Quant.) | Impurities (Limit) | ID  |
| :------------------------ | :---- | :------------------ | :----------------- | :-- |
| Specificity               | ✅     | ✅                   | ✅                  | ✅   |
| Linearity                 | ✅     | ✅                   | ✗                  | ✗   |
| Range                     | ✅     | ✅                   | ✗                  | ✗   |
| Accuracy                  | ✅     | ✅                   | ✗                  | ✗   |
| Precision (Repeatability) | ✅     | ✅                   | ✗                  | ✗   |
| Precision (Intermediate)  | ✅     | ✅                   | ✗                  | ✗   |
| LOD                       | ✗     | ✗                   | ✅                  | ✗   |
| LOQ                       | ✗     | ✅                   | ✗                  | ✗   |
| Robustness                | ✅     | ✅                   | ✅                  | ✗   |

  

**Interview Q: "What is the Analytical Target Profile (ATP) from ICH Q14?"**

> The ATP is a **prospective statement** defining what the analytical procedure should measure, the reportable result, and the performance criteria the procedure must meet. It is analogous to the QTPP for formulation. Example ATP: "The procedure shall quantify [Drug X] in tablet formulation with accuracy of 98–102% recovery, precision RSD ≤2.0%, over the range of 80–120% of label claim, in the presence of known degradation products." The ATP guides method development and defines what "validated" means for that specific procedure. It enables lifecycle management — if you change the method (e.g., HPLC to UPLC), you revalidate against the same ATP rather than starting from scratch.

| :----------------- | :------------------ | :------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------- |

| Maximum Daily Dose | Reporting Threshold | Identification Threshold | Qualification Threshold |
| :----------------- | :------------------ | :----------------------- | :---------------------- |

| 1–10 mg            | 1.0% or 50 µg TDI   | —                        | —                       |

### Q3C(R8): Residual Solvents

  

| Class       | Toxicity                                                                  | Example Solvents                                                                                                 | Limit                                  |
| :---------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------ | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------- |
| **Class 1** | Known human carcinogens, strongly suspected                               | Benzene (2 ppm), Carbon tetrachloride (4 ppm), 1,2-Dichloroethane (5 ppm)                                        | **Avoid — use only if no alternative** |
| **Class 2** | Non-genotoxic animal carcinogens or known causes of irreversible toxicity | Methanol (3000 ppm), DCM (600 ppm), Acetonitrile (410 ppm), Toluene (890 ppm), DMF (880 ppm), Pyridine (200 ppm) | **Limit to specified PDE**             |
| **Class 3** | Low toxic potential                                                       | Ethanol (5000 ppm), Acetone (5000 ppm), Ethyl acetate (5000 ppm), DMSO (5000 ppm), Heptane (5000 ppm)            | **≤5000 ppm total or ≤0.5%**           |

  

### Q3D(R2): Elemental Impurities

  

| Class        | Elements                               | Control Strategy                                                     |
| :----------- | :------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Class 1**  | As, Cd, Hg, Pb                         | **Always evaluate** — high toxicity; require testing of drug product |
| **Class 2A** | Co, Ni, V                              | High probability of occurrence; **evaluate across all routes**       |
| **Class 2B** | Ag, Au, Ir, Os, Pd, Pt, Rh, Ru, Se, Tl | Lower probability; evaluate based on risk assessment                 |
| **Class 3**  | Ba, Cr, Cu, Li, Mo, Sb, Sn             | Low oral toxicity; high PDEs; risk assessment may be sufficient      |

  

**Method:** ICP-MS (preferred) or ICP-OES per USP <232>/<233>

  

### ICH M7(R2): Mutagenic Impurities

  

| Aspect                                       | Detail                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| :------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Scope**                                    | Assessment and control of DNA-reactive (mutagenic) impurities to limit cancer risk                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| **TTC (Threshold of Toxicological Concern)** | **1.5 µg/day** for lifetime exposure (≥10 year treatment)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |
| **Higher limits for shorter exposure**       | ≤1 month: 120 µg/day; ≤1 year: 20 µg/day; 1–10 years: 10 µg/day                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| **Classification**                           | Class 1 (known mutagenic carcinogen) → control to compound-specific limit; Class 2 (known mutagen, unknown carcinogenicity) → control to TTC; Class 3 (alerting structure, no mutagenicity data) → test or control; Class 4 (alerting structure, not mutagenic) → treat as normal impurity; Class 5 (no alerting structure) → treat as normal impurity |

  

**Interview Q: "What is TTC and how does it relate to nitrosamine impurities?"**

> TTC = Threshold of Toxicological Concern = 1.5 µg/day. Below this level, the cancer risk is considered acceptable (<1 in 100,000 over a lifetime). However, after the **2018 nitrosamine crisis** (NDMA found in valsartan, ranitidine), FDA/EMA established **compound-specific Acceptable Intake (AI)** limits for specific nitrosamines that are LOWER than TTC: NDMA = 96 ng/day; NDEA = 26.5 ng/day; NMBA = 96 ng/day. Testing requires ultra-sensitive LC-MS/MS methods at sub-ppb levels. This crisis led to global recalls, manufacturing process changes, and new regulatory expectations for nitrosamine risk assessment for ALL marketed products (not just sartans).

## 1.5 ICH Q8(R2) — PHARMACEUTICAL DEVELOPMENT

  

> [!IMPORTANT]

> **Q8 is the MOST important ICH guideline for formulation R&D scientists.** It defines QbD (Quality by Design) and the entire pharmaceutical development framework. Know this inside-out.

  

| Concept                                   | Definition                                                                                                                     | Example                                                                                    |
| :---------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **QTPP** (Quality Target Product Profile) | Prospective summary of quality characteristics a drug product should achieve to ensure desired efficacy and safety             | "IR tablet; 500mg strength; dissolution ≥85% in 30 min; 24-month shelf-life at 30°C/75%RH" |
| **CQA** (Critical Quality Attribute)      | Physical, chemical, biological, or microbiological property that must be within an appropriate limit to ensure desired quality | Dissolution rate, assay, content uniformity, particle size, degradation products           |
| **CPP** (Critical Process Parameter)      | A process parameter whose variability impacts a CQA                                                                            | Granulation moisture content, compression force, coating spray rate                        |
| **Design Space**                          | Multidimensional combination of input variables and process parameters demonstrated to provide quality assurance               | Granulation: moisture 2–4%, impeller speed 200–400 RPM, endpoint: LOD 1.5–2.5%             |
| **Control Strategy**                      | Planned set of controls to ensure process performance and product quality                                                      | In-process checks + final testing + specification limits                                   |
| **PAT** (Process Analytical Technology)   | Systems for real-time analysis during manufacturing                                                                            | NIR for blend uniformity; Raman for polymorph monitoring                                   |

  
**QbD Workflow (from Q8):**

  

```

QTPP → Identify CQAs → Risk Assessment (FMEA) →

DoE (Screening → Optimization) → Define Design Space →

Develop Control Strategy → File in CTD Module 3.2.P.2

```

  

**Interview Q: "What is the difference between traditional development and QbD?"**

  

| Aspect                    | Traditional                        | QbD (ICH Q8)                                                       |
| :------------------------ | :--------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Development approach**  | One-factor-at-a-time (OFAT)        | Systematic DoE; multivariate                                       |
| **Process understanding** | Limited; empirical                 | Comprehensive; mechanistic                                         |
| **Specifications**        | Based on batch data                | Based on product performance + clinical relevance                  |
| **Manufacturing**         | Fixed process; test-and-release    | Flexible within design space; continuous verification              |
| **Quality**               | Tested into product                | Built into product by design                                       |
| **Post-approval changes** | Require prior approval supplements | Changes within design space → no prior approval needed             |
| **Regulatory advantage**  | None                               | Potential for real-time release testing; reduced regulatory burden |

## 1.7 ICH Q10 — PHARMACEUTICAL QUALITY SYSTEM

  

| Aspect               | Detail                                                                                                                                    |
| :------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **What it covers**   | Model for an effective pharmaceutical quality system throughout the product lifecycle                                                     |
| **Key elements**     | Process performance & product quality monitoring; CAPA; Change management; Management review                                              |
| **Lifecycle stages** | Stage 1: Pharmaceutical Development → Stage 2: Technology Transfer → Stage 3: Commercial Manufacturing → Stage 4: Product Discontinuation |
| **Enablers**         | Knowledge management; Quality risk management (Q9)                                                                                        |

  

**Interview Q: "How does Q10 apply during R&D (Stage 1)?"**

> Even during development, Q10 requires: (1) **Knowledge management** — document ALL development knowledge (formulation rationale, rejected excipients with reasons, failed experiments). (2) **GMP-appropriate practices** — development batch records should be traceable even if not full GMP. (3) **Technology transfer readiness** — develop methods and processes with transfer in mind. (4) **CAPA for development issues** — if a development batch fails, investigate root cause just like in production. This ensures seamless transition from development to commercial manufacturing.

## 2.2 SCHEDULE M — GMP REQUIREMENTS

  

> **Schedule M of the Drugs and Cosmetics Rules, 1945** defines GMP requirements for pharmaceutical manufacturing in India. **Revised Schedule M** (2024 notification) significantly tightened requirements to align with WHO GMP.

  

### Key Schedule M Requirements

  

| Area                  | Requirement                                                                                                              |
| :-------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Premises**          | Adequate space; proper design to prevent cross-contamination; smooth walls/floors; separate areas for different products |
| **Equipment**         | Qualified (IQ/OQ/PQ); calibrated; made of non-reactive materials; cleaning validated                                     |
| **Personnel**         | Qualified personnel; training records maintained; hygiene program                                                        |
| **Documentation**     | BMRs/BPRs; SOPs; specifications; validation protocols; training records                                                  |
| **Production**        | Written procedures; in-process controls; line clearance; yield reconciliation                                            |
| **Quality Control**   | Independent QC department; adequate lab facilities; reference standards; retention samples                               |
| **Quality Assurance** | QA oversight; self-inspections; product quality review; stability monitoring                                             |
| **Water system**      | Purified water (IP grade) for manufacturing; WFI for injectables                                                         |
| **HVAC**              | Classified areas for sterile products; temperature/humidity control; pressure differentials                              |
| **Waste management**  | Proper disposal of reject materials, chemicals, and expired products                                                     |

  

### Schedule M vs WHO GMP vs EU GMP

  

| Aspect                   | Schedule M (India)                           | WHO GMP                                                | EU GMP                                             |
| :----------------------- | :------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------- |
| **Legal status**         | Legally binding in India                     | International standard; basis for WHO prequalification | Legally binding in EU/EEA                          |
| **Scope**                | All drug manufacturers in India              | WHO prequalification applicants                        | All MA holders manufacturing in EU                 |
| **Stringency**           | Medium (revised 2024 brought closer to WHO)  | Medium-High                                            | Highest (Annex 1 for sterile is the gold standard) |
| **Inspection**           | State inspectors + CDSCO (for export/WHO PQ) | WHO Pre-Qualification Team (PQT)                       | National competent authorities (e.g., BfArM, ANSM) |
| **Data integrity focus** | Increasing but less rigorous than FDA/EU     | Moderate                                               | Strong (EU Annex 11 for computerized systems)      |

## 2.3 NEW DRUGS AND CLINICAL TRIALS RULES, 2019

  

| Aspect                   | Detail                                                                                            |
| :----------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Replaced**             | Schedule Y of D&C Rules (the old clinical trial framework)                                        |
| **Key provision**        | Defined timelines for CDSCO review (90 working days → recently reduced to 45)                     |
| **Ethics committees**    | Mandatory registration; informed consent requirements strengthened                                |
| **Compensation**         | Clear framework for trial participant injury/death compensation                                   |
| **2026 Amendment**       | Prior-intimation mechanism replaces test license for non-high-risk R&D drugs                      |
| **Academic experiments** | Academic institutions conducting research may use intimation route for non-commercial manufacture |
| **Global alignment**     | Brought India closer to ICH GCP (E6) standards                                                    |

## 2.4 PHARMACOPOEIA OF INDIA (IP)

  

| Aspect              | Detail                                                                             |
| :------------------ | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Current edition** | IP 2022 (with supplements)                                                         |
| **Published by**    | Indian Pharmacopoeia Commission (IPC), Ghaziabad                                   |
| **Legal status**    | Official standard for drugs manufactured/marketed in India (D&C Act, Section 3(b)) |
| **Equivalents**     | USP (USA), PhEur/EP (EU), BP (UK), JP (Japan)                                      |

  

**Key Differences IP vs USP:**

  

| Aspect                     | IP                                  | USP                    |
| :------------------------- | :---------------------------------- | :--------------------- |
| **Dissolution apparatus**  | Same USP <711> referenced           | USP <711> original     |
| **Stability conditions**   | Zone IVa (30°C/75%RH for long-term) | Zone II (25°C/60%RH)   |
| **Microbial limits**       | IP methods (aligned with PhEur)     | USP <61>/<62>          |
| **Naming convention**      | Uses BAN/INN names                  | Uses USAN names        |
| **Tablet test references** | References IP general chapters      | USP <701>, <905>, etc. |
  

**Interview Q: "If IP and USP have different specifications for the same drug, which do you follow?"**

> In India, IP is the legally binding standard for products marketed domestically. For products exported to the US, USP is binding. If a company manufactures for both markets, the product must meet BOTH specifications. In practice, companies often develop internal specifications that are tighter than both IP and USP to ensure compliance in all markets simultaneously.

## 3.1 APPLICATION TYPES — IND, NDA, ANDA, 505(b)(2)

  

| Application   | Full Name                | Purpose                                                     | Sponsor                                      | Key Requirements                                                                                                |
| :------------ | :----------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **IND**       | Investigational New Drug | Permission to begin human clinical trials in US             | Innovator                                    | Pre-clinical data; chemistry data; clinical protocol; investigator brochure                                     |
| **NDA**       | New Drug Application     | Marketing approval for NEW drug                             | Innovator                                    | Full safety + efficacy data; complete CMC; clinical trials (Phase I–III)                                        |
| **ANDA**      | Abbreviated NDA          | Marketing approval for GENERIC drug                         | Generic company (Sun, DRL, Cipla, Aurobindo) | **No clinical trials needed** (except BE study); demonstrate pharmaceutical equivalence + bioequivalence to RLD |
| **505(b)(2)** | —                        | Approval relying on FDA's prior findings of safety/efficacy | Any company                                  | Partial new data + reference to existing FDA findings; used for reformulations, new routes, fixed-dose combos   |

  

### ANDA (Generic Drug) Pathway — What Indian Companies Do

  

```

STEP 1: Select Reference Listed Drug (RLD) from Orange Book

   → Identify RLD product, strength, route, dosage form

   → Check patent status and exclusivity in Orange Book

       ↓

STEP 2: Develop Pharmaceutical Equivalent

   → Same API, same strength, same dosage form, same route

   → Same or comparable excipients (Q1/Q2 sameness for some products)

   → Develop stability-indicating analytical methods

       ↓

STEP 3: Conduct Bioequivalence Study

   → Design: Randomized, 2-period, 2-sequence, crossover

   → Fasted and/or fed conditions

   → 90% CI for Cmax and AUC₀₋t must fall within 80.00–125.00%

   → BCS-based biowaiver possible for Class I/III drugs

       ↓

STEP 4: File ANDA

   → CTD format (Module 1–5, but Module 4/5 = BE study only)

   → Include: Pharmaceutical development (3.2.P.2), specifications,

     method validation, stability data (≥3 exhibit batches),

     dissolution profiles (f2 comparison with RLD)

       ↓

STEP 5: Paragraph IV Certification (if patents exist)

   → Certify that RLD patents are invalid or not infringed

   → May trigger 30-month stay (patent litigation by innovator)

       ↓

STEP 6: FDA Review

   → OGD (Office of Generic Drugs) reviews ANDA

   → Pre-approval inspection (PAI) of manufacturing facility

   → Deficiency letters → Company responds

       ↓

STEP 7: Approval → Launch

   → AB-rated in Orange Book → automatic substitution at pharmacy

```

| Aspect             | Detail                                                                                   |

**Interview Q: "What is a Paragraph IV certification and why is it important?"**

> When filing an ANDA, you must certify regarding each patent listed in the Orange Book. **Para I:** No patent listed. **Para II:** Patent has expired. **Para III:** Will not market until patent expires (agree to wait). **Para IV:** Patent is invalid or will not be infringed by the generic. A Para IV certification is the aggressive route — it triggers a notification to the patent holder, who then has 45 days to file a patent infringement lawsuit. If they sue, FDA imposes a 30-month stay (ANDA not approved during this period while litigation proceeds). The FIRST generic to file a Para IV gets **180-day exclusivity** — a massive commercial advantage worth millions. This is why Indian generics companies like DRL, Sun Pharma, and Aurobindo invest heavily in patent challenges.
