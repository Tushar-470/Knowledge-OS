## 1.1 HIGH-PERFORMANCE LIQUID CHROMATOGRAPHY (HPLC)

  

### A) Working Principle

  

**HPLC** separates compounds in a mixture based on their differential interactions between a **mobile phase** (liquid solvent) and a **stationary phase** (column packing).

  

**Separation Mechanism by Mode:**

  

| Mode                         | Stationary Phase                | Mobile Phase                          | Separation Based On           | When To Use                                     |
| :--------------------------- | :------------------------------ | :------------------------------------ | :---------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------- |
| **Reverse Phase (RP-HPLC)**  | Non-polar (C18, C8)             | Polar (water + ACN or MeOH)           | Hydrophobicity (Log P)        | **~80% of all pharma methods**; most drugs      |
| **Normal Phase (NP-HPLC)**   | Polar (silica, amino, cyano)    | Non-polar (hexane + ethyl acetate)    | Polarity                      | Chiral separations; lipids; geometrical isomers |
| **Ion Exchange (IEC)**       | Charged groups (SCX, SAX)       | Buffer with varying ionic strength/pH | Charge                        | Proteins, amino acids, inorganic ions           |
| **Size Exclusion (SEC/GPC)** | Porous beads (dextran, agarose) | Aqueous or organic                    | Molecular size                | Protein aggregates; polymer MW distribution     |
| **HILIC**                    | Polar (silica, amide, diol)     | Organic-rich + aqueous                | Polar retention (reversed NP) | Very polar compounds poorly retained on C18     |
  

**Key Equations:**

  

| Parameter                    | Formula                             | What It Tells You                             |
| :--------------------------- | :---------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------- |
| **Retention Factor (k)**     | k = (tR - t₀) / t₀                  | How much the analyte is retained; ideal: 2–10 |
| **Resolution (Rs)**          | Rs = 2(tR2 - tR1) / (w1 + w2)       | Separation quality; target Rs ≥2.0            |
| **Theoretical Plates (N)**   | N = 16(tR/w)² or N = 5.54(tR/w₀.₅)² | Column efficiency; higher = better separation |
| **Height Equivalent (HETP)** | H = L/N                             | Lower H = better column performance           |
| **Tailing Factor (Tf)**      | Tf = W₀.₀₅ / 2f                     | Peak symmetry; ideal: 0.8–1.5 (USP: NMT 2.0)  |
| **Capacity Factor (α)**      | α = k₂/k₁                           | Selectivity between two peaks; α >1.05 needed |

  

### B) Instrumentation — Component-by-Component

  

```

Solvent Reservoir(s) → Degasser → Pump → Injector/Autosampler → Column → Detector → Data System

         ↑                                                                              ↓

    Mobile Phase                                                                   Chromatogram

    (A + B for gradient)                                                          (Empower/OpenLab)

```

  

| Component             | Function                                   | Key Specifications                                              | Interview Detail                                                                        |
| :-------------------- | :----------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Solvent Reservoir** | Holds mobile phase                         | Glass bottles with inlet filters (2µm sinker)                   | Degas to remove dissolved O₂ (causes baseline noise)                                    |
| **Degasser**          | Removes dissolved gases                    | Inline vacuum or helium sparging                                | Helium sparging is gold standard but expensive                                          |
| **Pump**              | Delivers mobile phase at constant flow     | Binary or quaternary; 0.1–10 mL/min; precision ±0.1%            | **Isocratic** (constant composition) vs **Gradient** (changing %B over time)            |
| **Autosampler**       | Injects precise sample volume              | 1–100 µL typical; temperature-controlled (4–40°C)               | Carryover is a critical concern (needle wash program)                                   |
| **Column**            | Separation occurs here                     | C18, 250×4.6mm, 5µm (conventional); 150×4.6mm, 3µm (faster)     | Column temperature: 25–40°C; consistent temp crucial for retention time reproducibility |
| **Column Oven**       | Controls column temperature                | 20–80°C typically set at 25, 30, or 40°C                        | ±1°C variation can shift retention times by 5–10%                                       |
| **Detector (UV-PDA)** | Measures absorbance at set wavelength      | UV (fixed λ), PDA (190–800nm full spectrum); linearity to ~2 AU | **PDA advantage:** Peak purity assessment; can identify unknowns                        |
| **Detector (RID)**    | Refractive index change                    | Universal but less sensitive                                    | For sugars, polymers — no chromophore                                                   |
| **Detector (FLD)**    | Fluorescence emission                      | High sensitivity (10–1000× UV)                                  | For low-dose APIs (derivatization may be needed)                                        |
| **Detector (ELSD)**   | Light scattering from evaporated particles | Universal; semi-quantitative                                    | For lipids, surfactants, sugars                                                         |
| **Data System**       | Acquires, processes, reports data          | **Empower** (Waters), **OpenLab/ChemStation** (Agilent)         | Must be 21 CFR Part 11 compliant (audit trail, electronic signatures, access control)   |

  

### C) Method Development — Step by Step

  

```

STEP 1: UNDERSTAND THE ANALYTE

   → MW, pKa, Log P, UV absorption (λmax), solubility, functional groups

   → Literature review: USP/IP/PhEur monograph; published methods

   → Goal: Assay, related substances, dissolution analysis, or cleaning verification?

       ↓

STEP 2: SELECT CHROMATOGRAPHIC MODE

   → >90% pharma methods use RP-HPLC with C18 column

   → If drug is very polar (Log P <0): Consider HILIC or ion-pair RP

   → If chiral: Consider chiral column (Chiralpak AD-H, OD-H)

       ↓

STEP 3: SELECT COLUMN

   → Start with: C18, 250mm × 4.6mm, 5µm particle size, 100Å pore

   → Alternative columns to try: C8 (less retention), phenyl (π-π interactions),

     CN (different selectivity)

   → Sub-2µm columns (1.7–1.8µm) for UPLC

   → pH-stable columns for pH >8 (BEH C18, XBridge)

       ↓

STEP 4: SELECT MOBILE PHASE

   → Start with: Water (with 0.1% TFA or 0.05% H₃PO₄ for acidic buffer,

     pH 2–3) + Acetonitrile (ACN)

   → If peak tailing for basic drugs: Use phosphate buffer pH 6.8 or

     ammonium formate buffer pH 3.5

   → Gradient: Start 5% ACN → ramp to 95% ACN over 20–30 min

   → Based on results, switch to isocratic at optimal %B

       ↓

STEP 5: OPTIMIZE DETECTION

   → Run UV scan (190–400nm) to find λmax

   → Select wavelength: λmax for assay; 210nm for related substances

     (maximum sensitivity, but baseline noise higher)

   → Use PDA for peak purity check

       ↓

STEP 6: OPTIMIZE SEPARATION

   → Adjust %B (organic): ±5% to improve resolution

   → Adjust pH: ±0.5 pH units (especially for ionizable drugs — dramatic effect)

   → Adjust temperature: ±5°C

   → Change column selectivity: Try phenyl, C8, or pentafluorophenyl (PFP)

   → For complex samples: Use gradient elution

   → Target: Rs ≥2.0 for all critical pairs; k = 2–10; Tf ≤1.5

       ↓

STEP 7: FINALIZE AND DOCUMENT

   → Lock down all parameters; run system suitability

   → Write analytical method/SOP

   → Proceed to method validation (ICH Q2)

```

  

### D) System Suitability Testing (SST)

  

> [!IMPORTANT]

> **SST is performed BEFORE every analytical run.** Failure of SST = do NOT proceed. Results obtained without passing SST are INVALID. This is a GMP requirement and frequent audit finding.

  

| SST Parameter              | Acceptance Criteria (USP <621>)                 | How It's Measured                                            |
| :------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Resolution (Rs)**        | Rs ≥2.0 between critical pair                   | Inject resolution solution (drug + closest eluting impurity) |
| **Tailing Factor (Tf)**    | Tf ≤2.0 (USP); target 0.8–1.5                   | Measured at 5% peak height                                   |
| **Theoretical Plates (N)** | N ≥2000 (method-specific; often >5000 for HPLC) | Calculated from peak width                                   |
| **%RSD of peak area**      | RSD ≤2.0% for n=5 replicate injections (assay)  | 5 consecutive standard injections                            |
| **%RSD of retention time** | RSD ≤1.0%                                       | From same 5 injections                                       |
| **Capacity Factor (k')**   | k' ≥2.0                                         | Ensures adequate retention                                   |

  

**Interview Q: "What do you do if SST fails?"**

> Do NOT analyze samples. Troubleshoot systematically: (1) Check mobile phase preparation — is pH correct? fresh buffer? properly degassed? (2) Check column — has it been used >1000 injections? Is there a guard column? Try flushing with strong solvent. (3) Check system — any leaks? lamp energy adequate? injector needle clean? (4) If SST passes after fix, re-run standard; if SST still fails, replace column and/or prepare fresh mobile phase. Document all troubleshooting in the log book.

  

### E) Troubleshooting Common HPLC Problems

  

| Problem                          | Possible Causes                                                                                         | Solutions                                                                                                                               |
| :------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Peak tailing**                 | Secondary silanol interactions (basic drugs on acidic silica); contaminated column; void at column head | Use end-capped C18 column; add amine modifier (TEA 0.1%); reduce pH to 2–3 to protonate silanols; use BEH column; replace column if old |
| **Peak fronting**                | Column overloading; sample solvent mismatch                                                             | Reduce injection volume/concentration; match sample solvent to mobile phase composition                                                 |
| **Split peaks / Shoulder peaks** | Two polymorphic forms; partially degraded sample; column void                                           | Check sample purity; replace column; repack column head with glass beads                                                                |
| **Ghost peaks**                  | Contamination in mobile phase; carryover from previous injection; dirty vials                           | Use HPLC-grade solvents; run blank; increase needle wash volume; use new vials                                                          |
| **Baseline drift**               | Gradient elution (normal); mobile phase mixing issues; detector lamp aging                              | For gradient: use matched reference cell; for isocratic: check degasser; check lamp hours                                               |
| **Baseline noise**               | Air bubbles; contaminated detector cell; electrical interference; insufficient degassing                | Purge system; clean flow cell; check grounding; ensure proper degassing                                                                 |
| **Retention time shift**         | Temperature fluctuation; mobile phase composition change; column degradation                            | Stabilize column oven (±0.5°C); prepare fresh mobile phase daily; monitor column back-pressure                                          |
| **High back-pressure**           | Column frit blocked; narrow-bore tubing clogged; mobile phase viscosity                                 | Use guard column; filter samples (0.22µm); check tubing for kinks; use column inlet filter                                              |
| **Low sensitivity**              | Wrong wavelength; dilute sample; detector lamp end-of-life                                              | Check UV scan for λmax; concentrate sample; replace lamp (>2000h → replace)                                                             |
| **Broad peaks**                  | Large injection volume; column temperature too low; dead volume in tubing                               | Reduce injection volume; increase column temp; minimize tubing length between injector and column                                       |
| **Negative peaks**               | Sample solvent absorbs less than mobile phase at detection wavelength                                   | Change sample diluent to match mobile phase; change detection wavelength                                                                |
| **Carry-over**                   | Residual analyte in needle/loop from previous injection                                                 | Increase needle wash cycles; use strong wash solvent; run blank between high-conc samples                                               |

### G) Top 20 HPLC Interview Questions with Model Answers

  

**Q1: "Explain the difference between isocratic and gradient elution. When do you choose each?"**

> **Isocratic:** Mobile phase composition is constant throughout the run. Use when: all analytes have similar polarity and elute within a reasonable time (5–15 min). Simpler, faster equilibration, better for routine QC. **Gradient:** %B increases over time (e.g., 10→90% ACN). Use when: sample has analytes with widely different polarities; related substances method where impurities span a wide polarity range; complex samples. Disadvantage: requires column re-equilibration (10–15 column volumes) between injections → longer total runtime. In industry, **assay methods are usually isocratic; related substances methods are usually gradient.**

  

**Q2: "Why is C18 the most commonly used column in pharma?"**

> C18 (octadecylsilane) provides the best combination of: (1) Strong retention for most organic molecules via hydrophobic interaction (18-carbon chain); (2) Wide selectivity — works for both polar and non-polar drugs depending on mobile phase; (3) Excellent reproducibility between manufacturers and batches; (4) Very well-characterized — decades of data and established methods; (5) Available in many formats (250×4.6mm, 150×3.0mm, sub-2µm for UPLC). Alternative: C8 gives less retention (useful when C18 over-retains); Phenyl gives different selectivity via π-π interactions (useful for aromatic compounds).

  

**Q3: "Your HPLC assay result for a tablet is 85.0%. What do you do?"**

> This is an **OOS (Out of Specification)** result (spec: 90–110%). Do NOT discard or re-test without investigation. Follow the OOS investigation procedure: **Phase 1 — Lab Investigation:** (1) Review calculations, integration parameters, data entry. (2) Check standard preparation, dilutions, volumetric flasks used. (3) Verify system suitability passed. (4) Check instrument log — any issues? (5) Examine chromatogram — any anomalies (extra peaks, baseline issues)? If assignable lab error found → document, discard result, re-prepare and re-test. If NO lab error → **Phase 2 — Extended Investigation:** Involve QA and formulation. Re-sample from the same batch. Investigate batch record — any manufacturing deviations? Test additional samples. The 85% result CANNOT be ignored — it must be resolved with documented scientific justification.

  

**Q4: "What is the difference between UV and PDA detectors?"**

> **UV detector** operates at a single fixed wavelength (or dual wavelength). Records absorbance at that wavelength only. **PDA (Photodiode Array) detector** records the FULL UV-Vis spectrum (190–800nm) at every time point during the run, creating a 3D dataset (absorbance × wavelength × time). PDA advantages: (1) **Peak purity** — can assess if a peak is a single compound or co-eluting with an impurity (purity angle < purity threshold → pure peak). (2) Can identify unknowns by comparing UV spectra with library. (3) Can extract chromatograms at any wavelength post-run. Industry standard: Almost all modern HPLC systems use PDA. Interview tip: always mention peak purity when discussing PDA.

  

**Q5: "What is ALCOA+ and why is it important?"**

> **ALCOA+** is the data integrity framework mandated by FDA and all regulatory agencies. It stands for: **A**ttributable (who did it?), **L**egible (can you read it?), **C**ontemporaneous (recorded at the time of activity), **O**riginal (first recording), **A**ccurate (correct and truthful). The **"+"** adds: **C**omplete, **C**onsistent, **E**nduring, **A**vailable. In HPLC context: All raw data files must have audit trails enabled; no data can be deleted; all processing (integration) changes must be logged with reason; only original data is valid. Violation of ALCOA+ is the #1 reason for FDA 483 observations and Warning Letters in pharma. Indian companies have received major citations for audit trail issues.

  

**Q6: "Explain how you would develop an HPLC method for a new drug substance."**

> Systematic approach: (1) Literature search — check if USP/EP monograph exists; search published methods. (2) Characterize the molecule — MW, pKa, Log P, UV absorption, stability. (3) Based on Log P >0 → choose RP-HPLC with C18. (4) Run UV scan → select λmax (e.g., 254nm). (5) Start with generic gradient: 5→95% ACN with 0.1% TFA buffer, C18 250×4.6mm, 5µm, 1.0 mL/min, 30°C. (6) Observe retention — adjust %B for isocratic; optimize pH if basic drug tails. (7) Stress the drug (forced degradation) → ensure method separates all degradants (stability-indicating). (8) Optimize for SST criteria (Rs >2.0, Tf <1.5, N >5000). (9) Document as SOP → proceed to validation.

  

**Q7: "What is the difference between LOD and LOQ?"**

> **LOD (Limit of Detection):** Lowest amount that can be DETECTED but NOT necessarily quantified. Signal-to-noise ratio ≥3:1. Used to demonstrate method sensitivity. **LOQ (Limit of Quantitation):** Lowest amount that can be QUANTIFIED with acceptable accuracy and precision. S/N ≥10:1. Used as the reporting threshold for impurities. **Methods for determination:** (1) Signal-to-noise: LOD = 3× noise; LOQ = 10× noise. (2) Standard deviation of response: LOD = 3.3σ/S; LOQ = 10σ/S (where σ = SD of y-intercept, S = slope). (3) Visual evaluation. Industry standard: Typically report impurities above LOQ (≥0.05% or as per ICH Q3B).

  

**Q8: "How do you ensure your HPLC method is stability-indicating?"**

> A stability-indicating method must separate the API peak from ALL known and potential degradation products. Proof: (1) Conduct **forced degradation** under ICH conditions (acid, base, oxidative, thermal, photolytic, humidity). (2) Achieve 5–20% degradation. (3) Run all stressed samples by the method. (4) Demonstrate that all degradant peaks are **resolved from the API peak** (Rs ≥1.5 at minimum, ideally ≥2.0). (5) Confirm **peak purity** of the API peak in all stressed samples using PDA — purity angle must be less than purity threshold. (6) Perform **mass balance** — assay + sum of impurities + degradants ≈ 100% (98–102%). Document all of this in the method development report.

  

**Q9: "What is the Van Deemter equation and what does it mean practically?"**

> H = A + B/u + Cu, where H = plate height, u = linear velocity. **A term (eddy diffusion):** Multiple paths through packed column → use smaller, uniform particles (sub-2µm = UPLC). **B term (longitudinal diffusion):** Band broadening due to diffusion → significant only at very low flow rates. **C term (mass transfer):** Resistance to mass transfer between phases → decreases with smaller particles and thinner films. **Practical meaning:** There's an optimal flow rate (minimum of H vs u curve) where you get maximum efficiency. For conventional 5µm HPLC: optimum ~1.0 mL/min. For sub-2µm UPLC: can run at higher flow without losing efficiency → faster analysis. This is WHY UPLC is faster and better than HPLC.

  

**Q10: "What are the differences between HPLC and UPLC?"**

  

| Parameter               | HPLC                          | UPLC                                        |
| :---------------------- | :---------------------------- | :------------------------------------------ |
| **Particle size**       | 3–5 µm                        | 1.7–1.8 µm (sub-2µm)                        |
| **Back-pressure**       | <6000 psi (400 bar)           | Up to 15,000–22,000 psi (1000–1500 bar)     |

## 1.2 GAS CHROMATOGRAPHY (GC)

  

### When Used in Pharma

  

| Application              | Why GC                                     | Example                                          |
| :----------------------- | :----------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------- |
| **Residual Solvents**    | ICH Q3C — volatile solvents from synthesis | Ethanol, methanol, DCM, acetone, toluene in API  |
| **Headspace GC**         | Volatile analysis without liquid injection | Residual solvents in tablet/capsule dosage forms |
| **GC-MS**                | Identification of unknowns                 | Genotoxic impurities; extractables/leachables    |
| **Elemental impurities** | Combined with ICP-MS (not GC alone)        | USP <232>/<233>, ICH Q3D                         |

  

### Key Differences from HPLC

  

| Parameter              | HPLC                       | GC                                                            |
| :--------------------- | :------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Mobile phase**       | Liquid (water, ACN, MeOH)  | Gas (helium, nitrogen, hydrogen)                              |
| **Sample requirement** | Solution (aqueous/organic) | Must be VOLATILE (boiling point <300°C)                       |
| **Detector**           | UV/PDA, RID, FLD, ELSD     | FID (universal for organics), TCD, ECD, MS                    |
| **Column**             | Packed (silica particles)  | Capillary (open tubular, fused silica; 30m × 0.25mm × 0.25µm) |
| **Temperature**        | Column oven (25–80°C)      | Oven program (40–300°C ramp)                                  |
| **Injection**          | Autosampler (liquid)       | Split/splitless; headspace sampler                            |

  

### Interview Q: "Describe ICH Q3C residual solvent testing by headspace GC."

> **Model Answer:** ICH Q3C classifies residual solvents into 3 classes: **Class 1** (avoid — carcinogenic/toxic: benzene, CCl₄), **Class 2** (limit — e.g., DCM ≤600 ppm, methanol ≤3000 ppm, toluene ≤890 ppm), **Class 3** (low toxic risk — ≤5000 ppm: ethanol, acetone, ethyl acetate). Method: dissolve API in DMSO or water → headspace vial → heat at 80°C for 45 min → GC injects vapor phase → FID detection. Headspace avoids non-volatile matrix entering the column. USP <467> is the compendial method. Key SST: resolution between closely eluting solvents ≥1.0; %RSD of standard ≤15%.

- IP/BP often specify TLC for identification tests

  

### Key Parameters

| :------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |

## 2.1 UV-VISIBLE SPECTROSCOPY

  

### A) Principle

Molecules absorb UV-Vis radiation (190–800nm) → electronic transitions (π→π*, n→π*, σ→σ*) → absorbance proportional to concentration.

  

**Beer-Lambert Law:** A = εbc

- A = absorbance, ε = molar absorptivity (L/mol·cm), b = path length (cm), c = concentration (mol/L)

- **Linear range:** A = 0.2 to 0.8 (ideal); above 1.0 → deviation from linearity

  

### B) Instrumentation

  

```

Light Source → Monochromator → Sample Cell (cuvette) → Detector → Display

(D₂ lamp for UV)  (grating)     (1cm path length)    (PMT or       (spectrum)

(W lamp for Vis)                  (quartz for UV)     photodiode)

```

  

### C) Applications in Pharma R&D

  

| Application                     | How It's Used                                                                        |
| :------------------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Assay (simple formulations)** | Single-component analysis at λmax; used for dissolution sample analysis              |
| **Dissolution testing**         | Online UV measurement at λmax; fiber optic probes for automated dissolution          |
| **λmax determination**          | Scan 190–400nm to find absorption maximum → used to select HPLC detection wavelength |
| **Excipient compatibility**     | Color change (visible range) in stressed samples                                     |
| **Content uniformity**          | For single-component dosage forms (where HPLC is not necessary)                      |
| **pKa determination**           | pH-dependent absorbance changes                                                      |

  

### D) Interview Q: "When would you use UV-Vis instead of HPLC?"

> UV-Vis is simpler, faster, and cheaper. Use when: (1) Single-component analysis with no interfering excipients (e.g., dissolution samples where medium is simple). (2) Rapid screening. (3) Drug identification by λmax comparison. Use HPLC when: (1) Multiple components must be separated. (2) Impurity profiling required. (3) Complex matrix with interfering substances. (4) Higher sensitivity/specificity needed. In dissolution testing, UV-Vis is common for single-analyte methods; HPLC is used when excipients interfere at the detection wavelength or when multiple release components must be quantified.

## 2.2 FOURIER TRANSFORM INFRARED SPECTROSCOPY (FTIR)

  

### A) Principle

IR radiation (4000–400 cm⁻¹) absorbed by molecules → vibrational transitions (stretching, bending of bonds). Each functional group absorbs at a characteristic frequency → "molecular fingerprint."

  

### B) Key Functional Group Frequencies

  

| Functional Group       | Wavenumber (cm⁻¹) | Bond Type                                                                 |
| :--------------------- | :---------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **O-H (broad)**        | 3200–3600         | Alcohols, carboxylic acids (very broad for H-bonded)                      |
| **N-H**                | 3300–3500         | Amines, amides (primary amine: 2 peaks; secondary: 1 peak)                |
| **C-H (aliphatic)**    | 2850–3000         | Alkanes                                                                   |
| **C-H (aromatic)**     | 3000–3100         | Benzene rings                                                             |
| **C=O (carbonyl)**     | 1650–1780         | Ketones (~1715), esters (~1735), amides (~1650), carboxylic acids (~1710) |
| **C=C (aromatic)**     | 1450–1600         | Benzene ring stretches (multiple bands)                                   |
| **C-O**                | 1000–1300         | Ethers, esters, alcohols                                                  |
| **Fingerprint region** | 400–1500          | Unique to each compound — identity confirmation                           |

  

### C) Applications in Pharma R&D

  

| Application                    | Detail                                                                              |
| :----------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Identity test**              | Compare sample spectrum with reference standard spectrum; correlation >99% (IP/USP) |
| **Excipient compatibility**    | New peaks or peak shifts in binary mixture → interaction                            |
| **Polymorphism**               | Different polymorphs show different IR spectra (H-bonding differences)              |
| **Drug-excipient interaction** | Shift in carbonyl peak → interaction with excipient                                 |
| **Counterfeit detection**      | Rapid screening by ATR-FTIR                                                         |
| **Raw material testing**       | ATR-FTIR for incoming raw material ID testing (per cGMP)                            |

  

### D) FTIR Sampling Techniques

  

| Technique                              | Sample Form                                             | Use                                                         |
| :------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------ | :---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **KBr pellet**                         | Solid powder mixed with KBr, compressed                 | Traditional; most accurate                                  |
| **ATR (Attenuated Total Reflectance)** | Solid/liquid placed directly on crystal (diamond, ZnSe) | **Most common now** — no sample prep; fast; non-destructive |
| **Nujol mull**                         | Solid ground with mineral oil                           | For moisture-sensitive samples                              |
| **Liquid film**                        | Liquid between NaCl/KBr plates                          | Liquids/oils                                                |

## 2.3 NUCLEAR MAGNETIC RESONANCE (NMR)

  

### A) Principle

Nuclei with spin (¹H, ¹³C) absorb radio-frequency radiation in a strong magnetic field → transitions between spin states. Chemical shift (δ, ppm) depends on electron environment → structural elucidation.

  

### B) Key Information from NMR

  

| Parameter                          | What It Tells You                                                 |
| :--------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Chemical shift (δ)**             | Electronic environment of nuclei; functional group identification |
| **Integration**                    | Number of protons contributing to each signal (¹H NMR)            |
| **Splitting pattern (J-coupling)** | Number of neighboring protons (n+1 rule); bond connectivity       |
| **2D NMR (COSY, HSQC, HMBC)**      | Complete structural connectivity and stereochemistry              |

  

### C) ¹H NMR Chemical Shift Reference Chart

  

| δ (ppm) | Assignment                                   |
| :------ | :------------------------------------------- |
| 0–1     | CH₃ (methyl), Si-CH₃ (TMS reference at 0.00) |
| 1–2     | CH₂ (aliphatic methylene)                    |
| 2–3     | CH next to C=O, N-CH₃                        |
| 3–4     | O-CH₃ (methoxy), N-CH₂                       |
| 4–5     | O-CH₂, =CH₂                                  |
| 6–8     | Aromatic H; vinyl H                          |
| 9–10    | Aldehyde H                                   |
| 10–13   | Carboxylic acid H, OH (exchangeable)         |

  

### D) Applications in Pharma

  

| Application                     | When Used                                                                          |
| :------------------------------ | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Structure confirmation**      | Gold standard for API structure elucidation; required in DMF/ASMF                  |
| **Polymorph characterization**  | Solid-state NMR (¹³C CPMAS) distinguishes polymorphs                               |
| **Impurity identification**     | Combined with LC-NMR or off-line isolation                                         |
| **Quantitative NMR (qNMR)**     | Reference standard purity assignment without separate reference (primary standard) |
| **Co-crystal characterization** | Detects H-bonding interactions in co-crystal                                       |

## 2.4 MASS SPECTROMETRY (MS)

  

### A) Principle

Molecules are ionized → ions separated by mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) → detected → mass spectrum.

  

### B) Common Ionization Modes

  

| Mode                                         | Abbreviation | Used For                                                                             |
| :------------------------------------------- | :----------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Electrospray Ionization**                  | ESI          | Polar, ionizable molecules (most drugs, proteins); soft ionization; LC-MS compatible |
| **Atmospheric Pressure Chemical Ionization** | APCI         | Less polar molecules; good for small molecules                                       |
| **Matrix-Assisted Laser Desorption**         | MALDI        | Large biomolecules (proteins, polymers); combined with TOF                           |
| **Electron Ionization**                      | EI           | Volatile compounds; hard ionization → extensive fragmentation; GC-MS standard        |

  

### C) Mass Analyzers

  

| Analyzer                    | Resolving Power                 | Use                                                                          |
| :-------------------------- | :------------------------------ | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Quadrupole (Q)**          | Unit resolution (1 amu)         | Routine quantitation; LC-MS                                                  |
| **Triple Quadrupole (QqQ)** | Unit; MRM mode                  | Quantitative bioanalysis; genotoxic impurities; **most common in pharma QC** |
| **Time-of-Flight (TOF)**    | High resolution (10,000–60,000) | Accurate mass; impurity identification                                       |
| **Orbitrap**                | Very high resolution (100,000+) | Exact mass; structural elucidation; metabolite ID                            |
| **Ion Trap**                | Medium                          | MS^n experiments; structural fragments                                       |
  

### D) LC-MS/MS in Pharma R&D

  

| Application                           | Detail                                                  |
| :------------------------------------ | :------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Impurity identification**           | Exact mass → molecular formula → structure proposal     |
| **Genotoxic impurity (GTI) analysis** | ICH M7; trace-level quantitation (ppm/ppb)              |
| **Extractables & Leachables (E&L)**   | Container closure integrity; packaging compatibility    |
| **Bioanalytical methods**             | PK studies; drug and metabolite quantitation in plasma  |
| **Elemental impurities**              | ICP-MS for ICH Q3D (Pd, Ni, Pb, As, Cd, Hg, etc.)       |
| **Nitrosamine impurities**            | NDMA, NDEA quantitation at ppb level (post-2018 crisis) |

  

### Interview Q: "What is MRM mode in LC-MS/MS and why is it used?"

> **MRM (Multiple Reaction Monitoring)** is a targeted, highly selective and sensitive detection mode in triple-quadrupole MS. Q1 selects the **precursor ion** (parent), the collision cell fragments it, Q3 selects a specific **product ion** (fragment). This precursor→product transition is unique to each analyte → extremely high selectivity even in complex matrices (plasma, tissue). Sensitivity: sub-ng/mL levels. Used for: GTI analysis (detecting impurities at 1 ppm level); bioequivalence studies; nitrosamine testing. Example: For NDMA (m/z 75 → m/z 58 transition), MRM can detect at 0.3 ppb in drug substance.

## 3.1 DIFFERENTIAL SCANNING CALORIMETRY (DSC)

  

### A) Principle

Measures the **heat flow** difference between a sample and an empty reference pan as both are heated (or cooled) at a controlled rate. Detects: melting, crystallization, glass transition, desolvation, degradation.

  

### B) What DSC Tells You

  

| Thermal Event                 | DSC Signal                                    | What It Means                                                    |
| :---------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Melting (Tm)**              | Sharp **endothermic** peak                    | Crystalline material; characteristic of each polymorph           |
| **Glass Transition (Tg)**     | Baseline shift (step change in heat capacity) | Amorphous material; Tg determines physical stability of ASD      |
| **Crystallization (Tc)**      | Sharp **exothermic** peak                     | Amorphous → crystalline conversion on heating                    |
| **Desolvation / Dehydration** | Broad endotherm (50–150°C)                    | Loss of water (hydrate) or solvent (solvate)                     |
| **Decomposition**             | Broad/irregular endotherm at high temp        | Degradation (often overlaps with melting for unstable compounds) |
  

### C) Applications in Pharma R&D

  

| Application                              | How It's Used                                                                                   |
| :--------------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Polymorph identification**             | Different polymorphs have different melting points and enthalpy values                          |
| **Excipient compatibility**              | Binary mixtures: disappearance of API melting peak or appearance of new peak → interaction      |
| **Glass transition measurement**         | Tg of amorphous solid dispersion → if Tg > storage temp + 50°C → physically stable              |
| **Purity determination**                 | Van't Hoff equation applied to melting point depression → purity estimation                     |
| **Hydrate/solvate detection**            | Endotherm before melting = desolvation event                                                    |
| **Lyophilized product characterization** | Tg' (glass transition of maximally freeze-concentrated solute) → determines primary drying temp |
  

### D) Typical DSC Conditions

  

| Parameter             | Typical Setting                                                                      |
| :-------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Heating rate**      | 10°C/min (standard); 2°C/min for high-resolution; 20°C/min for screening             |
| **Temperature range** | 25–350°C (or higher if needed)                                                       |
| **Atmosphere**        | Nitrogen gas (50 mL/min) — prevents oxidation                                        |
| **Sample size**       | 2–5 mg                                                                               |
| **Pan type**          | Aluminum pan (crimped or hermetically sealed); pin-holed lid for desolvation studies |
| **Calibration**       | Indium standard (Tm = 156.6°C, ΔH = 28.71 J/g)                                       |

  

### E) Interview Q: "You see two melting peaks in your API's DSC thermogram. What does this mean?"

> Multiple possibilities: (1) **Two polymorphic forms** — Form I melts first, recrystallizes to more stable Form II, then Form II melts. This is called **enantiotropic polymorphism.** Confirm by XRPD. (2) **Solvate/hydrate desolvation** followed by melting of anhydrous form. (3) **Eutectic melting** if it's a mixture (e.g., API + excipient compatibility sample). (4) **Decomposition** overlapping with melting. To investigate: run at different heating rates (2, 10, 20°C/min) — polymorphic transitions are heating-rate dependent; decomposition is kinetic. Run modulated DSC (MDSC) to separate reversible (melting) from irreversible (decomposition) events. Confirm with XRPD and hot-stage microscopy.

## 3.2 THERMOGRAVIMETRIC ANALYSIS (TGA)

  

### What TGA Measures

**Weight change** as a function of temperature. Detects: moisture loss, solvent loss, decomposition.

  

### Key Applications

  

| Application                 | Observation                                                    |
| :-------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Moisture content**        | Weight loss at 25–120°C = surface/bound water                  |
| **Solvate quantification**  | Weight loss corresponding to molar ratio of solvent            |
| **Thermal stability**       | Onset of decomposition (Td) — critical for HME processing temp |
| **Excipient decomposition** | Compare Td of excipients to identify processing limits         |

  

### TGA + DSC Combined Interpretation

  

| DSC Event          | TGA Event                 | Interpretation                           |
| :----------------- | :------------------------ | :--------------------------------------- |
| Endotherm at 80°C  | Weight loss (~3%) at 80°C | **Desolvation/dehydration** (water loss) |
| Endotherm at 220°C | No weight loss at 220°C   | **Melting** (pure phase transition)      |
| Endotherm at 250°C | Weight loss at 250°C      | **Decomposition** (not melting)          |

## 3.3 X-RAY POWDER DIFFRACTION (XRPD / PXRD)

  

### A) Principle

X-rays diffracted by crystalline lattice planes → **Bragg's Law: nλ = 2d·sinθ** → unique diffraction pattern for each crystal form. Amorphous materials show a **broad halo** (no sharp peaks).

  

### B) What XRPD Tells You

  

| Observation                                    | Interpretation                                     |
| :--------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------- |
| **Sharp, well-defined peaks**                  | Crystalline material                               |
| **Broad amorphous halo**                       | Amorphous material (or amorphous solid dispersion) |
| **Different peak positions vs reference**      | Different polymorph or solvate                     |
| **Same peak positions, different intensities** | Preferred orientation or same polymorph            |
| **Peaks disappearing after milling**           | Amorphization during processing                    |

  

### C) Applications

  

| Application                  | Example                                                                 |
| :--------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Polymorph identification** | Each polymorph has unique XRPD pattern → fingerprint                    |
| **ASD confirmation**         | If API peaks disappear in ASD XRPD → confirmed amorphous dispersion     |
| **Stability monitoring**     | Appearance of crystalline peaks in ASD on stability → recrystallization |
| **Patent strategy**          | Patent specific polymorph based on unique XRPD pattern (2θ values)      |
| **Quality control**          | Confirm correct polymorph of incoming API                               |

  

### D) Interview Q: "How do you confirm that your solid dispersion is truly amorphous?"

> Use orthogonal techniques: (1) **XRPD** — absence of sharp crystalline peaks; presence of amorphous halo at 15–25° 2θ → primary evidence. (2) **DSC** — absence of API melting endotherm; presence of single Tg (Gordon-Taylor equation predicts Tg of miscible blend) → confirms molecular-level dispersion. (3) **FTIR/Raman** — shifts in characteristic drug peaks indicating drug-polymer interaction (H-bonding). (4) **PLM (Polarized Light Microscopy)** — amorphous material shows no birefringence under crossed polarizers. All four together provide irrefutable evidence. On stability, monitor for re-crystallization using XRPD at each timepoint.

## 3.4 DYNAMIC VAPOR SORPTION (DVS)

  

### What It Measures

**Moisture uptake** of a sample as a function of relative humidity (RH) at controlled temperature. Generates a **moisture sorption isotherm** (% weight change vs % RH).

  

### Classification from DVS

  

| Classification             | Weight Gain at 80% RH | Examples                    |
| :------------------------- | :-------------------- | :-------------------------- |
| **Non-hygroscopic**        | <0.2%                 | DCP, mannitol (crystalline) |
| **Slightly hygroscopic**   | 0.2–2.0%              | MCC, starch                 |
| **Moderately hygroscopic** | 2.0–15%               | PVP K30, SSG                |
| **Very hygroscopic**       | >15%                  | Sorbitol, glycerin          |

  

### Applications

- **API form selection:** Avoid hygroscopic salt/polymorph → choose least hygroscopic form

- **Excipient selection:** Avoid hygroscopic excipients for moisture-sensitive APIs

- **Packaging selection:** If drug is hygroscopic → Alu-Alu blister > PVC/PVDC blister > HDPE bottle

- **Hydrate formation detection:** Stepwise weight gain at specific RH indicates hydrate formation
