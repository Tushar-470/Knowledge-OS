## SECTION A: FORMULATION SCIENCE (10 Questions)

  

### T1: "Explain the difference between immediate-release and sustained-release tablets."

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "Immediate-release tablets are designed to disintegrate and release the drug quickly — typically ≥85% dissolution within 30 minutes. They're used when rapid onset of action is needed, such as analgesics. Sustained-release tablets are designed to release the drug gradually over an extended period — typically 8, 12, or 24 hours — maintaining therapeutic plasma levels and reducing dosing frequency. Mechanisms include: matrix systems (drug embedded in polymer matrix like HPMC — drug release by diffusion and erosion), reservoir systems (drug core coated with rate-controlling membrane like ethylcellulose), and osmotic systems (osmotic pressure drives drug release through a laser-drilled orifice, like OROS technology in Concerta). Key quality tests differ: IR tablets → disintegration time + single-point dissolution; SR tablets → multi-point dissolution profile over the entire release period."

  

🔴 **Common Mistake:** Giving only a textbook definition without mentioning mechanisms or quality tests.

  

⭐ **Impressive Addition:** "In my thesis, I developed SR matrix tablets using HPMC K100M — the drug release followed Higuchi kinetics (R² = 0.9945), indicating diffusion-controlled release from a swelling matrix."

  

---

  

### T2: "You have a BCS Class II drug. How would you improve its bioavailability?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "BCS Class II = high permeability, low solubility. The rate-limiting step is dissolution. My strategy, in order of increasing complexity:

> 1. **Micronization / Nanosizing** — increase surface area → faster dissolution (Noyes-Whitney equation). Example: Fenofibrate micronized → TriCor.

> 2. **Salt formation** — if the drug has ionizable groups, form a more soluble salt. Example: Diclofenac sodium vs diclofenac acid.

> 3. **Amorphous solid dispersion (ASD)** — disperse drug molecularly in a polymer (PVP-VA, HPMC-AS) using hot-melt extrusion or spray drying. Removes crystal lattice energy → supersaturation. Example: Sporanox (itraconazole ASD).

> 4. **Lipid-based delivery (SEDDS/SMEDDS)** — dissolve drug in lipid + surfactant + co-surfactant → self-emulsifies in GI tract. Example: Neoral (cyclosporine SMEDDS).

> 5. **Cyclodextrin complexation** — HP-β-CD inclusion complex increases apparent solubility. Example: Sporanox oral solution.

> 6. **Co-crystals** — multicomponent crystal with coformer improves solubility without changing API. Example: Entresto (sacubitril-valsartan co-crystal).

>

> Selection depends on: degree of solubility enhancement needed, drug dose, thermal stability (for HME), cost, and scalability."

  

🔴 **Common Mistake:** Listing techniques without real drug examples or selection rationale.

  

⭐ **Impressive Addition:** "For my thesis drug [Metformin], bioavailability wasn't the challenge since it's BCS Class III — but if I were developing a Class II drug, I'd start with a preformulation solubility screen across pH 1.2–6.8 to determine the pH-solubility profile and calculate the dose:solubility ratio to guide technique selection."

### T4: "What is the difference between wet granulation and dry granulation? When would you choose each?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

  

| Aspect | Wet Granulation | Dry Granulation |

|:---|:---|:---|

| **Process** | Mix powder + add liquid binder → granulate → dry → mill | Compact powder → break into granules (roller compaction or slugging) |

| **Binder** | Liquid (PVP K30 in water/IPA; HPMC in water) | Dry binder (MCC, pre-gelatinized starch) |

| **When to choose** | Drug is stable to moisture and heat; poor flow/compressibility needs improvement; high-dose drug needing content uniformity | Drug is **moisture-sensitive** or **heat-sensitive**; drug degrades with water exposure |

| **Advantages** | Better granule strength; improved flow; excellent content uniformity | No drying step → faster; no moisture exposure; suitable for heat/moisture-labile drugs |

| **Disadvantages** | Requires drying (energy, time); moisture may cause degradation | Generates fines; lower granule strength; may have flow issues |

| **Equipment** | High-shear granulator (Rapid Mixer Granulator); fluid-bed granulator | Roller compactor (Fitzpatrick Chilsonator); slugging press |

  

🔴 **Common Mistake:** Not mentioning when to choose each based on drug properties.

  

⭐ **Impressive:** "A third option is direct compression — no granulation at all. ### T6: "What is the Noyes-Whitney equation and why is it important?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "dM/dt = DA(Cs − Ct)/h

>

> Where: dM/dt = dissolution rate, D = diffusion coefficient, A = surface area of drug, Cs = saturation solubility, Ct = concentration in bulk medium, h = diffusion layer thickness.

>

> **Practical importance** — it tells us how to increase dissolution rate:

> - Increase **A** (surface area) → micronize or nanosize the drug

> - Increase **Cs** (solubility) → amorphous form, salt form, cyclodextrin complexation

> - Decrease **h** (diffusion layer) → increase agitation (higher RPM in dissolution)

> - Increase **D** → raise temperature (but fixed at 37°C for pharma)

> - Maintain **(Cs − Ct)** → maintain sink conditions (volume ≥3× saturation volume)

>

> This equation is the theoretical foundation for ALL solubility enhancement strategies."

### T7: "Explain polymorphism. Why does it matter in pharma?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "Polymorphism is the ability of a substance to exist in more than one crystalline form — same chemical composition but different crystal packing. Different polymorphs have different: melting points, solubility, dissolution rates, stability, and bioavailability. **Most famous example:** Ritonavir — Abbott had to reformulate when the original polymorph (Form I) converted to a more stable but less soluble Form II during manufacturing, rendering the product therapeutically inactive. **In R&D:** We must identify all polymorphs during preformulation (screen using DSC, XRPD, hot-stage microscopy), select the most suitable form (usually the thermodynamically stable form for long-term product, or a metastable form if higher solubility is needed with proper stabilization), and control the polymorph throughout manufacturing and stability. **Regulatory:** ICH Q6A Decision Tree #4 addresses when to set a polymorphic form specification. Patent implications: companies can patent specific polymorphs (e.g., Ranitidine Form I vs Form II litigation)."

### T8: "What is the difference between disintegration and dissolution?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "**Disintegration** is the physical process of a tablet breaking apart into smaller particles/granules in a liquid medium. Tested per USP <701> — tablet placed in disintegration apparatus (basket-rack assembly) in water/medium at 37°C. **Passes** when no residue remains on the screen or residue is a soft mass. Time limit: typically ≤30 min for uncoated tablets, ≤60 min for coated tablets. **Dissolution** is the chemical/physical process of the drug going into solution from the disintegrated particles. Tested per USP <711>. **Key distinction:** A tablet can disintegrate rapidly but dissolve slowly (e.g., if drug is poorly soluble). Disintegration is necessary but NOT sufficient for drug absorption. Dissolution is the rate-limiting step for BCS Class II drugs and is a much better predictor of in vivo performance. That's why regulatory agencies rely on dissolution — not disintegration — as the primary quality test for oral solid dosage forms."

### T9: "What is the role of HPMC in matrix tablets?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "HPMC (Hypromellose) is the most widely used hydrophilic matrix-forming polymer for sustained-release tablets. **Mechanism:** When the tablet contacts GI fluid, HPMC on the surface hydrates and forms a gel layer. This gel layer acts as a barrier controlling drug release. Drug is released by two mechanisms: **(1) Diffusion** — drug dissolves and diffuses through the gel layer; **(2) Erosion** — the gel layer gradually erodes, releasing drug. The balance between diffusion and erosion depends on HPMC grade (viscosity). **Grades:** K4M (low viscosity) → faster release, more erosion-dominant; K15M (medium); K100M (high viscosity) → slower release, more diffusion-dominant. **Typical concentration:** 20–40% of tablet weight. **Why HPMC over other polymers:** Non-toxic, well-characterized, robust gel formation independent of pH, regulatory acceptance globally, wide range of viscosity grades."

### T10: "What are Critical Quality Attributes (CQAs)? List 5 for a tablet."

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "CQAs are physical, chemical, biological, or microbiological properties that must be within appropriate limits to ensure the desired product quality. Per ICH Q8, CQAs are identified based on their impact on safety and efficacy.

>

> **5 CQAs for a tablet:**

> 1. **Dissolution / Drug Release** — directly affects bioavailability; THE most critical CQA

## SECTION B: ANALYTICAL TECHNIQUES (7 Questions)

  

### T11: "Explain the principle of HPLC and why it's the most important technique in pharma."

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "HPLC separates, identifies, and quantifies components in a mixture by passing a liquid mobile phase under high pressure through a column packed with stationary phase particles. Separation occurs because different compounds interact differently with the stationary phase — in reverse-phase HPLC (80% of pharma methods), more hydrophobic compounds are retained longer on the C18 column. **Why it's #1 in pharma:** (1) Quantitative — gives precise concentration data for assay, impurities, dissolution. (2) Specific — separates drug from degradation products, excipient peaks, impurities. (3) Versatile — works for almost all drug molecules with a chromophore. (4) Validated — well-established regulatory acceptance; ICH Q2(R2) validation framework. (5) Automated — modern autosamplers run 24/7 with full audit trail (21 CFR Part 11 compliant). Every pharma lab in the world has an HPLC."

### T12: "Your HPLC baseline is noisy. What would you check?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "Systematic troubleshooting approach:

> 1. **Mobile phase** — is it freshly prepared? Properly filtered (0.22µm) and degassed? Using HPLC-grade solvents? Buffer pH correct? → Prepare fresh mobile phase and re-check

> 2. **Detector lamp** — check lamp hours. UV lamp life ~2000 hours. If >1500h → replace. Check lamp energy in system diagnostics

> 3. **Flow cell** — contaminated? Flush with 1:1 methanol:water, then 100% methanol, then isopropanol

> 4. **Air bubbles** — in pump, tubing, or flow cell. Purge all lines. Check degasser is functioning

> 5. **Electrical** — system grounded? No electromagnetic interference from other equipment?

> 6. **Column** — old/contaminated column can cause irregular baseline. Try with a new column

> 7. **Check specific wavelength** — noise is worse at 210nm (far UV) than at 254nm. If using 210nm for related substances, some noise is normal

>

> **Document everything** in the instrument logbook — troubleshooting steps, root cause, corrective action."

### T13: "What is the difference between LOD and LOQ?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "**LOD (Limit of Detection):** Lowest amount of analyte that can be DETECTED — meaning you can see a peak, but you can't reliably quantify it. S/N ≥ 3:1. Calculation: LOD = 3.3σ/S. Used to demonstrate method sensitivity.

>

> **LOQ (Limit of Quantitation):** Lowest amount that can be QUANTIFIED with acceptable accuracy and precision. S/N ≥ 10:1. Calculation: LOQ = 10σ/S. At LOQ, you must demonstrate precision (RSD ≤10%) and accuracy (±20% recovery).

>

> **Practical significance:** LOQ sets the reporting threshold for impurities. Per ICH Q3B, impurities at or above the reporting threshold must be reported. Impurities between LOD and LOQ → detected but not quantified (report as '<LOQ'). Below LOD → not detected (report as 'ND')."

### T14: "How do you ensure your HPLC method is stability-indicating?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "A stability-indicating method resolves the drug peak from ALL degradation products. Proof via **forced degradation studies:**

>

> | Condition | Reagent | Duration | Target |

> |:---|:---|:---|:---|

> | Acid | 0.1N HCl, 60°C | 24–72h | 5–20% degradation |

> | Base | 0.1N NaOH, 60°C | 24–72h | 5–20% degradation |

> | Oxidative | 3% H₂O₂, RT | 24h | 5–20% degradation |

> | Thermal | 60°C dry heat | 7 days | 5–20% degradation |

> | Photolytic | ICH Q1B light | Per Q1B | 5–20% degradation |

>

> For each stressed sample: (1) Run by HPLC method → all degradant peaks must be resolved from drug peak (Rs ≥2.0). (2) PDA peak purity check → purity angle < purity threshold for drug peak. (3) Mass balance → assay + degradation products ≈ 100% (98–102%). If these are met, method is stability-indicating."

### T15: "What is system suitability and what happens if it fails?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "SST verifies that the HPLC system is performing adequately before sample analysis. Criteria per USP <621>: Resolution ≥2.0 between critical pair; tailing factor ≤2.0; theoretical plates ≥2000 (method-specific); %RSD of 5 replicate standard injections ≤2.0% (for assay). **If SST fails:** STOP. Do NOT analyze samples. Troubleshoot: check mobile phase, column, detector, injection system. Once fixed, re-run SST. Samples analyzed with failing SST are INVALID — this is a GMP requirement and a common FDA 483 observation."

### T16: "What is f2 similarity factor and how do you calculate it?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "f2 = 50 × log {[1 + (1/n) Σ(Rt − Tt)²]⁻⁰·⁵ × 100}. Acceptance: f2 ≥50 = similar profiles. Conditions: minimum 12 units per product; use mean values; only one timepoint >85% dissolved; minimum 3 timepoints; RSD at early timepoints ≤20%, later ≤10%. f2 = 100 means identical profiles. Used for: comparing generic vs innovator dissolution profiles; comparing pre- and post-change dissolution; supporting dissolution spec setting."

### T17: "When would you use UV-Vis instead of HPLC for dissolution analysis?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "UV-Vis is simpler, faster, and cheaper — use it when: (1) Single analyte — no co-eluting impurities or degradants at the detection wavelength. (2) Excipients don't interfere — no excipient absorbs at λmax. (3) Simple dissolution medium — no surfactant interference. (4) Higher throughput needed — UV can analyze faster than HPLC. Switch to HPLC when: multiple release components; excipient interference; stability samples where degradants must be separated; regulatory requirement. In practice, many IR tablet dissolution methods use UV-Vis, while ER tablets often use HPLC to ensure specificity over the long dissolution time."

### T18: "What is ICH Q1A? What stability conditions apply to India?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "ICH Q1A(R2) defines stability testing requirements for new drug substances and products. India is in ICH Climatic Zone IVa. Long-term: 30°C/75%RH. Accelerated: 40°C/75%RH. Testing frequency: Long-term — 0, 3, 6, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36 months. Accelerated — 0, 1, 2, 3, 6 months. Minimum 3 exhibit batches at pilot scale or larger. ### T19: "What is ALCOA+ and why should an R&D scientist care?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "ALCOA+ = Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate + Complete, Consistent, Enduring, Available. It's the data integrity framework. As an R&D scientist, every entry I make in a lab notebook, every HPLC chromatogram I process, every calculation I perform must comply. Specifically: (1) I initial and date every entry at the time I do it (Contemporaneous). (2) I never use correction fluid — single line strike-through with initials and date (Legible, Attributable). ### T20: "What is the difference between NDA and ANDA?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "**NDA (505(b)(1)):** Full application for a NEW drug. Requires complete safety and efficacy data — Phase I/II/III clinical trials. Filed by innovator company. Grants 5-year NCE exclusivity (+ possible patent protection). Costs ~$1–2 billion, takes 10–15 years. **ANDA (505(j)):** Abbreviated application for a GENERIC drug. References innovator's safety/efficacy data — NO clinical trials needed (only bioequivalence study). Must demonstrate pharmaceutical equivalence (same API, strength, dosage form, route) + bioequivalence (same rate and extent of absorption — 90% CI for Cmax and AUC within 80.00–125.00%). Much cheaper (~$2–5 million) and faster (~2–4 years). Indian companies primarily file ANDAs."

### T22: "Explain the OOS investigation procedure."

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

## SECTION D: PROBLEM SOLVING (3 Questions)

  

### T23: "If your tablet dissolution is failing at the 30-minute timepoint but passing at 45 minutes, what could be wrong?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "This suggests delayed disintegration or slow initial drug release. Possible causes and investigations:

> 1. **Tablet hardness too high** → excessive compression force → reduced porosity → slower water penetration → delayed disintegration. Check: measure hardness vs spec; review compression log.

> 2. **Insufficient disintegrant** → increase superdisintegrant (CCS or SSG) concentration. Test at 1.5× and 2× current level.

> 3. **Over-lubrication with MgSt** → hydrophobic coating on particles → poor wetting. Check: was MgSt blended >5 minutes? Reduce blending time to 2–3 minutes.

> 4. **Drug particle size too large** → slow dissolution after disintegration. Check: measure D50 of API lot. Compare with previous passing lots.

> 5. **Coating issue** (if film-coated) → coating too thick or insufficient pore former → delayed drug release.

>

> Approach: Don't change everything at once. Test one variable at a time. Use DoE if multiple factors suspected."

  

---

  

### T24: "You're asked to develop a formulation for a drug that degrades at 40°C. How does this affect your approach?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "A drug that degrades at 40°C is heat-sensitive. This impacts EVERY step:

> - **Granulation:** Cannot use wet granulation with heated drying. Use dry granulation (roller compaction) or direct compression.

> - **Drying:** If wet granulation is unavoidable, use fluid-bed drying at ≤30°C with extended time.

> - **Coating:** Aqueous film coating typically requires inlet air at 50–70°C → may be too high. Use lower temperature with longer coating time, or consider dry powder coating.

> - **Stability:** Accelerated stability at 40°C/75%RH will show degradation — this is expected. Long-term (30°C/75%RH for Zone IVa) is the key study. May need to propose shorter shelf-life or refrigerated storage.

> - **Excipients:** Avoid excipients that catalyze degradation at elevated temperature. Test drug-excipient compatibility at 30°C and 40°C.

> - **Packaging:** Consider desiccant and/or Alu-Alu blister to limit moisture exposure, which may accelerate thermal degradation."

  

---

  

### T25: "The QC team reports that your latest stability batch shows a new unknown impurity peak at the 3-month accelerated timepoint. What do you do?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "Structured approach:

> 1. **Confirm the peak:** Re-inject from the same preparation. Is it reproducible? Check blank, placebo, and mobile phase chromatograms — rule out ghost peak.

> 2. **Quantify:** Calculate area% against main peak. Is it above the ICH Q3B reporting threshold (0.1% for dose ≤1g)?

> 3. **Check specificity:** Review PDA spectrum — does it match any known degradant or excipient? Compare with forced degradation chromatograms — was this peak seen during method development?

> 4. **If above identification threshold (0.2%):** Identify the impurity using LC-MS/MS. Propose structure. Compare with known degradation pathways.

> 5. **If above qualification threshold (0.2%):** Must qualify (safety assessment) — toxicological evaluation or reference to published data.

> 6. **Root cause:** What degradation pathway? Hydrolysis? Oxidation? Does it appear only at 40°C or also at 30°C? Check mass balance.

> 7. **Action:** If this impurity grows, may need to update specification to include a limit for this specified impurity. May need to reformulate (add antioxidant, change packaging) or set a shorter shelf-life."

### P2: "The HPLC shows a split peak for your drug. What's happening?"

  

✅ **Model Answer:**

> "A split peak (shoulder or double peak at the retention time of the drug) can mean:

> 1. **Column void / channeling** → inspect column for gap at head; try replacing frit; or replace column

> 2. **Two polymorphic forms** dissolving at different rates → confirm by XRPD

> 3. **Sample solvent mismatch** → sample dissolved in a solvent much stronger than mobile phase → causes distortion. Solution: dilute in mobile phase

> 4. **Column overloading** → reduce injection volume or concentration

> 5. **Chemical degradation** → co-eluting degradant just barely separated. Check PDA peak purity

>

> First troubleshooting step: Inject a fresh standard solution prepared in mobile phase. If standard peak is sharp → problem is with sample. If standard also splits → problem is column or system."
